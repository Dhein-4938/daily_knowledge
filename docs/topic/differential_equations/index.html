Differential Equations✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A differential equation is an equation relating an unknown function $f$ to one or more of its derivatives. Formally, an $n$-th order ODE takes the form: $$F\!\left(x,\, y,\, y',\, y'',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n)}\right) = 0$$ where $y = f(x)$ is the unknown. For PDEs, the unknown $u$ depends on multiple variables and partial derivatives appear instead.

The order of a DE is the highest derivative present; the degree is the power of that highest-order term (when the equation is polynomial in derivatives). Linearity is the most structurally decisive property: a linear DE satisfies superposition — if $y_1$ and $y_2$ are solutions, so is $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$.

The general taxonomy:

| Class | Variables | Example |

|---|---|---|

| ODE | Single independent | $y'' + \omega^2 y = 0$ |

| PDE | Multiple independent | $\nabla^2 u = 0$ |

| SDE | Stochastic forcing | $dX = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW$ |

| DDE | Delayed argument | $y'(t) = f(y(t-\tau))$ |

Why It Matters

DEs are the native language of continuity. Virtually every physical law that involves rates — Newton's second law, Maxwell's equations, the Schrödinger equation, the Black-Scholes model, epidemiological SIR models — is a differential equation. Their significance is structural:

  • Physics: Newton's $F = ma$ is $m\ddot{x} = F(x, \dot{x}, t)$; quantum mechanics is built on the PDE $i\hbar\,\partial_t \psi = \hat{H}\psi$.
  • Engineering: Control systems, heat transfer, fluid dynamics (Navier-Stokes) all reduce to DEs.
  • Economics: Optimal growth (Ramsey model), option pricing (Black-Scholes PDE), and game dynamics (replicator equations).
  • Biology: Population dynamics, neural firing (Hodgkin-Huxley), morphogenesis (reaction-diffusion).
TIP: Insight
The fact that most DEs lack closed-form solutions is not a failure — it forced the development of entire fields: numerical analysis, functional analysis, dynamical systems theory, and ergodic theory. The "unsolvability" is mathematically productive.

Key Details

1. Existence and Uniqueness (Picard-Lindelöf)

For the IVP $y' = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0) = y_0$: if $f$ is continuous and Lipschitz in $y$, a unique local solution exists. Lipschitz failure → possible non-uniqueness (e.g., $y' = y^{1/2}$, $y(0)=0$ has infinitely many solutions).

2. Linear vs. Nonlinear

Linear DEs admit superposition and have well-developed solution theory (variation of parameters, Green's functions, spectral methods). Nonlinear DEs can exhibit chaos, bifurcations, and blow-up in finite time — qualitatively richer and analytically harder.

3. Phase Portraits and Qualitative Theory

Even without explicit solutions, the geometry of the vector field $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ reveals equilibria, limit cycles, stable/unstable manifolds, and bifurcation structure. This is the core of Poincaré's program.

4. Green's Functions

For a linear operator $\mathcal{L}$, the Green's function $G(x, s)$ satisfies $\mathcal{L}G = \delta(x-s)$. The solution to $\mathcal{L}y = f$ is then:

$$y(x) = \int G(x,s)\,f(s)\,ds$$

This encodes the impulse response and connects DEs to integral equations and functional analysis.

5. Numerical Methods

When closed forms fail: Euler, Runge-Kutta (RK4 is the workhorse), Adams-Bashforth (multistep), and stiff-equation solvers (implicit methods like BDF). For PDEs: finite differences, finite elements, spectral methods. Stability (Lax equivalence theorem) and consistency must both hold for convergence.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research frontiers include:

  • Neural ODEs: parameterizing the derivative $\dot{\mathbf{h}} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}, t)$ with a neural network, treating depth as continuous time. Enables memory-efficient training via the adjoint method.
  • Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): embedding PDE residuals into the loss function; competitive on forward/inverse problems where data is sparse.
  • Operator learning (DeepONet, Fourier Neural Operator): learning mappings between function spaces — effectively learning solution operators for families of PDEs.
  • Stochastic PDEs: rough path theory (Hairer's regularity structures, 2014 Fields Medal) provides rigorous foundations for SPDEs previously ill-defined under classical Itô calculus.
  • Long-time behavior: proving ergodicity, mixing rates, and invariant measure properties for dissipative PDEs (e.g., Navier-Stokes, KPZ equation).

Discussion Prompts

  1. Existence vs. computability: Picard-Lindelöf guarantees a unique solution to many IVPs, yet most lack closed forms. What does this say about the gap between mathematical existence and constructive knowledge — and where does numerical approximation sit in that gap?
  1. Discretization choices: Finite difference, finite element, and spectral methods each discretize the same PDE differently, with different error profiles and stability properties. When is spectral convergence worth the non-locality, and when do finite elements win on complex geometry?
  1. Neural ODEs and physical modeling: Neural ODEs and PINNs inject ML into DE-solving, but they optimize for empirical loss rather than rigorous error bounds. Does this represent a genuine paradigm shift in scientific computing, or a heuristic tool whose domain of trustworthy application remains poorly delineated?
