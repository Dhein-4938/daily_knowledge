Differential Equations✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A differential equation is an equation relating an unknown function $f$ and one or more of its derivatives. The order is the highest derivative that appears; the degree is the power of that derivative (when polynomial).

The general form of an $n$-th order ODE:

$$F\!\left(x,\, y,\, y',\, y'',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n)}\right) = 0$$

For PDEs, the unknown depends on multiple variables: $u = u(x_1, \ldots, x_k)$, and the equation involves partial derivatives $\partial u / \partial x_i$.

Classification axes:

| Axis | Values |

|---|---|

| Variable count | ODE vs. PDE |

| Linearity | Linear vs. nonlinear |

| Homogeneity | Homogeneous vs. inhomogeneous |

| Order | 1st, 2nd, … |

A linear ODE has the form $\sum_{k=0}^n a_k(x)\, y^{(k)} = g(x)$. Nonlinearity — e.g., $y'' + \sin y = 0$ (pendulum) — destroys superposition and makes closed-form solutions rare.

Why It Matters

DEs are the native language of continuous dynamics. Nearly every law of physics is a differential equation: Newton's second law $F = m\ddot{x}$, Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger's equation, the Navier–Stokes equations. Beyond physics:

  • Economics: optimal control (Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman PDE), Black–Scholes option pricing
  • Biology: epidemic models (SIR), neural firing (Hodgkin–Huxley)
  • Engineering: control systems, structural analysis, heat transfer
TIP: Deeper connection
Every ODE system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = F(\mathbf{x})$ defines a vector field on state space. Solutions are integral curves — geometry and analysis interlock here. This is why Differential Geometry and Dynamical Systems Theory are natural companions.

Key Details

1. Existence and Uniqueness (Picard–Lindelöf)

For the IVP $y' = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0)=y_0$: if $f$ is continuous and Lipschitz in $y$ on a rectangle around $(x_0, y_0)$, a unique local solution exists. Lipschitz failure signals potential blow-up or branching.

2. Linear Systems and the Matrix Exponential

$\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}$ has the exact solution $\mathbf{x}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{x}_0$. The eigenstructure of $A$ governs stability: all eigenvalues with $\text{Re}(\lambda) < 0$ → asymptotically stable equilibrium. This is the backbone of Linear Stability Analysis.

3. The Fundamental Solution / Green's Function

For a linear operator $\mathcal{L}u = f$, the Green's function $G(x, s)$ satisfies $\mathcal{L}_x G = \delta(x-s)$. The particular solution is then:

$$u(x) = \int G(x,s)\, f(s)\, ds$$

A powerful inversion framework linking PDEs to Functional Analysis.

4. Bifurcations in Nonlinear DEs

As a parameter $\mu$ varies, qualitative behavior can change abruptly — saddle-node, Hopf, pitchfork bifurcations. These are not perturbative phenomena; they require global geometric analysis (Phase Portraits, center manifold reduction).

5. Numerical Methods

When closed forms fail, discretization takes over. Runge–Kutta schemes (RK4 is the workhorse), multistep Adams methods, and stiff-equation solvers (BDF, implicit Runge–Kutta) each trade stability, accuracy, and cost differently. Stiffness — widely separated timescales — is the dominant practical challenge.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include:

  • Neural ODEs: parameterizing the vector field with a neural network and backpropagating through the ODE solver via the adjoint method — bridges DEs and deep learning.
  • Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): embedding PDE residuals into the loss function; promising for inverse problems and high-dimensional PDEs where classical methods hit the curse of dimensionality.
  • Mean-field and kinetic PDEs: Vlasov–Fokker–Planck equations for large particle systems are seeing renewed rigor alongside applications in multi-agent learning and interacting diffusions.
  • Data-driven discovery of DEs (SINDy and variants): sparse regression over a library of candidate terms to recover governing equations from trajectory data.

Discussion Prompts

  1. The Picard–Lindelöf theorem guarantees only local existence. What geometric or analytical features of a DE cause solutions to blow up in finite time, and how can one detect this without solving explicitly?
  1. Neural ODEs treat network depth as a continuous variable. What are the genuine mathematical trade-offs vs. discrete residual networks — and does the adjoint method introduce approximation errors that accumulate differently than standard backprop?
  1. Stiffness is often described informally as "widely separated timescales," but can be made precise via the eigenvalues of the Jacobian. How should this inform the choice between explicit and implicit solvers in practice, and when does operator splitting offer a better decomposition?
