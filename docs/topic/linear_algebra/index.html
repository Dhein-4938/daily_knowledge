Linear Algebra✓ reviewed

Math · Algebra  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Linear algebra is the study of vector spaces, linear maps between them, and their coordinate representations via matrices. Its atomic object is the linear equation: $$a_1 x_1 + a_2 x_2 + \cdots + a_n x_n = b$$ A system of such equations is compactly encoded as $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$.

The central abstraction is the linear map $T: V \to W$ satisfying $T(\alpha u + \beta v) = \alpha T(u) + \beta T(v)$. Every finite-dimensional linear map, once bases are chosen, is a matrix — making matrices both the computational engine and a coordinate-dependent representation of a coordinate-free object.

Key structures: vector spaces, subspaces, span, linear independence, basis, dimension, eigenvalues/eigenvectors, inner products, orthogonality, and canonical decompositions (LU, QR, SVD, eigendecomposition).

Why It Matters

Linear algebra is arguably the most load-bearing branch of applied mathematics. Its reach:

  • Geometry: Lines, planes, rotations, reflections, and projections are all linear (or affine) maps. Differential geometry linearizes curved spaces via the tangent space.
  • Differential equations: Solution spaces of linear ODEs/PDEs are vector spaces; spectral methods diagonalize operators.
  • Numerical analysis: Essentially every numerical algorithm reduces to solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ or computing eigenvalues.
  • Machine learning: Neural network layers are affine maps; attention mechanisms are matrix multiplications; PCA, SVD, and kernel methods are pure linear algebra.
  • Nonlinear problems: The Fréchet derivative at a point is the best linear approximation — so even nonlinear analysis locally reduces to linear algebra.
TIP: The Linearization Principle
Almost every local analysis of a smooth map $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ near a point $\mathbf{x}_0$ reduces to studying $Df(\mathbf{x}_0)$, the Jacobian matrix. Linear algebra isn't just useful for linear problems — it's the foundation for handling all smooth problems locally.

Key Details

  1. Rank-nullity theorem: For $T: V \to W$, $\dim(\ker T) + \dim(\operatorname{im} T) = \dim V$. This is the fundamental accounting identity — solutions exist iff $\mathbf{b} \in \operatorname{im}(A)$, and solution sets are affine subspaces of dimension $\dim(\ker A)$.
  1. Spectral theorem: A real symmetric matrix $A = A^\top$ is orthogonally diagonalizable: $A = Q\Lambda Q^\top$. This is the reason symmetric matrices appear everywhere in physics and statistics — their eigendecomposition is numerically stable and geometrically meaningful.
  1. Singular Value Decomposition (SVD): Every $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ factors as $A = U\Sigma V^\top$ with $U, V$ orthogonal and $\Sigma$ diagonal with non-negative entries. SVD reveals the true geometry of any linear map: it maps the right singular vectors to scaled left singular vectors. Numerically, it's the gold standard for rank estimation and pseudoinverse computation.
  1. Conditioning: The condition number $\kappa(A) = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ measures sensitivity of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ to perturbations. A large $\kappa$ signals near-singularity: small changes in $\mathbf{b}$ cause large swings in $\mathbf{x}$. This is the practical bottleneck in scientific computing, not just solvability in principle.
  1. Tensor products & multilinearity: Linear algebra extends naturally to tensors via the tensor product $V \otimes W$. Bilinear and multilinear maps are tamed by universal properties of tensor products — this bridge connects to representation theory, quantum mechanics, and deep learning (where weight tensors are ubiquitous).

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Randomized linear algebra: Randomized SVD and sketching algorithms (e.g., via Johnson-Lindenstrauss) solve high-dimensional problems in sublinear time with provable approximation guarantees. Used in large-scale ML pipelines.
  • Linear inverse problems with learned priors: Recent work (e.g., arXiv 2506, Exact Posterior Score Estimation) derives the exact posterior score in closed form for linear Gaussian inverse problems under diffusion model priors: posterior sampling reduces to denoising at an operator-dependent shifted pivot under anisotropic noise — a striking intersection of linear algebra, Bayesian inference, and generative modeling.
  • Numerical FEM eigenvalue problems: Nitsche-based weak boundary enforcement for elliptic eigenvalue problems (arXiv 2506) uses compact operator theory to derive convergence rates and adaptive a posteriori estimators — a live example of linear algebra driving PDE numerics.
  • Structured matrix algorithms: Exploiting sparsity, hierarchical structure (H-matrices, FFT), and low-rank structure to beat $O(n^3)$ dense complexity remains an open engineering problem at scale.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Coordinate freedom vs. computation: The abstract definition of a linear map is basis-independent, but matrix computation requires a choice of basis. How does the SVD reconcile these — and what does it mean that singular values are basis-invariant but singular vectors are not (up to unitary freedom)?
  1. Why is conditioning the "real" measure of difficulty? The condition number $\kappa(A)$ determines numerical sensitivity, but it's not intrinsic to the mathematical problem — it depends on the norm and scaling. How should one think about ill-conditioning: as a property of the problem, the representation, or the algorithm?
  1. Linearization as epistemology: Almost all of physics and engineering proceeds by linearizing around equilibria. What are the fundamental limits of this strategy — when does the linear approximation mislead rather than inform, and what structure (e.g., Lie brackets, curvature) captures what linearization misses?
