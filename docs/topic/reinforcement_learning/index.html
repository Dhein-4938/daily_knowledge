Reinforcement Learning✓ reviewed

Tech · Machine Learning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is the study of how an agent should select actions in a Markov Decision Process (MDP) to maximize cumulative reward, without prior knowledge of the environment's transition dynamics.

Formally, an MDP is a tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma)$ where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ the action space, $P(s' \mid s, a)$ the (unknown) transition kernel, $R(s, a)$ the reward function, and $\gamma \in [0,1)$ the discount factor. The agent's objective is to find a policy $\pi^*$ maximizing expected discounted return:

$$\pi^* = \arg\max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_\pi \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t) \right]$$

Unlike supervised learning (labeled data) or unsupervised learning (structure discovery), RL learns through interaction: the agent acts, receives a scalar reward signal, and updates its behavior accordingly. The critical tension at every step is the exploration–exploitation dilemma — the agent must balance sampling novel actions to reduce uncertainty against committing to the currently best-known action.

Why It Matters

RL is the natural framework for any sequential decision problem where feedback is delayed and the environment is partially or fully unknown. Key application domains:

  • Robotics & Control: locomotion, manipulation, sim-to-real transfer
  • Game AI: superhuman performance in Chess, Go (AlphaZero), Atari, StarCraft II
  • LLM Alignment: RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) fine-tunes language models toward human preferences, making RL central to modern AI safety work
  • Operations Research: scheduling, logistics, resource allocation
TIP: Connection to Dynamic Programming
RL can be seen as sample-based approximate dynamic programming. Classical DP methods (value iteration, policy iteration) require full knowledge of $P$; RL replaces exact Bellman updates with Monte Carlo or TD estimates drawn from experience — scaling to problems where the model is intractable or unavailable.

Key Details

  1. Temporal Difference Learning — TD methods like Q-learning and SARSA bootstrap value estimates from other estimates rather than waiting for full episode returns. Q-learning's update rule, $Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha\left[r + \gamma \max_{a'}Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)\right]$, is off-policy and converges to $Q^*$ under mild conditions.
  1. Policy Gradient Methods — Rather than learning a value function, REINFORCE and actor-critic methods directly optimize $\pi_\theta$ via gradient ascent on $\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)]$. This handles continuous action spaces naturally but suffers high variance.
  1. Function Approximation & Deep RL — Neural networks (DQN, PPO, SAC) allow generalization across large state spaces. They also introduce instability: bootstrapping + off-policy sampling + nonlinear approximation form the deadly triad that can cause divergence.
  1. Reward Hacking — Agents routinely find unexpected strategies that maximize the proxy reward while violating designer intent. This is a core challenge in alignment and motivates learned reward models (e.g., RLHF, DPO).
  1. Sample Efficiency — Model-free RL typically requires millions of environment interactions. Model-based RL addresses this by learning $\hat{P}$ to generate synthetic rollouts, but introduces compounding model error.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers as of early 2026:

  • RL for generative models: Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) and its physics-aware variants (e.g., PhysMoDPO for humanoid motion) reframe reward-signal alignment without an explicit reward model, offering training stability over classical RLHF pipelines.
  • Fine-grained reward modeling: Work like Visual-ERM shows that coarse embedding-similarity rewards are insufficient for vision-to-code tasks; multimodal generative reward models providing interpretable, pixel-level feedback substantially outperform rule-based alternatives.
  • Offline RL & conservative methods: Learning purely from logged datasets (no live interaction) via methods like IQL and TD3+BC addresses deployment safety in robotics and healthcare.
  • Hierarchical & multi-agent RL: Decomposing long-horizon tasks and coordinating multiple agents remain open problems with deep connections to Game Theory and Control Theory.

Discussion Prompts

  1. The Bellman equation assumes the Markov property — how does partial observability (POMDPs) break this, and what architectural choices in deep RL implicitly compensate for it?
  2. DPO sidesteps explicit reward modeling by reparameterizing the RLHF objective. What are the theoretical assumptions this requires, and where might they fail in practice?
  3. Sample efficiency in model-free RL scales poorly with state-space dimensionality. Is there a principled limit analogous to the PAC learning sample complexity, and what role does inductive bias play?
