Graph Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Combinatorics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A graph $G = (V, E)$ consists of a set of vertices $V$ and a set of edges $E \subseteq \binom{V}{2}$ (for undirected) or $E \subseteq V \times V$ (for directed). Edges encode pairwise relations between objects.

The fundamental distinction between graph types sets the stage for entirely different theories:

  • Undirected graph: edge $\{u, v\} = \{v, u\}$ — symmetry is baked in
  • Directed graph (digraph): edge $(u, v) \neq (v, u)$ — asymmetry enables modeling flow, causality, hierarchy
  • Weighted graph: each edge carries a scalar $w: E \to \mathbb{R}$, enabling optimization problems over path costs

Key invariants include the degree $\deg(v) = |\{e \in E : v \in e\}|$, with the handshaking lemma guaranteeing:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$

For digraphs, indegree and outdegree split this count, and the adjacency matrix $A$ where $A_{ij} = 1$ iff $(i,j) \in E$ becomes a primary algebraic handle on the structure.

Why It Matters

Graph theory is arguably the most versatile branch of discrete mathematics — it is the natural language of relational structure. Applications span:

  • Computer science: shortest paths (Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford), network flow (max-flow min-cut), scheduling (graph coloring), compiler register allocation
  • Biology: protein interaction networks, phylogenetic trees, connectomes
  • Social science: community detection, influence propagation, small-world phenomena
  • Physics: spin models on lattices, tensor network contraction, Feynman diagrams as graphs
  • Cryptography & complexity: graph isomorphism sits at an unusual complexity-theoretic position — not known to be in P nor NP-complete
TIP: Spectral Bridge
The eigenvalues of the Laplacian $L = D - A$ (where $D$ is the degree matrix) encode global connectivity. The second-smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ (the algebraic connectivity or Fiedler value) measures how well-connected a graph is — $\lambda_2 = 0$ iff the graph is disconnected. This spectral perspective connects graph theory to differential geometry via discrete Hodge theory.

Key Details

  1. Graph isomorphism — Determining whether two graphs $G \cong H$ (same structure up to vertex relabeling) is in complexity class GI, believed strictly between P and NP-complete. The Weisfeiler-Leman algorithm gives practical heuristics and underlies the expressiveness limits of Graph Neural Networks.
  1. Planarity — A graph is planar iff it embeds in $\mathbb{R}^2$ without edge crossings. Kuratowski's theorem: $G$ is planar $\iff$ it contains no subdivision of $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$. Planar graphs satisfy Euler's formula $V - E + F = 2$ and are always 4-colorable (Four Color Theorem, proved 1976 with computer assistance).
  1. Trees — A connected acyclic graph on $n$ vertices has exactly $n-1$ edges. Cayley's formula gives $n^{n-2}$ labeled trees on $n$ vertices — a striking result proved elegantly via Prüfer sequences.
  1. Graph minors — Robertson–Seymour theorem: graphs are well-quasi-ordered under the minor relation, implying every minor-closed family has a finite forbidden-minor characterization. This is one of the deepest results in combinatorics.
  1. Random graphs — The Erdős–Rényi model $G(n,p)$ exhibits sharp phase transitions: at $p = \frac{1}{n}$, a giant connected component suddenly emerges. This threshold behavior connects graph theory to statistical physics.
WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Active research areas like graph neural networks, quantum graph algorithms, and parameterized complexity results evolve rapidly — specific state-of-the-art claims here may be superseded.

Current State

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have renewed theoretical interest in graph isomorphism: the WL hierarchy precisely characterizes which graph properties a GNN can distinguish, spurring work on higher-order GNNs and equivariant architectures. Parameterized complexity of graph problems (treewidth, treedepth decompositions) is an active frontier, as is the study of graph limits (graphons) which give a analytic framework for sequences of dense graphs. Quantum computing promises polynomial speedups for certain graph problems via amplitude amplification and quantum walk algorithms.
