Transformer Architecture✓ reviewed

Tech · Machine Learning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO
A Transformer is a sequence-to-sequence neural architecture that dispenses entirely with recurrence and convolution, instead relying on scaled dot-product attention to model dependencies between all positions simultaneously.

The fundamental operation is Multi-Head Self-Attention. Given input matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, each head computes:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

where $Q = XW^Q$, $K = XW^K$, $V = XW^V$ are learned projections. Multiple heads run in parallel, their outputs concatenated and re-projected:

$$\text{MHA}(X) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)W^O$$

A Transformer block wraps MHA with residual connections, layer normalization, and a position-wise FFN (typically two linear layers with a GELU/ReLU nonlinearity). Since attention is permutation-invariant, positional encodings — sinusoidal, learned, or rotary (RoPE) — are injected to convey sequence order.

Why It Matters

Transformers unified NLP, vision, audio, and multimodal modeling under a single inductive-bias-light framework. Their $O(n^2)$ attention cost is the central scalability bottleneck, but their parallelism across positions (unlike RNNs) makes them GPU/TPU-friendly at training time. The architecture underlies GPT, BERT, T5, ViT, Whisper, AlphaFold 2, and virtually every frontier model as of 2026.

TIP
The FFN sublayer (often 4× wider than the residual stream) acts as a key-value memory — individual neurons store factual associations. This explains why scaling parameters in FFN layers improves knowledge retrieval more than scaling attention heads alone.

Key Details

  1. Residual stream as communication bus. Each layer reads from and writes to the same $d_\text{model}$-dimensional residual stream. Mechanistic interpretability frames this as parallel "circuits" adding their contributions, making superposition the dominant compression strategy.
  1. Positional encoding trade-offs. Sinusoidal (absolute) encodings generalize poorly beyond training length. Relative encodings (ALiBi, RoPE) enable better length extrapolation; RoPE encodes position as a rotation in complex space, achieving $\text{Re}[q e^{im\theta} \cdot (k e^{in\theta})^*] = \text{Re}[qk^* e^{i(m-n)\theta}]$, making attention scores depend only on relative offset $m-n$.
  1. Attention sink phenomenon. Empirically, the first token (often <BOS>) accumulates disproportionate attention mass across many heads — a consequence of softmax needing to "dump" probability somewhere when all content is irrelevant. Streaming inference systems (e.g., StreamingLLM) exploit this by always retaining the sink token in the KV cache.
  1. KV cache and inference cost. Autoregressive decoding materializes a KV cache of size $O(n \cdot n_\text{layers} \cdot 2d)$ per sequence. This is frequently the binding memory constraint at inference, motivating grouped-query attention (GQA) and multi-query attention (MQA) which share key/value heads across query heads.
  1. Training instability at scale. Pre-LayerNorm (applying norm before the sublayer) is more stable than post-LN for large models; gradient norms in post-LN grow with depth and require careful warmup. Most modern LLMs use pre-LN or RMSNorm variants.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts as of mid-2026 include reasoning-aware retrieval augmentation: rather than grounding a Transformer in semantically similar documents, frameworks like RA-RFT train retrievers to surface analogous reasoning patterns, improving mathematical reasoning benchmarks significantly over standard RLHF/GRPO fine-tuning. This signals a shift from treating retrieval as a knowledge lookup to treating it as a cognitive scaffold.

Separately, knowledge graph integration (e.g., Agents-K1) aims to give Transformers structured access to entity-relation graphs extracted from millions of papers, enabling multi-hop scientific reasoning that flat token context windows handle poorly. Positional encoding research has also expanded to graph domains, where truncated spectral and walk-based PEs for GNNs are now understood to have fundamentally different expressive power under truncation — a finding that likely informs PE design for structured-input Transformers.

Discussion Prompts

  1. The FFN layers store factual associations, but attention heads route information — does this suggest a clean functional decomposition, or do the roles blur at scale?
  2. RoPE's relative-offset property solves training-length generalization in theory; why do models still degrade on very long contexts in practice, and what does that reveal about context utilization?
  3. If reasoning-aware retrieval (RA-RFT) consistently outperforms semantic retrieval for multi-step problems, what does that imply about how Transformers internally represent "problem type" versus "problem content"?
