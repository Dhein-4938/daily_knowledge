Neural ODEs✓ reviewed

Tech · Machine Learning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

A Neural ODE replaces discrete layer stacking in a residual network with a continuous-depth analogue. Instead of defining a sequence of transformations $\mathbf{h}_{t+1} = \mathbf{h}_t + f(\mathbf{h}_t, \theta)$, it posits a hidden state governed by an ordinary differential equation:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{h}(t)}{dt} = f\bigl(\mathbf{h}(t),\, t,\, \theta\bigr)$$

where $f$ is a neural network. The output at "depth" $T$ is obtained by integrating from an initial condition $\mathbf{h}(0) = \mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{h}(T) = \mathbf{h}(0) + \int_0^T f\bigl(\mathbf{h}(t), t, \theta\bigr)\, dt$$

Any black-box ODE solver (Dormand–Prince, Adams–Bashforth, etc.) can approximate this integral. The solver chooses its own step sizes adaptively, making the computational graph implicit rather than fixed.

INFO
The number of function evaluations (NFE) of the ODE solver plays the role of "depth." Tighter error tolerances → more NFE → higher effective depth. This depth is dynamic and input-dependent.

Why It Matters

Memory efficiency. Backpropagation through the solver would store all intermediate states. Instead, Chen et al. (2018) derived the adjoint sensitivity method: integrate a co-state $\mathbf{a}(t) = -\partial L/\partial \mathbf{h}(t)$ backwards in time, requiring only $O(1)$ memory regardless of integration depth.

$$\frac{d\mathbf{a}}{dt} = -\mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{h}}$$

Continuous-time modeling. Time-series with irregular sampling (clinical records, financial ticks, physical sensors) fit naturally — no need to impute or bin. Latent ODEs combine a recurrent encoder with an ODE decoder to learn dynamics from sparse, irregular observations.

Normalizing flows. The FFJORD variant replaces change-of-variables determinant computation $\log|\det J|$ with the instantaneous form:

$$\frac{d \log p(\mathbf{z}(t))}{dt} = -\operatorname{tr}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}}\right)$$

This makes exact maximum-likelihood training of continuous flows tractable via Hutchinson's trace estimator ($O(d)$ vs. $O(d^3)$).

Key Details

  1. Adjoint method caveats. Numerical errors in the reverse-time integration can cause gradient inaccuracies. Checkpointing intermediate states or using symplectic solvers mitigates this; the "vanilla" adjoint can be unstable for stiff systems.
  1. Stiffness and solver choice. Many physical and learned dynamics are stiff (fast and slow timescales coexist). Explicit solvers (RK45) become prohibitively expensive; implicit or semi-implicit solvers (Radau, BDF) are needed but harder to differentiate through.
  1. Augmented Neural ODEs (ANODE). The universal approximation argument for Neural ODEs requires the vector field to be non-crossing (by uniqueness of ODE solutions). Augmenting the state with extra dimensions $[\mathbf{h}(t), \mathbf{a}(t)] \in \mathbb{R}^{d+p}$ breaks this constraint and dramatically expands expressibility.
  1. Relation to ResNets. Euler discretization of the ODE ($\Delta t = 1$) recovers the standard residual block. Neural ODEs are thus the continuum limit of very deep, weight-tied ResNets.
  1. Hamiltonian / symplectic variants. Constraining $f$ to derive from a learned Hamiltonian $H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$ enforces energy conservation and reversibility — critical for physical simulation and molecular dynamics.
TIP
The adjoint method is essentially the continuous analogue of backpropagation: both propagate a co-state signal backward through a computational process. This unification hints at why Neural ODEs sit at the intersection of optimal control theory and deep learning.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active lines of work include:

  • Score-based diffusion meets Neural ODEs. Diffusion models can be cast as SDEs/ODEs via the probability flow ODE (Song et al.), enabling deterministic sampling and exact likelihoods. Efficient ODE solvers (DPM-Solver, DDIM) are a major practical lever.
  • Neural PDEs and operator learning. Extending the ODE framework to PDEs via neural operators (FNO, DeepONet) for weather, fluid, and quantum systems.
  • Scalability. Training cost scales with NFE; recent work on step distillation and flow matching reduces this, trading the full solver for a learned few-step surrogate.
  • Theoretical expressiveness. Characterizing which flows are learnable and when augmentation is necessary remains open; connections to optimal transport (Brenier maps, Wasserstein geodesics) are being formalized.

Discussion Prompts

  1. The adjoint sensitivity method assumes the ODE solver produces a smooth, reversible trajectory — but adaptive solvers introduce discontinuous step-size jumps. How does this interact with gradient accuracy in practice, and are there regimes where the adjoint is genuinely unreliable?
  1. Flow matching reframes generative modeling as learning a vector field interpolating between noise and data distributions, bypassing the ODE solver during training. Does this make Neural ODEs obsolete as generative tools, or do exact-likelihood applications preserve their niche?
  1. Hamiltonian Neural ODEs conserve energy by construction, which is a strong inductive bias. When modeling real physical systems with dissipation (friction, viscosity), is it better to break the Hamiltonian constraint deliberately, or to model dissipation as a separate perturbation term?
