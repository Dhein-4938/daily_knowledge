Computational Complexity✓ reviewed

Math · Complexity Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Computational complexity theory classifies problems by the minimum resources any algorithm requires to solve them — not the cost of a specific algorithm, but an intrinsic property of the problem itself.

A complexity class is a set of problems solvable within a given resource bound on a particular computational model. The two primary resources are time and space, measured as functions of input size $n$.

Formally, for a deterministic Turing machine:

$$\text{TIME}(f(n)) = \{ L \mid L \text{ decided in } O(f(n)) \text{ steps} \}$$

$$\mathbf{P} = \bigcup_{k \geq 1} \text{TIME}(n^k), \quad \mathbf{PSPACE} = \bigcup_{k \geq 1} \text{SPACE}(n^k)$$

The nondeterministic analogue gives $\mathbf{NP}$: problems whose solutions can be verified in polynomial time, or equivalently, solved in polynomial time on a nondeterministic TM.

Why It Matters

Complexity theory draws the map of what is computably feasible, not just computable. This matters across:

  • Cryptography: RSA and elliptic-curve schemes rest on the assumed hardness of integer factoring and discrete log — problems believed outside $\mathbf{P}$ but in $\mathbf{NP}$.
  • Algorithm design: Knowing a problem is $\mathbf{NP}$-hard licenses approximation, randomization, or parametric approaches rather than futile search for exact poly-time solutions.
  • AI and ML: PAC learning complexity, VC dimension bounds, and the tractability of inference in graphical models all reduce to complexity questions.
  • Quantum computing: Complexity classes like $\mathbf{BQP}$ (bounded-error quantum polynomial time) let us reason about quantum advantage rigorously.
TIP: The Real Insight
Complexity theory shifts the question from "how fast is this algorithm?" (analysis of algorithms) to "how fast can any algorithm be?" This is a fundamentally different — and harder — question, often requiring lower-bound arguments rather than constructive proofs.

Key Details

1. Reductions and Completeness

A problem $A$ is polynomial-time reducible to $B$ ($A \leq_p B$) if any instance of $A$ can be transformed to an instance of $B$ in poly-time. $B$ is $\mathbf{NP}$-complete if $B \in \mathbf{NP}$ and every $A \in \mathbf{NP}$ reduces to $B$. Cook-Levin established 3-SAT as the first such problem.

2. The Complexity Hierarchy

$$\mathbf{P} \subseteq \mathbf{NP} \subseteq \mathbf{PSPACE} \subseteq \mathbf{EXPTIME}$$

All inclusions are known, but only $\mathbf{P} \subsetneq \mathbf{EXPTIME}$ is strict (by the time hierarchy theorem). Whether $\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{NP}$ remains open.

3. Randomization

$\mathbf{BPP}$ admits bounded-error probabilistic poly-time algorithms. Most believe $\mathbf{BPP} = \mathbf{P}$ — i.e., randomness provides no asymptotic advantage — but this is unproven. Derandomization connects tightly to circuit lower bounds.

4. Circuit Complexity

Boolean circuits offer a non-uniform model: $\mathbf{P/poly}$ captures problems solvable by polynomial-size circuits. Proving super-polynomial lower bounds for explicit functions is one of the deepest open problems; progress has stalled since Razborov–Smolensky (1987) for $\mathbf{AC}^0[p]$.

5. Oracle Separations

Relativization shows limits of proof techniques: there exist oracles $A, B$ such that $\mathbf{P}^A = \mathbf{NP}^A$ and $\mathbf{P}^B \neq \mathbf{NP}^B$. Any proof resolving $\mathbf{P}$ vs. $\mathbf{NP}$ must be non-relativizing.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include:

  • $\mathbf{P}$ vs. $\mathbf{NP}$: The central open problem; no credible resolution in sight. Ryan Williams' 2010 result ($\mathbf{NEXP} \not\subset \mathbf{ACC}^0$) was a rare circuit lower bound breakthrough.
  • GCT (Geometric Complexity Theory): Mulmuley and Sohoni's program uses algebraic geometry and representation theory to approach $\mathbf{P}$ vs. $\mathbf{NP}$; progress is slow but structurally novel.
  • Fine-grained complexity: Rather than poly vs. non-poly, this asks whether problems solvable in $O(n^2)$ can be solved in $O(n^{1.99})$. SETH (Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis) underpins many conditional lower bounds.
  • Quantum complexity: Clarifying the relationship between $\mathbf{BQP}$, $\mathbf{PH}$, and $\mathbf{PSPACE}$; it's known $\mathbf{BQP} \not\subset \mathbf{PH}$ relative to some oracle.
  • Meta-complexity: Studying the complexity of complexity problems themselves — e.g., is MCSP ($\text{Minimum Circuit Size Problem}$) $\mathbf{NP}$-hard?

Discussion Prompts

  1. The time hierarchy theorem proves $\mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{EXPTIME}$ via diagonalization — yet the same technique cannot separate $\mathbf{P}$ from $\mathbf{NP}$ due to relativization barriers. What does this reveal about the structure of a potential $\mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP}$ proof?
  1. Fine-grained complexity replaces the coarse poly/non-poly lens with exact exponent distinctions. Is this a deeper or more pragmatic theory than classical complexity, and what assumptions (like SETH) are we betting on when we use it?
  1. If $\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{NP}$ were proven constructively — yielding an explicit poly-time SAT algorithm with a large constant — how would that change the practical landscape of cryptography, optimization, and AI versus a merely existential proof?
