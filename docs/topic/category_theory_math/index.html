Category Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Abstract Algebra  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Category theory is the mathematics of structure and structure-preserving maps. Where set theory asks "what are things made of?", category theory asks "how do things relate?". It was introduced by Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane (1945) while formalizing constructions in algebraic topology.

INFO: Definition: Category
A category $\mathcal{C}$ consists of: - A collection of objects $\text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ - For each pair $A, B \in \text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$, a collection of morphisms $\text{Hom}(A, B)$ - A composition law: if $f \in \text{Hom}(A,B)$ and $g \in \text{Hom}(B,C)$, then $g \circ f \in \text{Hom}(A,C)$ - Identity morphisms $\text{id}_A \in \text{Hom}(A,A)$ for each object Subject to associativity: $(h \circ g) \circ f = h \circ (g \circ f)$, and unit laws: $f \circ \text{id}_A = f = \text{id}_B \circ f$.

A functor $F : \mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2$ is a structure-preserving map between categories — it sends objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms while respecting composition:

$$F(g \circ f) = F(g) \circ F(f), \quad F(\text{id}_A) = \text{id}_{F(A)}$$

A natural transformation $\eta : F \Rightarrow G$ between two functors provides a coherent family of morphisms $\eta_A : F(A) \to G(A)$ such that for every morphism $f : A \to B$, the naturality square commutes: $G(f) \circ \eta_A = \eta_B \circ F(f)$.

Why It Matters

Category theory is uniquely powerful because it abstracts away the interior of mathematical objects. The same categorical construction — limits, colimits, adjunctions — appears independently in topology, algebra, logic, and computer science. This lets you prove theorems once and apply them everywhere.

TIP: The Adjunction Insight
Adjoint functors $F \dashv G$ (where $\text{Hom}(FA, B) \cong \text{Hom}(A, GB)$ naturally) capture an enormous range of mathematical duality: free/forgetful, product/exponential, quantifier/substitution. MacLane's dictum: "Adjoint functors arise everywhere."

In functional programming, category theory is the semantic backbone. Monads are monoids in the category of endofunctors; the fmap of Haskell's Functor typeclass is literally a functor mapping. In logic, the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence triangulates propositions, types, and objects of a cartesian closed category.

Key Details

  1. Morphisms over elements. A monoid is a category with one object — its elements are morphisms. A group is the same with all morphisms invertible. A preorder is a category where $|\text{Hom}(A,B)| \leq 1$. The categorical perspective unifies these with no extra effort.
  1. Universal properties. Products, coproducts, limits, and colimits are defined purely by universal properties — existence and uniqueness up to unique isomorphism. This is categorically the right way to define constructions; it guarantees they're canonical.
  1. Yoneda Lemma. Arguably the most important theorem in category theory:

$$\text{Nat}(\text{Hom}(A, -),\ F) \cong F(A)$$

An object is completely determined by how other objects map into it. This grounds representability and is the categorical foundation of sheaf theory and algebraic geometry.

  1. Size issues. Naïve category theory runs into set-theoretic paradoxes (the category of all sets). Handling this requires Grothendieck universes, or working with small vs. large categories explicitly.
  1. Contravariant functors. A contravariant functor $F : \mathcal{C}^{op} \to \mathcal{D}$ reverses arrows. This is how cohomology arises from homology — it's the same structure, dualised.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include Higher Category Theory (∞-categories, (∞,1)-categories via quasi-categories or complete Segal spaces), which has transformed modern homotopy theory through Lurie's Higher Topos Theory. Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) uses categorical semantics to give a computational foundation for mathematics where identity is homotopy. Applied category theory is growing rapidly — compositional approaches appear in quantum computing (ZX-calculus), systems biology, and database schema migration.

Discussion Prompts

  2. Adjoint functors capture free/forgetful dualities across mathematics — can you identify an adjunction lurking in a domain you work in (databases, type systems, physics)?
  3. Higher category theory demands that equality itself be replaced by coherent homotopy equivalence. What does this mean for the foundations of proof?
