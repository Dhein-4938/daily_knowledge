Elliptic Curves✓ reviewed

Math · Number Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
An elliptic curve over a field $K$ (with $\text{char}(K) \neq 2, 3$) is a smooth, projective algebraic curve of genus one equipped with a distinguished identity point $O$. In affine coordinates it takes the Weierstrass form: $$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b, \quad a, b \in K$$ Non-singularity requires the discriminant condition: $$\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0$$ which ensures no cusps or self-intersections. The projective closure adds $O = [0:1:0]$ as the unique point at infinity.

The genus-one condition is load-bearing: genus zero gives a conic (topologically a sphere), while genus $\geq 2$ prohibits a group law by Hurwitz. Genus one is the unique sweet spot admitting both a non-trivial topology and an algebraic group structure.

Why It Matters

Elliptic curves sit at an extraordinary intersection of fields:

  • Number theory: Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem reduced the problem to showing every semistable elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ is modular (Taniyama–Shimura–Weil conjecture). The BSD conjecture — one of the seven Millennium Problems — concerns the rank of $E(\mathbb{Q})$.
  • Cryptography (ECC): The discrete logarithm problem on $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is believed harder than its multiplicative-group analogue, enabling shorter keys at equivalent security. Curve25519 and NIST P-256 underpin modern TLS.
  • Factorization: Lenstra's elliptic curve method (ECM) is the best known algorithm for finding mid-size factors, running in $O\!\left(e^{\sqrt{2\ln p \ln \ln p}}\right)$ time in the factor $p$.
TIP: Topology dictates algebra
Over $\mathbb{C}$, every elliptic curve is isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ for a lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z} + \tau\mathbb{Z}$, $\text{Im}(\tau) > 0$. The group law on the curve is addition mod $\Lambda$ — the torus topology forces commutativity and associativity without any algebraic sleight of hand.

Key Details

  1. The Group Law geometrically: Given points $P, Q \in E(K)$, draw the line $PQ$; it meets $E$ at a third point $R'$ (Bézout guarantees this in $\mathbb{P}^2$). Reflect $R'$ across the $x$-axis to obtain $P + Q$. The identity $O$ is the point at infinity, and $-P = (x, -y)$ for $P = (x,y)$.
  1. $j$-invariant classifies curves up to isomorphism (over $\bar{K}$):

$$j(E) = 1728 \cdot \frac{4a^3}{4a^3 + 27b^2}$$

Two curves are isomorphic over $\bar{K}$ iff they share the same $j$-invariant. Twists — curves with the same $j$ but non-isomorphic over $K$ — are classified by $H^1(\text{Gal}(\bar{K}/K), \text{Aut}(E))$.

  1. Mordell's Theorem: $E(\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \oplus E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$, where $r \geq 0$ is the rank and the torsion subgroup is finite (Mazur's theorem limits it to 15 possibilities). Computing $r$ in general remains open.
  1. Over finite fields: $|E(\mathbb{F}_p)|$ satisfies Hasse's bound $|{|E(\mathbb{F}_p)| - (p+1)}| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$. The Frobenius endomorphism $\phi: (x,y) \mapsto (x^p, y^p)$ satisfies $\phi^2 - t\phi + p = 0$ for trace $t$ with $|t| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$.
  1. Char 2 and 3: The short Weierstrass form breaks down. General Weierstrass: $y^2 + a_1 xy + a_3 y = x^3 + a_2 x^2 + a_4 x + a_6$. This matters practically — hardware implementations over $\mathbb{F}_{2^m}$ (binary fields) use distinct formulas.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • BSD conjecture: Partial results exist (rank 0 and 1 cases via Kolyvagin/Gross–Zagier), but the general case is open. Computational verification has been pushed to large ranks ($r \geq 28$ examples known).
  • Arithmetic statistics: Goldfeld's conjecture predicts average rank $= \frac{1}{2}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$; Bhargava–Shankar proved average rank $< 1$ (and $< 0.885$ in improved bounds).
  • Post-quantum concerns: ECC is broken by Shor's algorithm on a quantum computer. NIST's post-quantum standards (CRYSTALS-Kyber, etc.) do not use elliptic curves, though isogeny-based schemes (SIDH/CSIDH) attempted to — SIDH was broken in 2022 by Castryck–Decru.
  • $p$-adic and $\ell$-adic methods: Iwasawa theory studies $E$ over $\mathbb{Z}_p$-extensions; $\ell$-adic Tate modules encode arithmetic of $E$ via Galois representations.

Discussion Prompts

  1. The group law on an elliptic curve is defined by a geometric construction (chord-and-tangent), yet it is provably associative — a highly non-trivial algebraic fact. What is the cleanest proof of associativity, and what does it reveal about the structure?
  1. Hasse's bound $|E(\mathbb{F}_p)| \approx p + 1$ has an intuitive explanation via the "square root cancellation" heuristic for character sums. How does the Riemann Hypothesis for curves (Weil conjectures) formalize this, and what is the geometric content of that proof?
  1. SIDH's catastrophic 2022 break showed that isogenies between supersingular curves leak too much structure when auxiliary torsion points are revealed. What does this say more broadly about how extra structure in cryptographic group actions can be a liability rather than a feature?
