Theory of Evolution✓ reviewed

Science · Biology  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Evolution is the change in heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations, driven by processes acting on genetic variation: natural selection, genetic drift, mutation, and gene flow.

The four postulates underpinning natural selection (Darwin/Wallace, ~1858):

  1. Superfecundity — populations produce more offspring than can survive
  2. Variation — individuals differ in heritable traits
  3. Differential fitness — traits confer unequal survival/reproduction rates
  4. Heritability — fit traits are passed to offspring

This can be captured in the Price equation, which decomposes evolutionary change in a trait $z$ across one generation:

$$\Delta \bar{z} = \underbrace{\text{Cov}(w_i, z_i)/\bar{w}}_{\text{selection}} + \underbrace{E(w_i \Delta z_i)/\bar{w}}_{\text{transmission bias}}$$

where $w_i$ is individual fitness and $\bar{w}$ is mean population fitness. The first term is pure selection; the second captures developmental/mutational transmission effects.

Why It Matters

Evolution is the unifying framework of all biology — without it, comparative anatomy, molecular phylogenetics, antibiotic resistance, and vaccine design are incoherent. Its practical reach extends far:

  • Medicine: understanding pathogen evolution (SARS-CoV-2 variant emergence), cancer as somatic evolution, personalized genomics
  • Agriculture: artificial selection, resistance management in crops and pests
  • Computer science: evolutionary algorithms, genetic programming, neural architecture search inspired by fitness landscapes
  • Astrobiology: LUCA (Last Universal Common Ancestor, ~3.5–3.8 Ga) anchors hypotheses about life's origin and its possible forms elsewhere
TIP: Deep Connection — Fitness Landscapes
Sewall Wright's metaphor of a fitness landscape — where genotype space is mapped to fitness — bridges population genetics and complex systems theory. Epistasis creates rugged landscapes where gradient ascent (selection) gets stuck at local optima, a concept directly imported into machine learning as loss-surface analysis.

Key Details

1. The Modern Synthesis (~1930s–1950s)

Darwin lacked a mechanism of inheritance. The fusion of Mendelian genetics, population genetics (Fisher, Wright, Haldane), and paleontology into the Modern Synthesis resolved this: DNA is the physical substrate of heritable variation.

2. Neutral Theory (Kimura, 1968)

Most molecular evolution is not driven by selection but by random fixation of neutral mutations via genetic drift. The neutral substitution rate equals the mutation rate $\mu$, independent of population size — a non-obvious and initially controversial result.

$$k = \mu$$

This is foundational to molecular clocks used in phylogenetic dating.

3. Levels of Selection

Selection can operate at gene, individual, kin-group, or (controversially) species level. Hamilton's rule for kin selection: altruistic behavior evolves when

$$rb > c$$

where $r$ = relatedness, $b$ = benefit to recipient, $c$ = cost to actor.

4. Speciation Mechanisms

New species arise via reproductive isolation: allopatric (geographic separation), sympatric (ecological divergence without separation), and parapatric (along environmental gradients). The biological species concept (Mayr) defines species by reproductive isolation, but breaks down for asexual organisms and archaea.

5. Extended Evolutionary Synthesis

Post-2000 work incorporates phenomena the Modern Synthesis underemphasized: epigenetic inheritance, developmental plasticity, niche construction, and evolvability itself as a selectable trait.

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
The "Extended Evolutionary Synthesis" remains actively contested — consensus on how much it revises (vs. supplements) the Modern Synthesis is not yet settled as of this note's writing.

Current State

Active research frontiers include:

  • evo-devo (evolutionary developmental biology): how changes in gene regulatory networks produce morphological novelty with minimal mutational load
  • ancient DNA and paleogenomics: sequencing archaic hominins (Denisovans, Neanderthals) at population-genetic resolution, revealing introgression events
  • experimental evolution: evolving populations of E. coli (Lenski's LTEE, now >80,000 generations) directly observing mutation fixation dynamics
  • evolutionary medicine: co-evolutionary arms races between hosts and pathogens modeled as Red Queen dynamics
WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Ancient DNA methods and LTEE milestone generation counts are advancing rapidly; specific claims here reflect the state of knowledge through early 2026.

Discussion Prompts

  1. The neutral theory and natural selection are often framed as competing explanations for molecular evolution — but are they genuinely in tension, or do they operate at different timescales and levels of resolution?
  1. The Price equation is mathematically general enough to describe cultural evolution and learning dynamics. Does this generality make it a deep insight or an empty tautology?
  1. If evolvability (the capacity to produce heritable variation) is itself heritable and selectable, does this imply evolution has "meta-evolved" its own search strategy — and what does that imply for how we design evolutionary algorithms?
