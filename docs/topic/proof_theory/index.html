Proof Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO
Proof theory treats proofs as first-class mathematical objects — inductively defined data structures (trees, sequences, boxed lists) built from axioms and inference rules of a formal system. Its analysis is purely syntactic, operating on symbol manipulation rather than semantic interpretation.

The central objects of study are formal derivations. Given a logical system $\mathcal{L}$ with axiom schemas $\mathcal{A}$ and inference rules $\mathcal{R}$, a proof of $\phi$ is a finite tree where:

$$\frac{\Gamma_1 \vdash \phi_1 \quad \cdots \quad \Gamma_n \vdash \phi_n}{\Gamma \vdash \phi} \in \mathcal{R}$$

This structural view underpins all of proof theory — whether you're studying sequent calculi, natural deduction, or type-theoretic systems.

Why It Matters

Proof theory sits at the intersection of foundations, computation, and philosophy of mathematics. Its significance spans several domains:

  • Foundations of mathematics: Hilbert's program sought to justify all of mathematics via finitary proofs. Gödel's incompleteness theorems were proof-theoretic bombshells — establishing that no consistent extension of PA can prove its own consistency (within the system).
  • The Curry-Howard correspondence: Proofs in intuitionistic logic correspond directly to programs in typed lambda calculi ($\Pi$-types ↔ universal quantifiers, $\Sigma$-types ↔ existential quantifiers). Proof theory is type theory.
  • Automated reasoning: Proof-search algorithms (resolution, tableaux, sequent calculus search) power SMT solvers, proof assistants (Coq, Lean, Isabelle), and formal verification.
  • Computational complexity: Proof complexity studies the minimum size of proofs in propositional systems — directly connected to the $\mathbf{P}$ vs $\mathbf{NP}$ question.
TIP
The Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence extends the proofs-as-programs analogy to category theory: proofs ↔ programs ↔ morphisms. This triality makes proof theory a unifying language across logic, CS, and algebra.

Key Details

  1. Sequent Calculus vs. Natural Deduction: Gentzen's two formalisms. Sequents $\Gamma \vdash \Delta$ (multiple conclusions) make structural rules explicit — weakening, contraction, cut. The cut-elimination theorem (Gentzen's Hauptsatz) states that every proof with cuts has a cut-free equivalent; it's the syntactic analogue of interpolation and implies consistency.
  1. Ordinal Analysis: Assigns a proof-theoretic ordinal $|T|$ to a theory $T$ — the supremum of ordinals provably well-founded in $T$. For PA, $|PA| = \varepsilon_0$. For stronger systems like $\Pi^1_1\text{-CA}_0$, ordinals reach into the Bachmann–Howard hierarchy. This measures "how much transfinite induction" a theory encodes.
  1. Structural Proof Theory: Studies how the shape of proofs encodes logical structure. Substructural logics (linear, relevant, affine) arise by restricting structural rules. Linear logic, for instance, drops weakening and contraction — turning proofs into resource-sensitive computations.
  1. Reverse Mathematics: Works in the opposite direction — given a theorem, what axioms are necessary to prove it? Most classical analysis falls into five subsystems ($\mathsf{RCA}_0$ through $\Pi^1_1\text{-CA}_0$), revealing a tight calibration between mathematical content and foundational strength.
  1. Proof Mining (Unwinding): Kohlenbach's program extracts quantitative information (bounds, rates of convergence) from non-constructive proofs. A proof of $\forall x\, \exists y\, P(x,y)$ can often be mined for a computable witness $f$ such that $P(x, f(x))$.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) and the univalence axiom — extending proof-theoretic foundations into higher-dimensional type theory where proofs of equality are paths.
  • Proof complexity lower bounds: Proving that tautologies require super-polynomial proofs in Extended Frege or stronger systems remains open and deeply tied to circuit complexity.
  • Cyclic proof systems: Proofs with back-edges (cycles) for inductive/coinductive reasoning — soundness is enforced by global trace conditions rather than structural induction.
  • Probabilistic and quantum proof systems: Interactive proof complexity (IP = PSPACE, MIP* = RE) blurs the boundary between proof theory and computational complexity in striking ways.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Cut-elimination guarantees that any provable sequent has a direct proof — but cut-free proofs can be non-elementarily longer. What does this explosion in proof length say about the relationship between logical power and computational tractability?
  1. Ordinal analysis assigns $\varepsilon_0$ to PA and much larger ordinals to impredicative systems. Is there a sense in which these ordinals explain the consistency of the theory, or are they merely a calibration tool that relocates rather than resolves foundational questions?
  1. The Curry-Howard correspondence makes every proof a program. Does proof normalization (cut-elimination, $\beta$-reduction) constitute a form of computation, and if so, what is being computed — the proof's content, its meaning, or something else?
