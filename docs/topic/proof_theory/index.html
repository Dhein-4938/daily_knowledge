Proof Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Proof theory treats proofs as first-class mathematical objects — syntactic entities that can be studied, classified, and transformed using mathematical techniques. Rather than asking "is this statement true?" (the model-theoretic question), proof theory asks "what is the structure of the derivation that establishes it?"

INFO: Formal Definition
A proof in a formal system $\mathcal{S}$ is a finite, inductively-defined data structure (tree, sequence, or DAG) whose leaves are axioms of $\mathcal{S}$ and whose internal nodes correspond to applications of inference rules. A proof of $\varphi$ is a witness that $\mathcal{S} \vdash \varphi$.

The central syntactic/semantic duality:

$$\underbrace{\mathcal{S} \vdash \varphi}_{\text{proof theory (syntax)}} \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad \underbrace{\mathcal{M} \models \varphi}_{\text{model theory (semantics)}}$$

Soundness and completeness theorems (e.g., Gödel's completeness theorem for FOL) are precisely what connects these two columns.

Why It Matters

Proof theory sits at the intersection of foundations, computation, and meaning:

  • Foundations of mathematics: Hilbert's program sought to secure all of mathematics via finitary proofs. Gödel's incompleteness theorems refuted the program's maximalist goals but sharpened the questions enormously.
  • Curry–Howard correspondence: Proofs in intuitionistic logic are identical to programs in typed lambda calculi — a fact with sweeping consequences for programming language theory, dependent types, and verified software.
  • Proof assistants: Coq, Lean, Agda, and Isabelle are direct applications; the type-theoretic foundations they rely on are proof-theoretic in nature.
  • Ordinal analysis: Assigns a "proof-theoretic ordinal" $|T|$ to a theory $T$, measuring its consistency strength. E.g., $|\mathsf{PA}| = \varepsilon_0$.
TIP: The Curry–Howard Iceberg
The surface reading is "proofs ↔ programs, propositions ↔ types." The deeper consequence is that proof normalization = program execution. Cut-elimination in sequent calculus corresponds to $\beta$-reduction in the $\lambda$-calculus. This makes proof theory a theory of computation, not merely of deduction.

Key Details

  1. Sequent calculus & natural deduction are the two dominant proof systems. Gentzen's Hauptsatz (cut-elimination theorem, 1935) is the field's cornerstone result: every proof using the "cut" rule (modus ponens generalized) can be transformed into a cut-free proof, establishing consistency of first-order arithmetic by transfinite induction up to $\varepsilon_0$.
  1. Ordinal analysis calibrates theories by the least ordinal whose well-foundedness cannot be proved inside the theory. The ladder runs: $\mathsf{PRA} < \mathsf{PA}$ (at $\varepsilon_0$) $< \mathsf{ATR}_0$ (at $\Gamma_0$, the Feferman–Schütte ordinal) $< \Pi^1_1\text{-}\mathsf{CA}_0 \cdots$
  1. Reverse mathematics inverts the usual direction: given a theorem, find the weakest axiomatic system that proves it. Most theorems of classical analysis fall into one of five canonical subsystems ($\mathsf{RCA}_0, \mathsf{WKL}_0, \mathsf{ACA}_0, \mathsf{ATR}_0, \Pi^1_1\text{-}\mathsf{CA}_0$).
  1. Proof complexity asks about the size of shortest proofs. The $\mathsf{P}$ vs. $\mathsf{NP}$ problem is equivalent to asking whether tautologies have polynomial-size proofs in some propositional proof system (Cook–Reckhow theorem). Extended Frege systems remain a major open battleground.
  1. Proof mining (Kohlenbach) extracts computational content — explicit witnesses and bounds — from non-constructive proofs, yielding quantitative results in analysis and ergodic theory without reproving results from scratch.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) and the Univalent Foundations program (Voevodsky), which enriches proof theory with homotopy-theoretic structure; proofs of equality become paths in a space.
  • Deep inference (Guglielmi's calculus of structures) allows inference rules to apply inside any formula context, breaking the asymmetry of sequent calculus and enabling more fine-grained proof-complexity analyses.
  • Automated theorem proving via SAT/SMT solvers and interactive proof assistants, with large formal libraries (Mathlib in Lean 4) pushing the boundary of what machine-checked mathematics covers.
  • Proof-theoretic semantics (Prawitz, Dummett) grounds meaning in proof conditions rather than truth conditions — a philosophical alternative to Tarskian model theory with implications for anti-realist semantics.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Cut-elimination guarantees consistency but requires transfinite induction beyond the theory's own reach — does this circularity undermine Hilbert's program, or just redirect it toward ordinal analysis?
  2. If proofs are programs (Curry–Howard), what is the computational interpretation of a proof by contradiction or the law of excluded middle? What does this imply for classical vs. constructive mathematics?
  3. Proof complexity lower bounds (e.g., for the pigeonhole principle in Resolution) are some of the few unconditional complexity separations we have — why hasn't this approach yet cracked $\mathsf{P} \neq \mathsf{NP}$?
