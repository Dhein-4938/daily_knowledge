Epigenetics✓ reviewed

Science · Genomics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Epigenetics is the study of heritable changes in gene expression that occur without alteration of the underlying DNA nucleotide sequence. The "epi-" prefix (Greek: ἐπι-, "over/upon") signals a layer of regulatory information sitting above the genome itself.

Formally, the epigenome encodes a mapping:

$$\mathcal{E}: \text{Genome} \times \text{Environment} \times \text{Developmental State} \rightarrow \text{Gene Expression Profile}$$

where the same DNA sequence $\mathcal{G}$ can produce radically different phenotypic outputs $\phi$ depending on the epigenetic state $\epsilon$. Two cells in your body share an identical genome yet differ profoundly — that delta is epigenetic.

Why It Matters

Epigenetics bridges the longstanding gap between genotype and phenotype, and between nature and nurture. Key domains of impact:

  • Development & Differentiation — A fertilized zygote's totipotent genome must selectively silence and activate gene programs to produce ~200 distinct human cell types. Epigenetic marks orchestrate this without rewriting DNA.
  • Disease — Aberrant methylation patterns are hallmarks of cancer. Tumor suppressor genes are frequently silenced via hypermethylation rather than mutation.
  • Transgenerational Inheritance — Environmental stressors (famine, toxins) can imprint epigenetic marks that persist across generations, challenging strict Mendelian models.
  • Pharmacology — HDAC inhibitors and DNMT inhibitors are approved cancer therapies, demonstrating that the epigenome is a druggable target.
TIP: Connection to Information Theory
The epigenome functions as a context-sensitive compression scheme — the same symbolic genome is "decompressed" differently in different cellular contexts. This has inspired computational models of cell identity using epigenomic state vectors analogous to embeddings in ML.

Key Details

1. DNA Methylation

The addition of a methyl group (–CH₃) to cytosine residues, predominantly at CpG dinucleotides, typically represses transcription. Catalyzed by DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs). Notably stable — persists through replication via maintenance methylase DNMT1 recognizing hemi-methylated DNA.

$$5\text{-cytosine} + \text{SAM} \xrightarrow{\text{DNMT}} 5\text{-methylcytosine} + \text{SAH}$$

2. Histone Modification

Histones — the protein octamers around which DNA wraps — carry extensible N-terminal tails subject to post-translational modifications: acetylation (loosens chromatin → active), methylation (context-dependent), phosphorylation, ubiquitylation. The combinatorial logic of these marks is called the histone code hypothesis.

3. Chromatin Remodeling

ATP-dependent complexes (SWI/SNF, ISWI families) physically reposition nucleosomes, exposing or occluding transcription factor binding sites independent of covalent modification.

4. Non-Coding RNA Regulation

Small RNAs (miRNA, siRNA, piRNA) and long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) modulate expression post-transcriptionally and can also recruit chromatin-modifying complexes to specific loci — a sequence-directed form of epigenetic regulation.

5. Genomic Imprinting

A subset of genes (~80 in humans) are expressed monoallelically in a parent-of-origin-specific manner. The silenced allele is marked epigenetically during gametogenesis. Disruptions cause Prader-Willi and Angelman syndromes — same genomic region, different phenotype depending on which parental allele is affected.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research frontiers as of mid-2026:

  • Single-cell epigenomics (scATAC-seq, CUT&RUN at single-cell resolution) is revealing stochastic heterogeneity in chromatin states within nominally identical populations.
  • Phase separation — transcriptional condensates formed by intrinsically disordered regions of transcription factors and Mediator complexes may compartmentalize epigenetic machinery; the mechanistic details remain contested.
  • Epigenetic clocks (Horvath, PhenoAge, DunedinPACE) use CpG methylation patterns to estimate biological age with surprising precision, spawning an entire longevity-interventions research track.
  • CRISPR-based epigenome editing (dCas9 fused to DNMT3A, TET1, p300, KRAB) enables locus-specific epigenetic perturbation without sequence editing — a powerful dissection tool and emerging therapeutic modality.
  • Transgenerational inheritance mechanisms remain mechanistically unclear in mammals; whether environmentally-induced marks survive the near-complete epigenetic reprogramming of early embryogenesis is an open and contentious question.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Inheritance paradox — Mammalian embryos undergo near-complete erasure of parental epigenetic marks post-fertilization. If this is true, what mechanisms could plausibly explain robust transgenerational epigenetic inheritance of environmentally-induced phenotypes in mammals vs. C. elegans?
  1. Causality direction — Epigenetic state correlates with disease, but correlation isn't causation. How would you design experiments to distinguish epigenetic changes that drive pathology from those that are merely downstream consequences?
  1. Computational analogy — If the genome is hardware and the epigenome is firmware, what are the limits of this analogy? In what ways does the epigenome fail to behave like a deterministic configuration layer, and what does that imply for epigenome editing as therapy?
