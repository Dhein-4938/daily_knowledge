Core Concept

INFO: Boolean Satisfiability (SAT)
Given a Boolean formula $\varphi$ over variables $x_1, \dots, x_n$, determine whether there exists an assignment $\sigma: \{x_i\} \to \{\top, \bot\}$ such that $\varphi(\sigma) = \top$. If so, $\varphi$ is satisfiable; otherwise unsatisfiable.

In practice, formulas are presented in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF): a conjunction of clauses, each a disjunction of literals (a variable or its negation):

$$\varphi = \bigwedge_{i=1}^{m} \left( \bigvee_{j=1}^{k_i} l_{ij} \right)$$

The SAT problem is the canonical NP-complete problem — established by the Cook–Levin theorem (1971). Every problem in NP reduces to SAT in polynomial time, making it a universal target for combinatorial problem encoding.

Why It Matters

SAT occupies a peculiar position: theoretically intractable in the worst case, yet practically dominant in real-world verification tasks. Its importance spans several domains:

  • Hardware/software verification: Model checkers (e.g., CBMC, IC3/PDR) encode system reachability as SAT/SMT queries
  • AI planning: Classical planning (STRIPS) encodes plan-existence as SAT
  • Cryptanalysis: Algebraic attacks encode cipher inversion as SAT instances
  • Compiler optimization: Instruction scheduling, register allocation via pseudo-Boolean SAT
  • Theorem proving: SAT backends underpin tools like Lean's decide tactic for decidable propositions
TIP: SAT as a lingua franca
Because NP-completeness guarantees polynomial reductions from all NP problems to SAT, a fast SAT solver is implicitly a fast solver for hundreds of problems simultaneously. This universality explains the intense engineering investment in solver performance.

Key Details

1. DPLL and CDCL

Modern solvers descend from the Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland (DPLL) backtracking procedure (1962). The decisive leap came with Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL) in the 1990s (GRASP, Chaff): when a contradiction is reached, the solver analyzes the implication graph to derive a learned clause that prunes the search space globally. This transforms worst-case exponential search into something empirically tractable on structured instances.

2. Unit Propagation and BCP

Boolean Constraint Propagation (BCP) — aka unit propagation — is the inner loop of every CDCL solver. When a clause has exactly one unassigned literal, that literal is forced. Speed of BCP (via the two-watched-literal scheme) dominates solver runtime.

3. VSIDS Heuristic

Variable State Independent Decaying Sum (VSIDS) scores variables by recent conflict involvement, decaying older scores. This bias toward "hot" variables exploits locality in structured industrial instances and is why modern solvers scale where pure random backtracking fails.

4. Phase Saving

Rather than defaulting to $\bot$ on a variable, solvers remember the last assigned polarity. This phase saving heuristic dramatically reduces thrashing on problems with large satisfying regions.

5. #SAT and Beyond

The counting variant, #SAT (count satisfying assignments), is #P-complete — strictly harder than NP under standard assumptions. This matters for probabilistic inference in graphical models and is not amenable to the same CDCL tricks.

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Solver performance figures from the Wikipedia extract (2007-era, "tens of thousands of variables") are long obsolete. Competition-grade solvers (CaDiCaL, Kissat, Glucose) routinely handle millions of variables and billions of clauses in industrial instances as of the mid-2020s.

Current State

The annual SAT Competition continues to drive solver engineering. Active research threads include:

  • Machine learning for solver configuration: GNN-based branching heuristics (NeuroSAT, G2SAT) learn structure from training distributions, outperforming VSIDS on some crafted instances
  • Parallel and distributed SAT: Portfolio solvers (e.g., Mallob) partition clause-sharing across cores; cube-and-conquer splits the search space combinatorially
  • MaxSAT and UNSAT cores: Optimization variants with strong ties to pseudo-Boolean optimization and weighted partial MaxSAT for industrial scheduling
  • SAT modulo theories (SMT): Integrating SAT with theory solvers (linear arithmetic, arrays, bit-vectors) via DPLL(T) — the architecture underlying Z3, CVC5

Discussion Prompts

  1. Practical tractability vs. theoretical hardness: CDCL solvers crush industrial instances that are structurally "easy" yet still NP-complete. What structural properties (community structure, backbone size, clause density near the phase transition) explain this gap — and can they be exploited a priori?
  1. The P vs. NP interface: SAT is NP-complete, but learned clause databases can grow exponentially. In what sense does a CDCL solver implicitly "prove" unsatisfiability, and how does resolution proof complexity relate to solver runtime in practice?
  1. Neural SAT solvers: Graph neural networks trained on random SAT instances generalize poorly to structured industrial instances. Is this a data distribution problem, an architectural limitation, or evidence that the two families genuinely require different algorithmic strategies?
