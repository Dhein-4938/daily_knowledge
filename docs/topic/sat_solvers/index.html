SAT Solvers✓ reviewed

Logic · Automated Reasoning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

A SAT solver is an algorithm that decides whether a Boolean formula — typically in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) — has a satisfying variable assignment. Formally:

$$\text{Given } \varphi(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \text{CNF}, \text{ find } \mathbf{v} \in \{0,1\}^n \text{ s.t. } \varphi(\mathbf{v}) = \top, \text{ or report UNSAT}$$

A CNF formula is a conjunction of clauses, each clause being a disjunction of literals (variables or their negations). The hardness of SAT is governed by clause-to-variable ratio; near the phase transition (~4.267 for 3-SAT), instances become exponentially harder.

[!info] Cook–Levin Theorem

SAT was the first problem proven NP-complete (Cook 1971, Levin 1973). Every problem in NP reduces to SAT in polynomial time, making it the canonical hard problem. Solving SAT in deterministic polynomial time would prove P = NP.

Why It Matters

SAT solvers are a cornerstone of modern computer science despite the NP-completeness barrier, because practical instances are often far easier than worst-case:

  • Hardware verification: Equivalence checking and model checking for circuits (bounded model checking reduces directly to SAT)
  • Software analysis: Symbolic execution, program synthesis, bug finding
  • AI planning & scheduling: STRIPS-style planning encodes as SAT
  • Cryptanalysis: Algebraic attacks on stream/block ciphers
  • Combinatorics: Resolving open conjectures (e.g., Schur number five proven via SAT in 2016)

[!tip] SAT solvers are often the hidden engine behind higher-level tools — SMT solvers (Z3, CVC5) reduce to SAT for the Boolean skeleton, while model checkers like IC3/PDR use incremental SAT calls internally.

Key Details

1. DPLL and CDCL

The dominant paradigm is Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL), extending the Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland (DPLL) backtracking algorithm. When a conflict is reached, the solver performs resolution on the implication graph to derive a learned clause, then backtracks non-chronologically. This transforms exponential search into targeted pruning.

2. Unit Propagation and BCP

Boolean Constraint Propagation (BCP) — the forced assignment of unit clauses — is the inner loop and consumes ~80% of runtime. The two-watched-literal scheme makes BCP nearly O(1) amortized per propagation.

3. Variable & Restart Heuristics

  • VSIDS (Variable State Independent Decaying Sum): prioritizes variables involved in recent conflicts — captures a form of learned relevance
  • Luby restarts: periodic restart schedule provably near-optimal for certain random distributions
  • Phase saving: remembers last polarity for each variable, exploiting structure in industrial instances

4. Preprocessing

Modern solvers (CaDiCaL, Kissat) apply aggressive preprocessing: variable elimination via resolution, subsumption, blocked clause elimination. These can reduce formula size by orders of magnitude before search begins.

5. Proof Logging

Industrial-strength solvers now emit DRAT proofs (Delete Resolution Asymmetric Tautology), enabling independent verification of UNSAT certificates — critical for formal hardware proofs.

Current State

[!warning] Note: this information may be dated

The frontier is neuro-symbolic hybridization. Recent work (G-RRM, 2026) demonstrates that recurrent reasoning models can guide CDCL solvers by predicting branching decisions. The key finding: neural guidance yields substantial speedups (up to 33×) only when (1) the combinatorial search space is large, and (2) the solver can override incorrect neural hints. Critically, CaDiCaL 3.0.0 showed no significant speedup because it always commits to injected hints without recovery — underscoring that graceful degradation under imperfect advice is architecturally non-trivial.

Parallel to this, MaxSAT and #SAT (counting satisfying assignments) solvers are seeing active development for probabilistic inference and optimization workloads. The annual SAT Competition continues to drive incremental improvements in heuristics and preprocessing.

Discussion Prompts

  1. CDCL's learned clauses can be seen as a form of compression of the search history — in what sense does a good learned clause encode more information than a bad one, and how does this relate to proof complexity?
  1. The G-RRM results suggest that neural guidance is most valuable in large search spaces but requires the solver to be able to ignore bad hints. Does this point toward a general principle for safe neuro-symbolic integration beyond SAT?
  1. SAT solvers routinely solve "NP-hard" industrial instances with millions of variables — yet worst-case instances with ~hundreds of variables can defeat them. What does this gap tell us about the structure of real-world problem distributions, and is NP-completeness the right lens for practical solver design?
