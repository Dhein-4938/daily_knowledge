Quantum Computing✓ reviewed

Tech · Quantum Computing  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

A quantum computer exploits two phenomena absent in classical systems — superposition and entanglement — to process information in fundamentally different ways.

INFO: Qubit
The basic unit is the qubit, described by a state vector in a 2D complex Hilbert space: $$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle, \quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$$ Measurement collapses $|\psi\rangle$ to $|0\rangle$ with probability $|\alpha|^2$ or $|1\rangle$ with probability $|\beta|^2$ — the Born rule.

An $n$-qubit register lives in a $2^n$-dimensional Hilbert space, enabling a single unitary operation to act on all $2^n$ basis states simultaneously. This isn't free parallelism — measurement still yields only $n$ classical bits — but cleverly designed algorithms use wave interference to amplify the amplitude of correct answers and suppress wrong ones.

Why It Matters

The exponential Hilbert space scaling has concrete consequences:

  • Cryptography: Shor's algorithm factors integers in $O((\log N)^3)$ time, breaking RSA and ECC. Classical best-known algorithms run in $O(e^{(\log N)^{1/3}})$ — sub-exponentially slower.
  • Search: Grover's algorithm searches an unstructured database of $N$ items in $O(\sqrt{N})$ queries vs. classical $O(N)$.
  • Simulation: Feynman's original motivation — simulating quantum systems (chemistry, materials) — scales polynomially on a quantum computer but exponentially on classical hardware.
TIP: Not universally faster
Quantum speedup is problem-specific. For most everyday computation — sorting, databases, parsing — no known quantum advantage exists. The power lies in exploiting the structure of problems with algebraic or periodic symmetry.

Key Details

  1. Decoherence is the central engineering obstacle. Real qubits interact with their environment, collapsing superpositions prematurely. Coherence time $T_2$ must significantly exceed gate operation time. Current superconducting qubits achieve $T_2 \sim 100\,\mu s$; useful fault-tolerant computation likely requires $T_2 \sim$ seconds.
  1. Fault-tolerant thresholds. The threshold theorem states that if physical error rate $p < p_{th}$ (roughly $10^{-2}$ for surface codes), arbitrarily long computation is possible via quantum error correction — at the cost of $\sim 1000\times$ physical-to-logical qubit overhead.
  1. Gate model vs. other paradigms. The circuit/gate model (sequences of unitary gates) is the dominant abstraction, but alternatives include adiabatic quantum computing, measurement-based (cluster state) computing, and topological qubits (Majorana fermions), each with distinct noise profiles.
  1. Physical implementations differ radically in trade-offs:
  • Superconducting qubits (IBM, Google): fast gates ($\sim 10\text{–}100\,ns$), but requires dilution refrigeration at $\sim 15\,mK$.
  • Ion traps: very low error rates, slow gates ($\sim 1\,ms$), room-temperature isolation possible.
  • Si/SiGe quantum dots: CMOS-compatible, relevant recent first-principles band alignment work improving heterostructure design for spin qubits.
  1. Quantum advantage ≠ quantum supremacy for useful tasks. Claims of supremacy (Google 2019, others) involve sampling tasks with no practical utility — important as proof of principle, but not yet economically relevant.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts (as of early 2026):

  • Error correction at scale: Moving from NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) devices toward fault-tolerant logical qubits. Google's surface code experiments have demonstrated below-threshold error rates in small patches.
  • Qubit connectivity and compilation: Sparse qubit graphs require SWAP overhead; co-design of algorithms and hardware topology is an open problem.
  • Post-quantum cryptography (PQC): NIST has standardized lattice-based schemes (CRYSTALS-Kyber, Dilithium) as classical defenses against Shor-capable hardware.
  • Si/SiGe heterostructure engineering: First-principles DFT band offset predictions across full alloy composition range are enabling better quantum dot design.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Fault tolerance overhead: The ~1000:1 physical-to-logical qubit ratio means a "1000 logical qubit" machine needs ~1M physical qubits. Does this fundamentally change your intuition about when quantum computers become practically relevant?
  1. Computational complexity: Quantum computers are believed to solve BQP problems efficiently — but BQP is not believed to contain NP-complete problems. What does this say about the limits of quantum speedup, and why does the public discourse so often conflate the two?
  1. Hybrid algorithms: Near-term algorithms like VQE (Variational Quantum Eigensolver) offload classical optimization onto quantum hardware iteratively. Is this a genuine path to useful advantage, or a workaround masking the absence of fault tolerance?
