Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems✓ reviewed

Math · Logic  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO
Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem: Any consistent formal system $F$ capable of expressing basic arithmetic contains a sentence $G_F$ (the "Gödel sentence") such that neither $G_F$ nor $\neg G_F$ is provable in $F$. Gödel's Second Incompleteness Theorem: No such system $F$ can prove its own consistency — formally, $F \nvdash \text{Con}(F)$.

The proof hinges on arithmetization of syntax: Gödel assigned a unique natural number (a Gödel number) to every symbol, formula, and proof sequence, enabling the system to reason about its own provability predicate $\text{Prov}_F(\ulcorner \phi \urcorner)$ using ordinary arithmetic. A diagonal argument then constructs $G_F$ — a sentence that, informally, asserts "I am not provable in F". If $F$ proves $G_F$, it's inconsistent; if it proves $\neg G_F$, it's also inconsistent (since $G_F$ is actually true in the standard model). So a consistent $F$ can prove neither.

The theorems apply to any system satisfying:

  • Expressiveness: encodes Peano Arithmetic (or any theory that interprets $\mathsf{PA}$)
  • Consistency: no proof of $\bot$
  • Recursive axiomatization: the set of axioms is decidable (enumerable by an algorithm)

$$F \text{ consistent} + F \supseteq \mathsf{PA} \implies \exists G_F : F \nvdash G_F \land F \nvdash \neg G_F$$

Why It Matters

Gödel's results demolished Hilbert's Program (1920s), which sought a finite, complete, and consistent axiomatization for all of mathematics. Completeness and consistency cannot coexist once arithmetic is in scope.

The theorems thread into multiple disciplines:

  • Philosophy of Mathematics: Fuels debates between Platonists (true but unprovable statements exist "out there") and formalists (truth is internal to a system).
  • Computability Theory: Church's proof of the undecidability of the Entscheidungsproblem and Turing's halting problem are structurally parallel — each uses a self-referential diagonal. The incompleteness theorems and the halting problem are inter-reducible.
  • Computer Science: No sufficiently powerful proof assistant can be both sound and complete. Formal verification systems (Coq, Lean) must either accept unprovable truths or work in deliberately limited fragments.
  • Complexity Theory: Connections to independence results in set theory (e.g., $\mathsf{P} \neq \mathsf{NP}$ may be independent of $\mathsf{ZFC}$).
TIP
The second theorem is often more practically limiting: it means you cannot bootstrap trust in a system from within itself. This is why security proofs and type-system soundness proofs always live in a meta-theory stronger than the object theory.

Key Details

  1. ω-consistency vs. Consistency: Gödel's original first theorem required the stronger condition of ω-consistency (the system doesn't prove $\exists x\,\neg\phi(x)$ while also proving $\phi(0), \phi(1), \phi(2), \ldots$). Rosser (1936) strengthened the result to require only plain consistency, via the Rosser trick.
  1. The Gödel Sentence Is True (in $\mathbb{N}$): $G_F$ asserts its own unprovability, and since $F$ is consistent, it genuinely is unprovable — so $G_F$ is a true arithmetic statement. This is not circular: "truth" here means truth in the standard model $\mathbb{N}$, which is external to $F$.
  1. Scope of Application: The theorems don't apply to all formal systems. Presburger arithmetic (addition only, no multiplication) is consistent, complete, and decidable — multiplication is what gives arithmetic enough expressive power for Gödel encoding.
  1. Con(F) is Provable in Stronger Systems: $\mathsf{PA}$ cannot prove $\text{Con}(\mathsf{PA})$, but $\mathsf{ZFC}$ can (assuming $\mathsf{ZFC}$ is itself consistent). This creates an infinite regress of meta-theories, each unable to vouch for itself.
  1. Independence vs. Unprovability: Incompleteness is not the same as falsity. The Gödel sentence and statements like the Continuum Hypothesis (independent of $\mathsf{ZFC}$) are neither provable nor disprovable — they can be added as new axioms in either direction without introducing contradiction.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research extends incompleteness into several directions:

  • Proof-theoretic ordinals: Measuring the strength of theories via ordinal analysis (e.g., $\varepsilon_0$ for $\mathsf{PA}$) provides a fine-grained hierarchy beyond what Gödel sentences capture.
  • Reverse mathematics: Calibrates which axioms are needed for specific theorems, revealing that many classical results sit precisely at the boundary of $\mathsf{PA}$ or subsystems of second-order arithmetic.
  • Incompleteness in set theory: The independence of large cardinal axioms, the Continuum Hypothesis, and determinacy axioms from $\mathsf{ZFC}$ is an ongoing area — with Hugh Woodin's $\Omega$-logic and ultimate-$L$ program attempting to resolve set-theoretic indeterminacy.
  • Algorithmic randomness: Chaitin's incompleteness theorem reframes the first theorem in terms of Kolmogorov complexity: for any consistent theory $F$, there is a constant $L$ such that $F$ cannot prove that any specific string has complexity $> L$.

P vs NP Problem✓ reviewed

Math · Complexity Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Formal Definition
P is the class of decision problems solvable by a deterministic Turing machine in time $O(n^k)$ for some constant $k$, where $n$ is input size. NP is the class of decision problems where a proposed solution (a certificate) can be verified in polynomial time by a deterministic Turing machine.

The central question: does $\text{P} = \text{NP}$?

Equivalently, if a nondeterministic Turing machine can solve a problem in polynomial time (by "guessing" a certificate and verifying it), can a deterministic machine do the same? The asymmetry between finding and checking is the crux.

Formally, a language $L \in \text{NP}$ iff there exists a polynomial $p$ and a polynomial-time verifier $V$ such that:

$$x \in L \iff \exists\, c \in \{0,1\}^{p(|x|)} : V(x, c) = 1$$

The conjecture $\text{P} \neq \text{NP}$ is nearly universal among complexity theorists, but no proof exists in either direction.

Why It Matters

The stakes extend far beyond theoretical elegance:

  • Cryptography: Almost all public-key cryptography (RSA, elliptic curves) relies implicitly on $\text{P} \neq \text{NP}$ — if $\text{P} = \text{NP}$, integer factorization and discrete logarithm become tractable, collapsing current security infrastructure.
  • Optimization: Thousands of NP-hard combinatorial problems (TSP, SAT, graph coloring) would become efficiently solvable, revolutionizing logistics, circuit design, and drug discovery.
  • Mathematics itself: Proof verification is in NP. If $\text{P} = \text{NP}$, finding proofs of theorems would be no harder than checking them — a seismic epistemological shift.
  • AI and search: Heuristic methods dominate because we assume exact search is intractable. A $\text{P} = \text{NP}$ result would invalidate that assumption.

It is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems ($1,000,000 USD prize each), underscoring its standing as the central open question in computer science.

Key Details

  1. NP-completeness as the hard core. A problem is NP-complete if it is in NP and every NP problem reduces to it in polynomial time (Cook-Levin, 1971). SAT was the first; now thousands of NP-complete problems are known. Solving any one efficiently solves all of NP.
  1. The reduction hierarchy. Reductions are the connective tissue: $A \leq_p B$ means $A$ is polynomial-time reducible to $B$. NP-completeness is defined via many-one (Karp) reductions; stronger notions (Turing reductions) define NP-hardness without NP-membership.
  1. Relativization barrier. Baker, Gill, and Solovay (1975) showed oracles $A, B$ exist such that $\text{P}^A = \text{NP}^A$ and $\text{P}^B \neq \text{NP}^B$. Any proof technique that relativizes (works regardless of oracle) cannot resolve P vs NP — ruling out large classes of classical arguments.
  1. Natural proofs barrier (Razborov-Rudich, 1994). A wide class of circuit lower-bound proof strategies would, if successful, also yield efficient pseudorandom generator breaks — something most cryptographers believe impossible. This indirectly obstructs lower-bound approaches to P ≠ NP.
  1. Algebrization barrier. Aaronson-Wigderson (2009) extended relativization to algebraic settings, showing that even algebraic techniques are insufficient. Three known barriers (relativization, natural proofs, algebrization) collectively rule out the most natural proof strategies.
TIP: The Gap Between Barriers and Impossibility
The barriers show current techniques fail — they do not prove the problem is unprovable. New structural insights (e.g., from proof complexity or geometric complexity theory) may circumvent all three.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research frontiers include Geometric Complexity Theory (Mulmuley & Sohoni), which encodes complexity separations as representation-theoretic problems in algebraic geometry — a radically different approach that sidesteps known barriers. Proof complexity (studying the length of formal proofs) offers another avenue. No credible proof attempts have withstood scrutiny as of early 2026.

Information Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Information Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Information theory is the mathematical framework for quantifying, storing, and transmitting information. Founded by Claude Shannon in 1948 ("A Mathematical Theory of Communication"), it answers a deceptively simple question: how much information is contained in a message?

INFO: Shannon Entropy
For a discrete random variable $X$ with probability mass function $p(x)$, the Shannon entropy is: $$H(X) = -\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) \log_2 p(x) \quad \text{(bits)}$$ Entropy measures the average uncertainty before observing $X$ — equivalently, the minimum average bits needed to encode its outcomes.

For a fair coin, $H = -2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\log_2\frac{1}{2} = 1$ bit. A biased coin with $P(\text{H})=0.99$ has $H \approx 0.08$ bits — you already know what's coming. Entropy is maximized by the uniform distribution.

Why It Matters

Shannon's framework is foundational because it separates logical structure from physical substrate. A bit is a bit whether stored in magnetic domains, optical pits, or synaptic weights. This abstraction enables:

  • Data compression: ZIP, MP3, JPEG all exploit source coding theorems — you cannot compress below the entropy rate of the source
  • Error-correcting codes: Reed-Solomon codes (CDs, Voyager transmissions), LDPC codes (DSL, 5G) approach the channel capacity $C = \max_{p(x)} I(X;Y)$
  • Cryptography: Shannon's notion of perfect secrecy formalizes what one-time pads achieve
  • Machine learning: Cross-entropy loss, KL divergence, mutual information — all direct descendants
  • Neuroscience: Neural codes are analyzed as noisy channels; efficient coding hypotheses explain retinal ganglion cell behavior
TIP: The Dual Role of Entropy
Shannon entropy is formally identical to Boltzmann's thermodynamic entropy $S = -k_B \sum p_i \ln p_i$. This isn't coincidence — both measure missing information about a system's microstate. Landauer's principle makes this concrete: erasing one bit dissipates at least $k_B T \ln 2$ joules of heat.

Key Details

1. Mutual Information

$$I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X \mid Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)}$$

This is the reduction in uncertainty about $X$ after observing $Y$. It's symmetric, non-negative, and equals zero iff $X \perp Y$.

2. Channel Capacity (Noisy-Channel Coding Theorem)

For any channel with capacity $C$ and transmission rate $R < C$, there exist codes achieving arbitrarily low error probability. For $R > C$, reliable communication is impossible. This is Shannon's master theorem — existence without construction.

3. Source Coding Theorem

A source with entropy $H$ bits/symbol can be compressed to $H$ bits/symbol on average but no further. Huffman coding achieves this optimally for symbol-by-symbol codes; arithmetic coding approaches it for sequences.

4. KL Divergence (Relative Entropy)

$$D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \geq 0$$

Measures the information cost of modeling distribution $P$ with $Q$. Not a metric (asymmetric). Central to variational inference, EM algorithm, and hypothesis testing.

5. Rate-Distortion Theory

Extends lossiness: given tolerance for distortion $D$, what is the minimum bitrate $R(D)$? JPEG operates implicitly on this tradeoff. Lossy compression is only well-posed through this lens.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts as of mid-2026:

  • Information theory for deep learning: explaining generalization via mutual information bounds, the information bottleneck principle (Tishby et al.), and debates about its empirical validity
  • Quantum information theory: von Neumann entropy $S(\rho) = -\text{tr}(\rho \log \rho)$, quantum channel capacity, entanglement as a resource — the field remains in rapid development
  • Neural representations: recent work (e.g., Jiang et al. 2026) shows that LLMs encode scalar "value" estimates linearly in activation space — an information-geometric question about how high-dimensional belief states compress into low-dimensional features
  • Causal information theory: integrating directed information and transfer entropy with causal inference frameworks (Pearl/Judea Pearl tradition)
TIP: LLM Connection
The cross-entropy loss used to train language models minimizes $H(p_{\text{true}}, p_{\theta})$, pushing model distribution $p_\theta$ toward the data distribution. Perplexity is literally $2^{H}$ — the effective branching factor of prediction. Information theory is not just an analogy for ML; it is the loss function.

Linear Algebra✓ reviewed

Math · Algebra  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Linear algebra is the study of vector spaces, linear maps between them, and their coordinate representations via matrices. Its atomic object is the linear equation: $$a_1 x_1 + a_2 x_2 + \cdots + a_n x_n = b$$ A system of such equations is compactly encoded as $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$.

The central abstraction is the linear map $T: V \to W$ satisfying $T(\alpha u + \beta v) = \alpha T(u) + \beta T(v)$. Every finite-dimensional linear map, once bases are chosen, is a matrix — making matrices both the computational engine and a coordinate-dependent representation of a coordinate-free object.

Key structures: vector spaces, subspaces, span, linear independence, basis, dimension, eigenvalues/eigenvectors, inner products, orthogonality, and canonical decompositions (LU, QR, SVD, eigendecomposition).

Why It Matters

Linear algebra is arguably the most load-bearing branch of applied mathematics. Its reach:

  • Geometry: Lines, planes, rotations, reflections, and projections are all linear (or affine) maps. Differential geometry linearizes curved spaces via the tangent space.
  • Differential equations: Solution spaces of linear ODEs/PDEs are vector spaces; spectral methods diagonalize operators.
  • Numerical analysis: Essentially every numerical algorithm reduces to solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ or computing eigenvalues.
  • Machine learning: Neural network layers are affine maps; attention mechanisms are matrix multiplications; PCA, SVD, and kernel methods are pure linear algebra.
  • Nonlinear problems: The Fréchet derivative at a point is the best linear approximation — so even nonlinear analysis locally reduces to linear algebra.
TIP: The Linearization Principle
Almost every local analysis of a smooth map $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ near a point $\mathbf{x}_0$ reduces to studying $Df(\mathbf{x}_0)$, the Jacobian matrix. Linear algebra isn't just useful for linear problems — it's the foundation for handling all smooth problems locally.

Key Details

  1. Rank-nullity theorem: For $T: V \to W$, $\dim(\ker T) + \dim(\operatorname{im} T) = \dim V$. This is the fundamental accounting identity — solutions exist iff $\mathbf{b} \in \operatorname{im}(A)$, and solution sets are affine subspaces of dimension $\dim(\ker A)$.
  1. Spectral theorem: A real symmetric matrix $A = A^\top$ is orthogonally diagonalizable: $A = Q\Lambda Q^\top$. This is the reason symmetric matrices appear everywhere in physics and statistics — their eigendecomposition is numerically stable and geometrically meaningful.
  1. Singular Value Decomposition (SVD): Every $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ factors as $A = U\Sigma V^\top$ with $U, V$ orthogonal and $\Sigma$ diagonal with non-negative entries. SVD reveals the true geometry of any linear map: it maps the right singular vectors to scaled left singular vectors. Numerically, it's the gold standard for rank estimation and pseudoinverse computation.
  1. Conditioning: The condition number $\kappa(A) = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ measures sensitivity of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ to perturbations. A large $\kappa$ signals near-singularity: small changes in $\mathbf{b}$ cause large swings in $\mathbf{x}$. This is the practical bottleneck in scientific computing, not just solvability in principle.
  1. Tensor products & multilinearity: Linear algebra extends naturally to tensors via the tensor product $V \otimes W$. Bilinear and multilinear maps are tamed by universal properties of tensor products — this bridge connects to representation theory, quantum mechanics, and deep learning (where weight tensors are ubiquitous).

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Randomized linear algebra: Randomized SVD and sketching algorithms (e.g., via Johnson-Lindenstrauss) solve high-dimensional problems in sublinear time with provable approximation guarantees. Used in large-scale ML pipelines.
  • Linear inverse problems with learned priors: Recent work (e.g., arXiv 2506, Exact Posterior Score Estimation) derives the exact posterior score in closed form for linear Gaussian inverse problems under diffusion model priors: posterior sampling reduces to denoising at an operator-dependent shifted pivot under anisotropic noise — a striking intersection of linear algebra, Bayesian inference, and generative modeling.
  • Numerical FEM eigenvalue problems: Nitsche-based weak boundary enforcement for elliptic eigenvalue problems (arXiv 2506) uses compact operator theory to derive convergence rates and adaptive a posteriori estimators — a live example of linear algebra driving PDE numerics.
  • Structured matrix algorithms: Exploiting sparsity, hierarchical structure (H-matrices, FFT), and low-rank structure to beat $O(n^3)$ dense complexity remains an open engineering problem at scale.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Coordinate freedom vs. computation: The abstract definition of a linear map is basis-independent, but matrix computation requires a choice of basis. How does the SVD reconcile these — and what does it mean that singular values are basis-invariant but singular vectors are not (up to unitary freedom)?
  1. Why is conditioning the "real" measure of difficulty? The condition number $\kappa(A)$ determines numerical sensitivity, but it's not intrinsic to the mathematical problem — it depends on the norm and scaling. How should one think about ill-conditioning: as a property of the problem, the representation, or the algorithm?
  1. Linearization as epistemology: Almost all of physics and engineering proceeds by linearizing around equilibria. What are the fundamental limits of this strategy — when does the linear approximation mislead rather than inform, and what structure (e.g., Lie brackets, curvature) captures what linearization misses?
