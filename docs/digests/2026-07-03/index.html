Number Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Number Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Number theory is the branch of mathematics concerned with the integers $\mathbb{Z}$, arithmetic functions on them, and structures derived from or generalizing them — including rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$, algebraic integers, and $p$-adic numbers.

The field fractures into several interlocking subdomains:

  • Elementary number theory — divisibility, congruences, the fundamental theorem of arithmetic
  • Algebraic number theory — fields $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$, rings of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$, ideal class groups
  • Analytic number theory — encoding arithmetic in complex-analytic objects; the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^{-s}$ is the canonical example
  • Diophantine geometry — integer/rational solutions to polynomial equations, viewed through algebraic geometry
  • Diophantine approximation — how well irrationals are approximable by rationals; e.g., Dirichlet's theorem guarantees $|x - p/q| < 1/q^2$ has infinitely many solutions for any irrational $x$

The prime numbers are the irreducible atoms. Their distribution is governed by the Prime Number Theorem:

$$\pi(x) \sim \frac{x}{\ln x} \quad \text{as } x \to \infty$$

whose proof depends entirely on the analytic behavior of $\zeta(s)$ in the complex plane.

Why It Matters

TIP: The unreasonable applicability of "pure" mathematics
Gauss called number theory the "queen of mathematics" precisely because it seemed untouchably pure. Then RSA (1977) made prime factorization the security foundation of the modern internet — a direct conversion of pure number theory into infrastructure.

Cryptography is the most consequential application. RSA relies on the apparent asymmetry between multiplying large primes (trivial) and factoring their product (believed hard). Elliptic curve cryptography — used in TLS, Bitcoin, signal protocol — rests on the group structure of $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$, a direct product of algebraic number theory and algebraic geometry.

Beyond cryptography: error-correcting codes (algebraic coding theory), pseudorandom number generation, lattice-based post-quantum cryptography (hardness of SVP/CVP), and the distribution of quantum energy levels (Riemann zeros ↔ GUE eigenvalues).

Key Details

  1. The Riemann Hypothesis — The nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ all lie on the critical line $\text{Re}(s) = \frac{1}{2}$. If true, it gives the sharpest known bounds on $\pi(x)$: $|\pi(x) - \text{Li}(x)| = O(\sqrt{x}\ln x)$. Unproven; one of the Millennium Prize Problems.
  1. Modularity and Langlands — Fermat's Last Theorem ($a^n + b^n = c^n$ has no positive integer solutions for $n > 2$) was proved by Wiles in 1995 via the modularity theorem: every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ is modular. This is a special case of the Langlands program — a vast conjectured dictionary between Galois representations and automorphic forms.
  1. $p$-adic numbers — The completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ under the $p$-adic absolute value $|x|_p = p^{-v_p(x)}$ yields $\mathbb{Q}_p$, an entirely different metric space. By Ostrowski's theorem, $|\cdot|_\infty$ and $\{|\cdot|_p\}$ are the only nontrivial absolute values on $\mathbb{Q}$. The adeles $\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{R} \times \prod_p' \mathbb{Q}_p$ unify all completions simultaneously.
  1. Goldbach's Conjecture — Every even integer $> 2$ is the sum of two primes. Unresolved since 1742. Vinogradov (1937) proved every sufficiently large odd integer is the sum of three primes; Chen (1973) proved every large even integer is $p + P_2$ (prime + semiprime).
  1. Integrability meets number theory — Zeros of $\zeta(s)$ appear to follow the same eigenvalue statistics as random Hermitian matrices (GUE), connecting number theory to quantum chaos — a relationship with no proof yet.

Current State

Active frontiers as of mid-2026 include: progress on the Langlands program (geometric Langlands proof announced 2024 by Fargues–Scholze circle), post-quantum lattice cryptography standardization (NIST PQC finalists), and analytic methods for gaps between primes following Zhang/Maynard.

The arXiv papers in the source material (quantum chaos, PTA gravitational waves, embodied AI) are not directly relevant to number theory — the quantum chaos paper touches on integrability breaking, which echoes the number-theoretic study of $\zeta(s)$ zeros and quantum chaos, but represents a separate physics research thread.

Graph Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Combinatorics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A graph $G = (V, E)$ consists of a set of vertices $V$ and a set of edges $E \subseteq \binom{V}{2}$ (for undirected) or $E \subseteq V \times V$ (for directed). Edges encode pairwise relations between objects.

The fundamental distinction between graph types sets the stage for entirely different theories:

  • Undirected graph: edge $\{u, v\} = \{v, u\}$ — symmetry is baked in
  • Directed graph (digraph): edge $(u, v) \neq (v, u)$ — asymmetry enables modeling flow, causality, hierarchy
  • Weighted graph: each edge carries a scalar $w: E \to \mathbb{R}$, enabling optimization problems over path costs

Key invariants include the degree $\deg(v) = |\{e \in E : v \in e\}|$, with the handshaking lemma guaranteeing:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$

For digraphs, indegree and outdegree split this count, and the adjacency matrix $A$ where $A_{ij} = 1$ iff $(i,j) \in E$ becomes a primary algebraic handle on the structure.

Why It Matters

Graph theory is arguably the most versatile branch of discrete mathematics — it is the natural language of relational structure. Applications span:

  • Computer science: shortest paths (Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford), network flow (max-flow min-cut), scheduling (graph coloring), compiler register allocation
  • Biology: protein interaction networks, phylogenetic trees, connectomes
  • Social science: community detection, influence propagation, small-world phenomena
  • Physics: spin models on lattices, tensor network contraction, Feynman diagrams as graphs
  • Cryptography & complexity: graph isomorphism sits at an unusual complexity-theoretic position — not known to be in P nor NP-complete
TIP: Spectral Bridge
The eigenvalues of the Laplacian $L = D - A$ (where $D$ is the degree matrix) encode global connectivity. The second-smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ (the algebraic connectivity or Fiedler value) measures how well-connected a graph is — $\lambda_2 = 0$ iff the graph is disconnected. This spectral perspective connects graph theory to differential geometry via discrete Hodge theory.

Key Details

  1. Graph isomorphism — Determining whether two graphs $G \cong H$ (same structure up to vertex relabeling) is in complexity class GI, believed strictly between P and NP-complete. The Weisfeiler-Leman algorithm gives practical heuristics and underlies the expressiveness limits of Graph Neural Networks.
  1. Planarity — A graph is planar iff it embeds in $\mathbb{R}^2$ without edge crossings. Kuratowski's theorem: $G$ is planar $\iff$ it contains no subdivision of $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$. Planar graphs satisfy Euler's formula $V - E + F = 2$ and are always 4-colorable (Four Color Theorem, proved 1976 with computer assistance).
  1. Trees — A connected acyclic graph on $n$ vertices has exactly $n-1$ edges. Cayley's formula gives $n^{n-2}$ labeled trees on $n$ vertices — a striking result proved elegantly via Prüfer sequences.
  1. Graph minors — Robertson–Seymour theorem: graphs are well-quasi-ordered under the minor relation, implying every minor-closed family has a finite forbidden-minor characterization. This is one of the deepest results in combinatorics.
  1. Random graphs — The Erdős–Rényi model $G(n,p)$ exhibits sharp phase transitions: at $p = \frac{1}{n}$, a giant connected component suddenly emerges. This threshold behavior connects graph theory to statistical physics.
WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Active research areas like graph neural networks, quantum graph algorithms, and parameterized complexity results evolve rapidly — specific state-of-the-art claims here may be superseded.

Current State

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have renewed theoretical interest in graph isomorphism: the WL hierarchy precisely characterizes which graph properties a GNN can distinguish, spurring work on higher-order GNNs and equivariant architectures. Parameterized complexity of graph problems (treewidth, treedepth decompositions) is an active frontier, as is the study of graph limits (graphons) which give a analytic framework for sequences of dense graphs. Quantum computing promises polynomial speedups for certain graph problems via amplitude amplification and quantum walk algorithms.

Differential Equations✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A differential equation is an equation relating an unknown function $f$ to one or more of its derivatives. Formally, an $n$-th order ODE takes the form: $$F\!\left(x,\, y,\, y',\, y'',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n)}\right) = 0$$ where $y = f(x)$ is the unknown. For PDEs, the unknown $u$ depends on multiple variables and partial derivatives appear instead.

The order of a DE is the highest derivative present; the degree is the power of that highest-order term (when the equation is polynomial in derivatives). Linearity is the most structurally decisive property: a linear DE satisfies superposition — if $y_1$ and $y_2$ are solutions, so is $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$.

The general taxonomy:

| Class | Variables | Example |

|---|---|---|

| ODE | Single independent | $y'' + \omega^2 y = 0$ |

| PDE | Multiple independent | $\nabla^2 u = 0$ |

| SDE | Stochastic forcing | $dX = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW$ |

| DDE | Delayed argument | $y'(t) = f(y(t-\tau))$ |

Why It Matters

DEs are the native language of continuity. Virtually every physical law that involves rates — Newton's second law, Maxwell's equations, the Schrödinger equation, the Black-Scholes model, epidemiological SIR models — is a differential equation. Their significance is structural:

  • Physics: Newton's $F = ma$ is $m\ddot{x} = F(x, \dot{x}, t)$; quantum mechanics is built on the PDE $i\hbar\,\partial_t \psi = \hat{H}\psi$.
  • Engineering: Control systems, heat transfer, fluid dynamics (Navier-Stokes) all reduce to DEs.
  • Economics: Optimal growth (Ramsey model), option pricing (Black-Scholes PDE), and game dynamics (replicator equations).
  • Biology: Population dynamics, neural firing (Hodgkin-Huxley), morphogenesis (reaction-diffusion).
TIP: Insight
The fact that most DEs lack closed-form solutions is not a failure — it forced the development of entire fields: numerical analysis, functional analysis, dynamical systems theory, and ergodic theory. The "unsolvability" is mathematically productive.

Key Details

1. Existence and Uniqueness (Picard-Lindelöf)

For the IVP $y' = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0) = y_0$: if $f$ is continuous and Lipschitz in $y$, a unique local solution exists. Lipschitz failure → possible non-uniqueness (e.g., $y' = y^{1/2}$, $y(0)=0$ has infinitely many solutions).

2. Linear vs. Nonlinear

Linear DEs admit superposition and have well-developed solution theory (variation of parameters, Green's functions, spectral methods). Nonlinear DEs can exhibit chaos, bifurcations, and blow-up in finite time — qualitatively richer and analytically harder.

3. Phase Portraits and Qualitative Theory

Even without explicit solutions, the geometry of the vector field $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ reveals equilibria, limit cycles, stable/unstable manifolds, and bifurcation structure. This is the core of Poincaré's program.

4. Green's Functions

For a linear operator $\mathcal{L}$, the Green's function $G(x, s)$ satisfies $\mathcal{L}G = \delta(x-s)$. The solution to $\mathcal{L}y = f$ is then:

$$y(x) = \int G(x,s)\,f(s)\,ds$$

This encodes the impulse response and connects DEs to integral equations and functional analysis.

5. Numerical Methods

When closed forms fail: Euler, Runge-Kutta (RK4 is the workhorse), Adams-Bashforth (multistep), and stiff-equation solvers (implicit methods like BDF). For PDEs: finite differences, finite elements, spectral methods. Stability (Lax equivalence theorem) and consistency must both hold for convergence.

Current State

Note: this information may be dated

Active research frontiers include:

  • Neural ODEs: parameterizing the derivative $\dot{\mathbf{h}} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}, t)$ with a neural network, treating depth as continuous time. Enables memory-efficient training via the adjoint method.
  • Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): embedding PDE residuals into the loss function; competitive on forward/inverse problems where data is sparse.
  • Operator learning (DeepONet, Fourier Neural Operator): learning mappings between function spaces — effectively learning solution operators for families of PDEs.
  • Stochastic PDEs: rough path theory (Hairer's regularity structures, 2014 Fields Medal) provides rigorous foundations for SPDEs previously ill-defined under classical Itô calculus.
  • Long-time behavior: proving ergodicity, mixing rates, and invariant measure properties for dissipative PDEs (e.g., Navier-Stokes, KPZ equation).

  1. Discretization choices: Finite difference, finite element, and spectral methods each discretize the same PDE differently, with different error profiles and stability properties. When is spectral convergence worth the non-locality, and when do finite elements win on complex geometry?

Category Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Abstract Algebra  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A category $\mathcal{C}$ consists of: - A collection of objects $\text{ob}(\mathcal{C})$ - For each pair $A, B \in \text{ob}(\mathcal{C})$, a set of morphisms $\text{Hom}(A, B)$ - A composition law: if $f \in \text{Hom}(A,B)$ and $g \in \text{Hom}(B,C)$, then $g \circ f \in \text{Hom}(A,C)$ - Identity morphisms: $\text{id}_A \in \text{Hom}(A,A)$ for each object $A$ Subject to: associativity $(h \circ g) \circ f = h \circ (g \circ f)$ and unit laws $f \circ \text{id}_A = f = \text{id}_B \circ f$.

Category theory, introduced by Eilenberg and Mac Lane (1945) in the context of algebraic topology, is often described as "the mathematics of mathematics." It abstracts away the internal structure of objects, focusing entirely on how structures relate — the morphisms between them.

A functor $F : \mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2$ is a structure-preserving map between categories: it sends objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, respecting composition and identities. A natural transformation $\eta : F \Rightarrow G$ between two functors is then a coherent family of morphisms $\eta_A : F(A) \to G(A)$ that commutes with all morphisms in the source category — it is a "morphism between morphisms between categories," completing a three-level hierarchy.

$$F(f) \circ \eta_A = \eta_B \circ G(f) \quad \forall f : A \to B$$

Why It Matters

Category theory serves as a unifying language across mathematics: limits/colimits subsume products, coproducts, equalizers, and pullbacks under one concept; adjunctions capture free/forgetful constructions and dualities ubiquitous across algebra and topology.

In computer science, the connection is deep and productive:

  • Monads (a monoid in the category of endofunctors) model computational effects in Haskell and other functional languages
  • Cartesian closed categories are the semantic backbone of the typed lambda calculus via the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence
  • Functors map directly onto fmap in Haskell's Functor typeclass
TIP: The Yoneda Perspective
The Yoneda Lemma — arguably the most important result in category theory — states that any object $A$ is completely determined by the functor $\text{Hom}(A, -)$: its relationships to all other objects. This is a precise formulation of the structuralist intuition that an object is its role in a system, not its internal constitution. $$\text{Nat}(\text{Hom}(A,-), F) \cong F(A)$$

Key Details

  1. Limits and Colimits: Universal constructions that generalize products, equalizers, pullbacks (limits) and coproducts, pushouts, quotients (colimits). A category with all small limits is called complete; all small colimits, cocomplete.
  1. Adjunctions: A functor pair $F \dashv G$ (left and right adjoint) satisfies $\text{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(FA, B) \cong \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, GB)$, naturally in $A$ and $B$. Adjoints are pervasive: free/forgetful, suspension/loop space, product/exponential.
  1. Equivalence vs. Isomorphism: Category theory enforces the discipline that the right notion of sameness between categories is equivalence (not strict isomorphism), where the unit and counit of an adjunction are natural isomorphisms — a lesson that extends into homotopy type theory.
  1. Higher Categories: $n$-categories have morphisms between morphisms between… up to level $n$, with $(\infty,1)$-categories (quasi-categories, complete Segal spaces) central to modern homotopy theory and derived algebraic geometry.
  1. Topos Theory: A topos is a category that behaves like the category of sets — it has a subobject classifier, power objects, and finite limits. Every topos defines an internal logic; Grothendieck toposes underpin modern algebraic geometry.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include $(\infty,1)$-category theory (Lurie's program via Higher Topos Theory), homotopy type theory (where types are $\infty$-groupoids and univalence is the categorical identity principle), and applied category theory — using categorical structure in databases (functorial data migration), machine learning (compositional models via string diagrams), and distributed systems (sheaf-based consistency).

String diagrams have recently gained traction as a graphical calculus for monoidal categories, offering an intuitive but rigorous proof framework used heavily in quantum computing and linguistics.
