Game Semantics✓ reviewed

Logic · Semantics  ·  ~5 min read

Core Concept

INFO
Game semantics interprets logical formulas as two-player games between a Verifier (∃loïse, Prover) and a Falsifier (∀bélarD, Opponent). A formula is true (or valid) in a given model if and only if the Verifier possesses a winning strategy — a function from game histories to moves that guarantees a win regardless of the Opponent's play.

Two distinct but related traditions dominate:

  • Dialogical Logic (Lorenzen & Lorenz, 1950s–60s): proof-theoretic in spirit; the game is between a Proponent defending a thesis and an Opponent attacking it. Validity is defined without reference to models — purely through the existence of a formal winning dialogue.
  • Game-Theoretical Semantics (GTS) (Hintikka, 1960s–): model-theoretic in spirit. For a formula $\varphi$ and model $\mathcal{M}$, the game $G(\mathcal{M}, \varphi)$ decomposes $\varphi$ structurally:

$$ G(\mathcal{M}, \varphi \lor \psi): \text{ Verifier chooses a disjunct.} \quad G(\mathcal{M}, \varphi \land \psi): \text{ Falsifier chooses a conjunct.} $$

$$ G(\mathcal{M}, \exists x\, \varphi): \text{ Verifier picks a witness } a \in |\mathcal{M}|. \quad G(\mathcal{M}, \forall x\, \varphi): \text{ Falsifier picks.} $$

Terminal positions are atomic: Verifier wins iff the atom holds in $\mathcal{M}$. Determined games (where one player always has a winning strategy) recover classical semantics; restricting to finite or constructive strategies yields intuitionistic or linear logics.

Why It Matters

Game semantics isn't merely a reformulation of Tarski-style truth — it reframes logic as interaction, which opens several crucial doors:

  1. Full Abstraction for PCF: Abramsky, Jagadeesan, Malacaria (and independently Hyland & Ong) used game semantics in the 1990s to give the first fully abstract denotational model for PCF (a prototypical functional language). This solved a ~20-year open problem by capturing observational equivalence exactly — strategies model programs up to their interactive behavior, not just their input-output functions.
  1. Linear Logic: The Curry-Howard correspondence for linear logic finds its most natural semantics here. The Opponent/Proponent duality maps cleanly onto the resource-sensitive connectives $\otimes, \parr, !$.
  1. Concurrency & Types: Game models handle concurrency naturally — interleaving of moves corresponds to thread scheduling. This yields compositional type systems for concurrent languages.
  1. Complexity: Winning strategies in games with bounded resources correspond to complexity classes; e.g., alternating polynomial-time ≅ PSPACE.
TIP
The shift from static truth-tables to dynamic strategy profiles is philosophically profound: it makes use of a formula (its role in dialogue or computation) constitutive of its meaning. This resonates with Wittgenstein's meaning-as-use, but with full mathematical precision.

Key Details

1. Strategies as morphisms.

In the categorical formulation (Hyland-Ong style), games are objects and innocent strategies (strategies whose moves depend only on the visible history, not the full play) are morphisms. This gives a compact closed category modeling higher-order computation.

2. Innocence and definability.

Not every strategy corresponds to a computable function. Innocent strategies — where the Verifier's next move depends only on the P-view (the Verifier's perspective of the history) — characterize exactly the definable higher-order functions in PCF. Without this restriction the model is too large.

3. Imperfect information & IF logic.

Hintikka's Independence-Friendly (IF) logic extends GTS to games of imperfect information, where the Verifier must sometimes move without knowing the Opponent's prior choices. This breaks the duality between $\exists/\forall$ and allows expression of game-theoretically intermediate notions of truth — the logic is not axiomatizable by any r.e. set.

4. Proof-search as game play.

In dialogical logic, a formula is intuitionistically provable iff the Proponent has a winning strategy under intuitionistic dialogue rules (Proponent may only use the last granted concession). This gives a proof-search procedure, not just a semantic criterion.

5. Connection to mu-calculus.

Model-checking for the modal mu-calculus $L_\mu$ is equivalent to determining winners of parity games. Parity games are in NP ∩ co-NP, and whether they're in P was a longstanding open question — resolved in 2017 by Calude et al. with a quasi-polynomial algorithm.

Riemannian Geometry✓ reviewed

Math · Geometry  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A Riemannian manifold is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a Riemannian metric $g$: a family of inner products $g_p : T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}$ on each tangent space, varying smoothly in $p$. This single structure encodes lengths, angles, areas, and volumes intrinsically — without reference to an ambient space.

The length of a smooth curve $\gamma : [a,b] \to M$ is:

$$L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}\!\left(\dot\gamma(t),\, \dot\gamma(t)\right)}\, dt$$

Geodesics — locally length-minimizing curves — satisfy the geodesic equation derived from the Levi-Civita connection $\nabla$ (the unique torsion-free metric-compatible connection):

$$\nabla_{\dot\gamma} \dot\gamma = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \ddot\gamma^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\,\dot\gamma^i\dot\gamma^j = 0$$

where $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ are the Christoffel symbols of $g$. Curvature is encoded in the Riemann tensor $R(X,Y)Z = \nabla_X\nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y\nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]}Z$, capturing how parallel transport around infinitesimal loops fails to be trivial.

Why It Matters

Riemannian geometry is the mathematical backbone of Einstein's general relativity, where spacetime is a pseudo-Riemannian manifold (signature $(-,+,+,+)$) and gravity is curvature. Beyond physics, it is foundational to:

  • Machine learning / data science: Optimization on matrix manifolds (e.g., SPD matrices, Grassmannians, Lie groups) uses Riemannian gradient descent.
  • Geometric analysis: Ricci flow (Perelman's proof of the Poincaré conjecture) evolves the metric by $\partial_t g_{ij} = -2R_{ij}$.
  • Representation theory: Symmetric spaces $G/K$ carry canonical invariant metrics, linking geometry to Lie group structure.
  • Topology: Comparison theorems (Bishop–Gromov, Bonnet–Myers) use curvature bounds to constrain global topology.
TIP: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic
Gauss's Theorema Egregium was the first hint: the Gaussian curvature of a surface is intrinsic — it depends only on the metric, not how the surface sits in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Riemann's 1854 Habilitationsvortrag radicalized this: geometry is entirely determined by $g$, no embedding required.

Key Details

  1. Sectional, Ricci, and Scalar Curvature: The Riemann tensor $R_{ijkl}$ has 20 independent components in 4D. Contracting gives the Ricci tensor $R_{ij} = R^k{}_{ikj}$ and then scalar curvature $R = g^{ij}R_{ij}$. The sectional curvature $K(\sigma)$ for a 2-plane $\sigma \subset T_pM$ is a single number measuring how geodesics through $p$ in that plane diverge relative to flat space.
  1. Comparison Geometry: If $\text{Ric} \geq (n-1)\kappa\, g$, the manifold behaves no worse than the space form of constant curvature $\kappa$. This lets you bound diameter, volume, and fundamental group purely from a curvature inequality — a powerful "soft" technique.
  1. The Exponential Map: $\exp_p : T_pM \to M$ sends a tangent vector $v$ to the endpoint of the unit-speed geodesic from $p$ in direction $v/|v|$ at time $|v|$. It is a local diffeomorphism near $0$; its breakdown radius is the injectivity radius $\text{inj}(p)$.
  1. Hodge Theory: On a compact Riemannian manifold, each de Rham cohomology class has a unique harmonic representative — a differential form $\omega$ with $\Delta \omega = 0$ (where $\Delta = d\delta + \delta d$ is the Hodge Laplacian). This bridges analysis and topology.
  1. Curvature–Topology Theorems: Positive sectional curvature $\Rightarrow$ finite fundamental group (Bonnet–Myers). Non-positive sectional curvature $\Rightarrow$ contractible universal cover (Cartan–Hadamard). These are among the deepest results tying local metric data to global structure.

  • Optimal transport geometry: The curvature-dimension condition $\mathrm{CD}(K,N)$ of Lott–Sturm–Villani extends Riemannian ideas to metric measure spaces, enabling "Riemannian geometry without smoothness."
  • Sub-Riemannian geometry: When the metric is defined only on a subbundle of $TM$, qualitatively different phenomena emerge (hypoelliptic operators, Carnot groups).
  • Applications in physics: Recent arXiv work (e.g., Duary 2026) exploits geodesic-adapted coordinate systems in global AdS spacetime — a pseudo-Riemannian setting — to derive Liénard–Wiechert fields and antipodal matching conditions, illustrating how intrinsic Riemannian toolkit transfers directly to curved spacetime electrodynamics.

Spectral Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Spectral theory generalizes the classical eigenvalue problem from finite-dimensional matrices to operators acting on infinite-dimensional spaces — chiefly Hilbert and Banach spaces. The central object is the spectrum of an operator $T$, defined as:

$$\sigma(T) = \{\lambda \in \mathbb{C} : (T - \lambda I) \text{ is not boundedly invertible}\}$$

Its complement $\rho(T) = \mathbb{C} \setminus \sigma(T)$ is the resolvent set, on which the resolvent operator

$$R(\lambda, T) = (T - \lambda I)^{-1}$$

is well-defined and analytic. This analytic dependence on $\lambda$ is what ties spectral theory deeply to complex analysis.

INFO
The spectrum decomposes into three disjoint parts: the point spectrum $\sigma_p(T)$ (eigenvalues), the continuous spectrum $\sigma_c(T)$ (no eigenvalue, but range not dense), and the residual spectrum $\sigma_r(T)$ (range not dense). For self-adjoint operators on Hilbert spaces, $\sigma_r(T) = \emptyset$.

For self-adjoint operators $T = T^*$, the spectral theorem guarantees a projection-valued measure $E$ such that:

$$T = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda \, dE(\lambda)$$

This is the infinite-dimensional analogue of diagonalization.

Why It Matters

Spectral theory is the mathematical backbone of quantum mechanics — the spectrum of a self-adjoint Hamiltonian $H$ is exactly the set of measurable energy values. Beyond physics, it permeates:

  • PDEs: Sturm–Liouville theory, where solutions decompose via eigenfunction expansions (e.g., Fourier series as spectral decomposition of $-d^2/dx^2$)
  • Numerical analysis: Stability and convergence of iterative solvers depend on the spectral radius $\rho(T) = \sup_{\lambda \in \sigma(T)} |\lambda|$
  • Graph theory / data science: Graph Laplacian spectra encode connectivity, enabling spectral clustering
  • Dynamical systems: Koopman operator theory recasts nonlinear dynamics as linear spectral problems on function spaces
TIP
The connection to analytic function theory isn't merely formal. The resolvent $R(\lambda, T)$ is analytic on $\rho(T)$, and spectral projections are recovered via contour integrals — the Dunford–Riesz calculus — meaning you can define $f(T)$ for any function analytic on a neighborhood of $\sigma(T)$.

Key Details

  1. Compact operators: On infinite-dimensional spaces, compact operators behave most like matrices. Their spectrum consists of $\{0\}$ plus at most countably many eigenvalues accumulating only at 0 (Riesz–Schauder theorem).
  1. Continuous vs. point spectrum: Unlike matrices, infinite-dimensional operators can have eigenvalue-free spectra. The free particle Hamiltonian $H = -\Delta$ on $L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ has purely continuous spectrum $[0, \infty)$ — no $L^2$ eigenfunctions exist, only distributional ones.
  1. Spectral gap: The gap $\delta = \inf\{|\lambda - \mu| : \lambda \in \sigma(T),\, \mu \in \sigma(T),\, \lambda \neq \mu\}$ controls mixing times in Markov chains and stability of ground states. A nonzero spectral gap is a prized property.
  1. Non-self-adjoint operators: The spectrum becomes highly sensitive to perturbations — pseudospectral analysis ($\varepsilon$-pseudospectra) replaces sharp spectral analysis. This matters for non-normal matrices and hydrodynamic stability.
  1. $C^*$-algebra framework: Spectral theory lifts naturally to abstract $C^*$-algebras via the Gelfand transform: a commutative $C^*$-algebra is isometrically isomorphic to $C(\sigma(a))$ for a normal element $a$, revealing spectrum as a purely algebraic invariant.

Note: this information may be dated

  • Spectral geometry: Inverse problems — "Can you hear the shape of a drum?" (Kac, 1966) — remain open in general; isospectral non-isometric manifolds exist.
  • AdS/CFT and operator algebras: Spectral properties of bulk operators in anti-de Sitter space connect to conformal field theory data via the operator-state correspondence, with recent work on Liénard–Wiechert fields in AdS illuminating how spectral/analytic structure controls boundary behavior at null infinity.
  • Koopman operator learning: Data-driven approximation of infinite-dimensional Koopman spectra (via DMD, EDMD) is an active intersection of spectral theory and machine learning.

Materials Science✓ reviewed

Science · Physics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Materials science is the study of the relationship between a material's internal structure—spanning atomic bonding, crystallographic order, defect topology, and microstructure—and its macroscopic properties (mechanical, electrical, thermal, optical). The governing paradigm is:

$$\text{Processing} \rightarrow \text{Structure} \rightarrow \text{Properties} \rightarrow \text{Performance}$$

This is not a linear chain but a feedback loop: desired performance targets constrain property specifications, which constrain structural requirements, which constrain viable processing routes.

INFO: **The Structure Hierarchy**
Structure in materials science spans multiple length scales: - Atomic (~Å): bonding type, crystal lattice, point defects - Nano/micro (nm–μm): grain boundaries, dislocations, precipitates, phases - Macro (mm+): porosity, surface finish, residual stress fields Each scale influences different property sets. Dislocations dominate yield strength; grain size governs fracture toughness via Hall-Petch scaling ($\sigma_y \propto d^{-1/2}$).

Why It Matters

Materials science is the hidden substrate of every technological era. The progression from Stone Age → Bronze Age → Iron Age → Silicon Age is literally a materials progression. Contemporary relevance includes:

  • Energy: battery electrode microstructure governs ion transport kinetics; photovoltaic efficiency depends on defect density and band-gap engineering
  • Biomaterials: scaffold porosity, surface chemistry, and mechanical compliance determine osseointegration and cell viability
  • Semiconductors: dopant concentration profiles, junction geometries, and dielectric interfaces are materials engineering problems disguised as electronics
  • Failure analysis: fracture mechanics, corrosion pathways, and fatigue crack propagation models prevent catastrophic failures in aviation, civil infrastructure, and pressure vessels
TIP: **Cross-disciplinary leverage point**
Materials science sits at the intersection of solid-state physics (band theory, phonons, defect physics) and chemical thermodynamics (phase diagrams, diffusion, nucleation theory). Mastery of both gives you predictive power that purely empirical materials selection cannot.

Key Details

1. Defects are features, not bugs.

Crystal perfection is the exception. Dislocations enable plastic deformation (work-hardening); dopant atoms in semiconductors are intentional point defects; grain boundaries scatter phonons to reduce thermal conductivity in thermoelectrics. The art is controlling defect type, density, and distribution.

2. Phase diagrams encode thermodynamic equilibrium.

The binary Fe–C phase diagram explains why quenched steel is hard (metastable martensite) vs. annealed steel (equilibrium ferrite + pearlite). Kinetics determines which equilibrium is actually reached: cooling rate is a processing lever that selects microstructural outcome.

3. Structure–property relationships are scale-sensitive.

The same material exhibits radically different properties at the nanoscale. Gold nanoparticles absorb green light (plasmon resonance) rather than reflecting it. This is not a chemical change—it is a size-induced shift in the electronic structure.

4. The Hall-Petch relation and its inversion.

$$\sigma_y = \sigma_0 + \frac{k}{\sqrt{d}}$$

Yield strength increases as grain size $d$ decreases—until grain boundaries become so dense (d < ~10 nm) that deformation shifts to grain-boundary sliding and strength drops (inverse Hall-Petch).

5. Bonding type as first-order predictor.

Ionic → brittle, high melting point, ionic conductor. Covalent → hard, brittle, semiconductor/insulator. Metallic → ductile, conductive. Van der Waals → soft, low melting point. Mixed bonding (e.g., intermetallics) produces hybrid behavior that often cannot be predicted by linear interpolation.

  • High-entropy alloys (HEAs): multi-principal-element alloys with configurational entropy stabilizing disordered solid solutions; unexpectedly good strength-toughness trade-offs
  • 2D materials beyond graphene: MXenes, transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), and their heterostructures for electronics and energy storage
  • Computational materials science: DFT, molecular dynamics, and machine-learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) are compressing the materials discovery cycle; the Materials Project database now hosts >150,000 computed compounds
  • Additive manufacturing (AM) microstructure control: laser powder-bed fusion creates non-equilibrium microstructures (columnar grains, cellular solidification) that require new processing–structure models
