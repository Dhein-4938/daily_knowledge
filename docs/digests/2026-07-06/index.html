Neural ODEs✓ reviewed

Tech · Machine Learning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

A Neural ODE replaces discrete layer stacking in a residual network with a continuous-depth analogue. Instead of defining a sequence of transformations $\mathbf{h}_{t+1} = \mathbf{h}_t + f(\mathbf{h}_t, \theta)$, it posits a hidden state governed by an ordinary differential equation:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{h}(t)}{dt} = f\bigl(\mathbf{h}(t),\, t,\, \theta\bigr)$$

where $f$ is a neural network. The output at "depth" $T$ is obtained by integrating from an initial condition $\mathbf{h}(0) = \mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{h}(T) = \mathbf{h}(0) + \int_0^T f\bigl(\mathbf{h}(t), t, \theta\bigr)\, dt$$

Any black-box ODE solver (Dormand–Prince, Adams–Bashforth, etc.) can approximate this integral. The solver chooses its own step sizes adaptively, making the computational graph implicit rather than fixed.

INFO
The number of function evaluations (NFE) of the ODE solver plays the role of "depth." Tighter error tolerances → more NFE → higher effective depth. This depth is dynamic and input-dependent.

Why It Matters

Memory efficiency. Backpropagation through the solver would store all intermediate states. Instead, Chen et al. (2018) derived the adjoint sensitivity method: integrate a co-state $\mathbf{a}(t) = -\partial L/\partial \mathbf{h}(t)$ backwards in time, requiring only $O(1)$ memory regardless of integration depth.

$$\frac{d\mathbf{a}}{dt} = -\mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{h}}$$

Continuous-time modeling. Time-series with irregular sampling (clinical records, financial ticks, physical sensors) fit naturally — no need to impute or bin. Latent ODEs combine a recurrent encoder with an ODE decoder to learn dynamics from sparse, irregular observations.

Normalizing flows. The FFJORD variant replaces change-of-variables determinant computation $\log|\det J|$ with the instantaneous form:

$$\frac{d \log p(\mathbf{z}(t))}{dt} = -\operatorname{tr}\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}}\right)$$

This makes exact maximum-likelihood training of continuous flows tractable via Hutchinson's trace estimator ($O(d)$ vs. $O(d^3)$).

Key Details

  1. Adjoint method caveats. Numerical errors in the reverse-time integration can cause gradient inaccuracies. Checkpointing intermediate states or using symplectic solvers mitigates this; the "vanilla" adjoint can be unstable for stiff systems.
  1. Stiffness and solver choice. Many physical and learned dynamics are stiff (fast and slow timescales coexist). Explicit solvers (RK45) become prohibitively expensive; implicit or semi-implicit solvers (Radau, BDF) are needed but harder to differentiate through.
  1. Augmented Neural ODEs (ANODE). The universal approximation argument for Neural ODEs requires the vector field to be non-crossing (by uniqueness of ODE solutions). Augmenting the state with extra dimensions $[\mathbf{h}(t), \mathbf{a}(t)] \in \mathbb{R}^{d+p}$ breaks this constraint and dramatically expands expressibility.
  1. Relation to ResNets. Euler discretization of the ODE ($\Delta t = 1$) recovers the standard residual block. Neural ODEs are thus the continuum limit of very deep, weight-tied ResNets.
  1. Hamiltonian / symplectic variants. Constraining $f$ to derive from a learned Hamiltonian $H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$ enforces energy conservation and reversibility — critical for physical simulation and molecular dynamics.
TIP
The adjoint method is essentially the continuous analogue of backpropagation: both propagate a co-state signal backward through a computational process. This unification hints at why Neural ODEs sit at the intersection of optimal control theory and deep learning.

Active lines of work include:

  • Score-based diffusion meets Neural ODEs. Diffusion models can be cast as SDEs/ODEs via the probability flow ODE (Song et al.), enabling deterministic sampling and exact likelihoods. Efficient ODE solvers (DPM-Solver, DDIM) are a major practical lever.
  • Neural PDEs and operator learning. Extending the ODE framework to PDEs via neural operators (FNO, DeepONet) for weather, fluid, and quantum systems.
  • Scalability. Training cost scales with NFE; recent work on step distillation and flow matching reduces this, trading the full solver for a learned few-step surrogate.
  • Theoretical expressiveness. Characterizing which flows are learnable and when augmentation is necessary remains open; connections to optimal transport (Brenier maps, Wasserstein geodesics) are being formalized.

  1. Flow matching reframes generative modeling as learning a vector field interpolating between noise and data distributions, bypassing the ODE solver during training. Does this make Neural ODEs obsolete as generative tools, or do exact-likelihood applications preserve their niche?

Measure Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A measure on a set $X$ is a function $\mu: \mathcal{F} \to [0, \infty]$ defined on a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)$ satisfying: 1. $\mu(\emptyset) = 0$ 2. Countable additivity: for pairwise disjoint $\{A_i\} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, $\mu\!\left(\bigsqcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mu(A_i)$ The triple $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ is a measure space.

The $\sigma$-algebra requirement is essential: not every subset of $\mathbb{R}$ is Lebesgue-measurable (Vitali sets witness this). The structure enforces closure under countable unions and complements, making infinite limiting operations coherent. The Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$ extends the intuitive length of intervals — $\lambda([a,b]) = b - a$ — to a vast class of "well-behaved" sets.

Why It Matters

Measure theory is the lingua franca of modern analysis. Its significance spans three pillars:

  • Integration: The Lebesgue integral $\int_X f \, d\mu$ dominates the Riemann integral in generality. It handles limits under the integral sign via dominated convergence and monotone convergence theorems — workhorses of analysis.
  • Probability: Every rigorous probability space is $(Ω, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ with $\mathbb{P}(Ω) = 1$. Concepts like conditional expectation, martingales, and stochastic processes are incomprehensible without it.
  • Functional Analysis: $L^p(X, \mu)$ spaces are defined measure-theoretically. Spectral theory for self-adjoint operators on Hilbert spaces relies on the spectral measure — a projection-valued measure on $\mathbb{R}$.
TIP: Physics Connection
In quantum mechanics, observables correspond to self-adjoint operators whose spectral decomposition yields a projection-valued measure $E(\cdot)$. The probability of measuring eigenvalue in Borel set $B$ from state $|\psi\rangle$ is $\langle \psi | E(B) | \psi \rangle$ — measure theory made physical.

Key Details

1. Carathéodory's Extension Theorem

You never define a measure on the full $\sigma$-algebra directly. Instead, define $\mu_0$ on an algebra (closed under finite operations), verify $\sigma$-additivity, and extend uniquely to the generated $\sigma$-algebra. This is how Lebesgue measure is constructed from interval lengths.

2. Absolute Continuity and the Radon–Nikodym Theorem

If $\nu \ll \mu$ (every $\mu$-null set is $\nu$-null), then $\exists$ measurable $f \geq 0$ such that:

$$\nu(A) = \int_A f \, d\mu \quad \forall A \in \mathcal{F}$$

The density $f = \frac{d\nu}{d\mu}$ is the Radon–Nikodym derivative — the rigorous foundation of probability densities.

3. Convergence Theorems

Three theorems govern interchange of limits and integrals:

  • Monotone Convergence: $0 \leq f_n \nearrow f \Rightarrow \int f_n \to \int f$
  • Dominated Convergence: $|f_n| \leq g \in L^1$, $f_n \to f$ a.e. $\Rightarrow \int f_n \to \int f$
  • Fatou's Lemma: $\int \liminf f_n \leq \liminf \int f_n$

4. Product Measures and Fubini–Tonelli

Given $(X, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ and $(Y, \mathcal{G}, \nu)$, the product measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ on $X \times Y$ satisfies $(\mu \otimes \nu)(A \times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B)$. Fubini's theorem licenses iterated integration when $f \in L^1(\mu \otimes \nu)$ — a condition that cannot be dropped (Fubini failure is a genuine pathology).

5. Signed and Complex Measures

Relaxing $\mu: \mathcal{F} \to [0,\infty]$ to $\mu: \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$) yields signed/complex measures. The Hahn decomposition partitions $X$ into positive and negative parts; the Jordan decomposition writes $\mu = \mu^+ - \mu^-$ with $\mu^\pm$ positive measures.

Current State

Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include geometric measure theory (rectifiability, varifolds, currents — used in minimal surface problems and image processing), optimal transport (Wasserstein distances between measures, with deep connections to PDEs and machine learning), and ergodic theory (measure-preserving dynamics). A probabilistic lens on measure-theoretic objects appears in recent analytic work — for instance, normalizing integrands to induce probability laws, then expressing analytic properties (monotonicity, log-convexity) via stochastic orders and covariance identities over induced distributions. This technique yields sharp inequalities for hypergeometric and integral-transform quotients.

Linear Logic✓ reviewed

Logic · Logic  ·  ~5 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Linear logic is a substructural logic introduced by Jean-Yves Girard (1987) that controls the use of the structural rules of contraction (using a hypothesis multiple times) and weakening (discarding a hypothesis). The result is a logic where propositions denote resources — entities that must be used exactly once unless explicitly marked otherwise.

In classical sequent calculus, Gentzen's structural rules allow:

$$\frac{\Gamma, A, A \vdash B}{\Gamma, A \vdash B} \text{(contraction)} \qquad \frac{\Gamma \vdash B}{\Gamma, A \vdash B} \text{(weakening)}$$

Linear logic restricts both. The canonical reading: if $A \vdash B$, consuming resource $A$ produces resource $B$ — you cannot freely copy or discard $A$.

Linear logic introduces a richer connective vocabulary to recover expressiveness:

| Connective | Symbol | Flavor | Intuition |

|---|---|---|---|

| Tensor | $A \otimes B$ | Multiplicative | Have both A and B simultaneously |

| Par | $A \parr B$ | Multiplicative | Dual of tensor |

| With | $A \mathbin{\&} B$ | Additive | Choose one of A or B |

| Plus | $A \oplus B$ | Additive | Externally given one of A or B |

| Of course | $!A$ | Exponential | A is unlimited (contraction/weakening restored) |

| Why not | $?A$ | Exponential | Dual of $!A$ |

The exponential modalities $!$ and $?$ are the precise gatekeepers that re-admit classical structural freedom when needed.

Why It Matters

Linear logic sits at an unusual crossroads — it is simultaneously a foundational proof theory, a practical programming language model, and a physics-adjacent formalism.

Programming languages. The Curry–Howard correspondence extends to linear logic: linear types correspond to linear proofs. A linear type system enforces that a value is used exactly once, making it ideal for modeling:

  • Memory ownership (Rust's borrow checker is a practical descendant)
  • Session types for typed communication protocols
  • Safe resource management without garbage collection

Game semantics. Girard's Geometry of Interaction reframes cut-elimination as token passing on a graph, with proofs as strategies in a game between Proponent and Opponent. This gives a fully abstract model for PCF and related languages.

Quantum information. No-cloning and no-deleting theorems in quantum mechanics mirror exactly the prohibition on contraction and weakening. Linear logic is therefore a natural logical substrate for quantum computation — $!A$ corresponds to classical (freely copyable) data.

TIP: The Resource Metaphor
Think of linear implication $A \multimap B$ as a vending machine: insert one $A$, receive one $B$. The machine is consumed in the transaction. Classical implication $A \Rightarrow B$ is a theorem that can be applied infinitely; linear implication is a process.

Key Details

  1. Multiplicative vs. Additive split. The two flavors of conjunction ($\otimes$ vs $\&$) and disjunction ($\parr$ vs $\oplus$) are genuinely distinct. $A \otimes B$ means you hold both resources simultaneously; $A \mathbin{\&} B$ means you can redeem either, but choosing one forfeits the other. Confusing these is the most common error for newcomers.
  1. MLL is decidable; MELL is not. Multiplicative linear logic (MLL, without exponentials) is decidable and has clean combinatorial proof theory. Adding exponentials ($!$/$?$) yields MELL, which is Turing-complete and undecidable — the exponentials recover the full power of classical logic.
  1. Proof nets. Girard introduced proof nets as a canonical (syntax-independent) representation of MLL proofs, eliminating bureaucratic rule-permutations. Correctness of a proof net is checkable by the Danos–Regnier criterion (acyclicity + connectedness of a "correction graph"). Extending proof nets to MELL remains an active research problem.
  1. Denotational semantics via monoidal categories. Linear logic corresponds to -autonomous categories (the categorical semantics of classical linear logic), while intuitionistic linear logic corresponds to symmetric monoidal closed categories. This is a strict refinement: cartesian closed categories (for intuitionistic logic) embed into SMCCs via the $!$ comonad.
  1. Phase semantics. The original Girard semantics uses phases — a commutative monoid with a distinguished set of "facts." This gives completeness but is not fully abstract; coherence spaces and later game-semantic models provide more refined interpretations.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research threads include:

  • Differential linear logic (Ehrhard & Regnier): adds a differentiation operator, connecting linear logic to Taylor expansion and the semantics of probabilistic and differentiable programs.
  • Graded/quantitative type systems: generalizing $!$ to a semiring of usage counts (e.g., use at most twice), giving finer resource tracking. This underlies systems like Granule and Idris 2's quantitative types.
  • Cyclic and non-wellfounded proofs: extending linear logic to infinite proof trees with productivity conditions, relevant for reasoning about coinductive data and streams.
  • Linear logic and deep learning: the connection to differential linear logic and automatic differentiation is being explored as a semantic foundation for gradient-based learning.

Descriptive Complexity✓ reviewed

Logic · Complexity Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Descriptive complexity characterizes computational complexity classes by the expressive power of logical languages over finite structures, rather than by resource bounds on abstract machines. The central insight: a decision problem is in a given complexity class if and only if it is definable by a sentence in a corresponding logical system.

INFO: Formal Setup
Fix a finite relational vocabulary $\sigma$. A query is a mapping from finite $\sigma$-structures to $\{0, 1\}$. Descriptive complexity identifies logical fragments $\mathcal{L}$ such that: $$\text{QUERY}(\mathcal{L}) = \mathcal{C}$$ where $\mathcal{C}$ is a complexity class and $\text{QUERY}(\mathcal{L})$ is the set of queries expressible in $\mathcal{L}$ (restricted to finite models).

The canonical correspondences (all on ordered structures unless noted):

| Logic | Complexity Class |

|---|---|

| $\Sigma_1^1$ (existential SO) | NP |

| SO (full second-order) | PH |

| FO + LFP (least fixed point) | P |

| FO + TC (transitive closure) | NL |

| FO + PFP (partial fixed point) | PSPACE |

| FO alone | AC⁰ (roughly) |

Why It Matters

The machine-independence of logical definitions provides evidence that complexity classes like P and NP are mathematically natural — they do not merely reflect quirks of Turing machines or RAM models. If a class has a clean logical characterization, it suggests the class carves nature at its joints.

TIP: Logic as a Proof Transfer Mechanism
Results proven in finite model theory (e.g., preservation theorems, 0-1 laws) automatically carry implications for complexity, and vice versa. The Immerman–Szelepcsényi theorem (NL = co-NL) was essentially rediscovered via the inductive definability of transitive closure in both directions.

The framework also clarifies why certain reductions work: many natural problems are NP-complete because they express existential quantification over combinatorial structures (colorings, satisfying assignments, matchings) — exactly $\Sigma_1^1$ sentences.

Key Details

1. Fagin's Theorem (1974) — The foundational result. NP = $\Sigma_1^1$: a class of finite structures is in NP iff it is the set of models of some existential second-order sentence. The proof is elegant: a Turing machine's accepting computation is itself a finite structure existentially quantifiable over.

2. The Role of Order — Most logical characterizations require a built-in linear order on the domain. Without it, FO + LFP does not capture P, and many correspondences break. Whether P has an order-independent logical characterization is tied to open questions about isomorphism-invariant computation.

3. Fixed-Point Operators as Iteration — LFP extends FO by allowing inductive definitions: you iteratively apply a formula until it stabilizes. Each stage corresponds to one step of a polynomial-time computation. PFP allows the fixpoint to not converge (divergence = reject), yielding PSPACE.

4. Immerman's Theorem — FO + TC captures NL on ordered structures. Transitive closure is the key primitive: graph reachability is the NL-complete problem, and all NL queries reduce to iterated reachability.

5. Descriptive vs. Circuit Complexity — FO alone corresponds roughly to constant-depth circuits (AC⁰), connecting logical quantifier depth to circuit depth. FO + COUNT (with modular counting) extends to TC⁰, linking numerical quantifiers to threshold gates.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated.

Active research concentrates on: (a) capturing P without an order predicate — the so-called order problem, related to the graph isomorphism question; (b) extending the framework to probabilistic and parameterized complexity classes; (c) connections to database query languages (Datalog ↔ LFP, SQL ↔ FO), where descriptive complexity directly informs query expressibility and optimization; and (d) links to proof complexity, where the logical depth of a formula correlates with the length of formal proofs.

The open question of whether there is a logic for P (on unordered structures) remains one of the deepest problems in finite model theory.
