SAT Solvers✓ reviewed

Logic · Automated Reasoning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

A SAT solver is an algorithm that decides whether a Boolean formula — typically in Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) — has a satisfying variable assignment. Formally:

$$\text{Given } \varphi(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \text{CNF}, \text{ find } \mathbf{v} \in \{0,1\}^n \text{ s.t. } \varphi(\mathbf{v}) = \top, \text{ or report UNSAT}$$

A CNF formula is a conjunction of clauses, each clause being a disjunction of literals (variables or their negations). The hardness of SAT is governed by clause-to-variable ratio; near the phase transition (~4.267 for 3-SAT), instances become exponentially harder.

[!info] Cook–Levin Theorem

SAT was the first problem proven NP-complete (Cook 1971, Levin 1973). Every problem in NP reduces to SAT in polynomial time, making it the canonical hard problem. Solving SAT in deterministic polynomial time would prove P = NP.

Why It Matters

SAT solvers are a cornerstone of modern computer science despite the NP-completeness barrier, because practical instances are often far easier than worst-case:

  • Hardware verification: Equivalence checking and model checking for circuits (bounded model checking reduces directly to SAT)
  • Software analysis: Symbolic execution, program synthesis, bug finding
  • AI planning & scheduling: STRIPS-style planning encodes as SAT
  • Cryptanalysis: Algebraic attacks on stream/block ciphers
  • Combinatorics: Resolving open conjectures (e.g., Schur number five proven via SAT in 2016)

[!tip] SAT solvers are often the hidden engine behind higher-level tools — SMT solvers (Z3, CVC5) reduce to SAT for the Boolean skeleton, while model checkers like IC3/PDR use incremental SAT calls internally.

Key Details

1. DPLL and CDCL

The dominant paradigm is Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL), extending the Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland (DPLL) backtracking algorithm. When a conflict is reached, the solver performs resolution on the implication graph to derive a learned clause, then backtracks non-chronologically. This transforms exponential search into targeted pruning.

2. Unit Propagation and BCP

Boolean Constraint Propagation (BCP) — the forced assignment of unit clauses — is the inner loop and consumes ~80% of runtime. The two-watched-literal scheme makes BCP nearly O(1) amortized per propagation.

3. Variable & Restart Heuristics

  • VSIDS (Variable State Independent Decaying Sum): prioritizes variables involved in recent conflicts — captures a form of learned relevance
  • Luby restarts: periodic restart schedule provably near-optimal for certain random distributions
  • Phase saving: remembers last polarity for each variable, exploiting structure in industrial instances

4. Preprocessing

Modern solvers (CaDiCaL, Kissat) apply aggressive preprocessing: variable elimination via resolution, subsumption, blocked clause elimination. These can reduce formula size by orders of magnitude before search begins.

5. Proof Logging

Industrial-strength solvers now emit DRAT proofs (Delete Resolution Asymmetric Tautology), enabling independent verification of UNSAT certificates — critical for formal hardware proofs.

Current State

[!warning] Note: this information may be dated

The frontier is neuro-symbolic hybridization. Recent work (G-RRM, 2026) demonstrates that recurrent reasoning models can guide CDCL solvers by predicting branching decisions. The key finding: neural guidance yields substantial speedups (up to 33×) only when (1) the combinatorial search space is large, and (2) the solver can override incorrect neural hints. Critically, CaDiCaL 3.0.0 showed no significant speedup because it always commits to injected hints without recovery — underscoring that graceful degradation under imperfect advice is architecturally non-trivial.

Parallel to this, MaxSAT and #SAT (counting satisfying assignments) solvers are seeing active development for probabilistic inference and optimization workloads. The annual SAT Competition continues to drive incremental improvements in heuristics and preprocessing.

Category Theory in Logic✓ reviewed

Logic · Category Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Categorical logic — sometimes called categorical semantics or categorical proof theory — is the systematic translation of logical syntax and semantics into the language of category theory. The central insight, crystallised around 1970 by Lawvere and later extended by Seely, Lambek, and Scott, is a tripartite correspondence:

| Logic | Type Theory | Category Theory |

|---|---|---|

| Proposition | Type | Object |

| Proof | Term / Program | Morphism |

| Entailment $A \vdash B$ | Function $A \to B$ | Arrow $f : A \to B$ |

INFO: **Core Correspondence**
An interpretation of a logical theory $\mathbb{T}$ in a category $\mathcal{C}$ is a functor $F : \text{Syn}(\mathbb{T}) \to \mathcal{C}$, where $\text{Syn}(\mathbb{T})$ is the syntactic category of $\mathbb{T}$ — the category freely generated by the theory's types and terms.

This lifts logic out of set-theoretic semantics and into a structural, compositional framework. Conjunction becomes the categorical product $A \times B$, implication becomes the internal hom $A \Rightarrow B$ (right adjoint to $-\times A$), and universal quantification is realised as a right adjoint to substitution functors between slice categories.

Why It Matters

Foundational unification. The Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence unifies three seemingly disparate fields: propositional logic ↔ simply-typed lambda calculus ↔ cartesian closed categories (CCCs). This is not a metaphor — it is a precise equivalence of syntactic and categorical structure.

Programming language semantics. Denotational semantics for typed functional languages is essentially categorical logic applied. Monads (Kleisli triples) model computational effects; fibrations model dependent types; comonads model context-sensitivity and coeffects.

Topos theory. Every elementary topos carries an internal higher-order intuitionistic logic. This means geometric morphisms between toposes correspond to interpretations of that logic, making topos theory a generalisation of both algebraic geometry and formal logic simultaneously.

$$\text{Subobject classifier } \Omega : \quad \text{Sub}(A) \cong \mathcal{C}(A, \Omega)$$

This single object $\Omega$ encodes the truth-value algebra of the entire internal logic.

Key Details

  1. Fibrations over categories. Dependent type theories are most naturally modelled via Grothendieck fibrations $p : \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{B}$, where the base tracks contexts and the total category tracks types-in-context. The Beck–Chevalley condition ensures substitution commutes with quantifiers.
  1. Hyperdoctrines. Lawvere's hyperdoctrine is a functor $P : \mathcal{C}^{op} \to \textbf{Pos}$ (or into a suitable 2-category) assigning a poset of propositions to each context object. Adjoints to reindexing functors model $\exists$ and $\forall$.
  1. Linear logic and monoidal categories. Classical and linear logics require richer categorical structure: $\ast$-autonomous categories for classical linear logic, symmetric monoidal closed categories for intuitionistic linear logic. This captures resource sensitivity — the absence of weakening and contraction corresponds to dropping the diagonal $\Delta : A \to A \otimes A$.
  1. Modal logic and comonads. Necessity $\Box$ is modelled by a comonad $(\Box, \varepsilon, \delta)$; possibility $\Diamond$ by a monad. This bridges categorical logic with categorical semantics for effects in PL theory.
  1. Coherence and proof identity. The categorical perspective forces attention to proof identity — when are two proofs the same? In a CCC, this is the question of when two morphisms are equal, which connects to normalisation, confluence, and decidability.
TIP: **Non-obvious insight**
The internal language of a category is not just a translation tool — it lets you reason inside the category using familiar logical notation, then transfer theorems back. This is how topos theorists prove results about sheaves by writing ordinary mathematical arguments in the Mitchell–Bénabou language.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include homotopy type theory (HoTT), where $\infty$-groupoids replace sets as the categorical semantics for identity types — the Univalence Axiom becomes a theorem in $(\infty,1)$-topos models. Displayed categories and two-level type theory are emerging tools for formally working with fibrations inside proof assistants. Categorical semantics for graded and quantitative type theories (tracking resource usage with semiring annotations) is rapidly maturing, with connections to differential privacy and cost analysis.

Modal Logic✓ reviewed

Logic · Logic  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Modal logic extends classical propositional or predicate logic with unary operators for necessity ($\Box$) and possibility ($\Diamond$), allowing reasoning about statements qualified by mode — what must be true vs. what could be true.

The two primitive operators are dual:

$$\Diamond P \equiv \neg\Box\neg P$$

That is, "$P$ is possible" iff "it is not necessary that $\neg P$." The semantics are given by Kripke frames — a pair $(W, R)$ where $W$ is a set of possible worlds and $R \subseteq W \times W$ is an accessibility relation:

$$\mathcal{M}, w \models \Box P \iff \forall v\,(wRv \Rightarrow \mathcal{M}, v \models P)$$

$$\mathcal{M}, w \models \Diamond P \iff \exists v\,(wRv \wedge \mathcal{M}, v \models P)$$

Truth is world-relative. A formula valid in all worlds of all models is a modal tautology; the target is identifying which axiom systems characterize which frame conditions.

Why It Matters

Modal logic is the backbone of several critical reasoning frameworks:

  • Epistemology (epistemic logic): $\Box P$ means "agent knows $P$." The axiom $\Box P \to P$ (knowledge implies truth) distinguishes knowledge from mere belief.
  • Deontic logic: $\Box P$ reads "it is obligatory that $P$." Here $\Box P \to P$ fails — obligations can go unmet.
  • Temporal logic: $\Box P$ becomes "always $P$"; $\Diamond P$ becomes "eventually $P$." This underpins formal verification (model checking in hardware/software).
  • Provability logic (GL): $\Box P$ means "$P$ is provable in PA," connecting to Gödel incompleteness.
  • Computer science: program correctness, type theory, and process calculi all draw on modal reasoning.
TIP: The Frame Correspondence Theorem
Restrictions on $R$ correspond precisely to modal axioms. Reflexivity ($wRw$) gives T ($\Box P \to P$); transitivity gives 4 ($\Box P \to \Box\Box P$); symmetry gives B ($P \to \Box\Diamond P$). This makes the axiom-frame correspondence a classification machine — you can read off semantic properties from syntactic axioms and vice versa.

Key Details

1. Hierarchy of normal modal logics

The base system K adds just the distribution axiom $\Box(P \to Q) \to (\Box P \to \Box Q)$ plus necessitation ($\vdash P \Rightarrow\, \vdash \Box P$). Richer systems stack additional axioms: K → T → S4 → S5, each corresponding to stronger frame conditions (reflexive, transitive, equivalence relation).

2. Soundness and completeness

Every normal modal logic is sound w.r.t. its class of frames. Completeness is harder — Kripke completeness fails for some logics (e.g., certain intermediate logics). Canonical model constructions are the standard completeness proof technique.

3. Bisimulation invariance

Modal formulas are exactly the bisimulation-invariant fragment of first-order logic (van Benthem's theorem). This places modal logic at a sweet spot: expressively bounded, yet decidable and tractable.

4. Multimodal and graded operators

Extensions allow multiple accessibility relations ($[a], \langle a \rangle$ per agent in epistemic logic) or counted quantification ($\Diamond_{\geq k} P$: "at least $k$ accessible worlds satisfy $P$"). Description logics used in OWL/semantic web are essentially multimodal.

5. Neighborhood semantics

An alternative to Kripke frames using neighborhood functions $N: W \to \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{P}(W))$. This generalizes the relational semantics and captures non-normal modal logics (where the necessitation rule fails), relevant for modeling non-ideal agents.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research threads include:

  • Dynamic epistemic logic (DEL): modeling how knowledge changes after public/private announcements — central to social epistemology and multi-agent AI.
  • Hyperintensional logics: going beyond possible-worlds by distinguishing necessarily equivalent propositions (relevant for reasoning about aboutness, belief in contradictions).
  • Provability and set-theoretic applications: modal logic over multiverse set theory (forcing as a modal operator), explored by Hamkins and colleagues.
  • Coalgebraic modal logic: unifying modal semantics via coalgebras, yielding uniform completeness results for broad classes of logics.
  • LLM-agent epistemic modeling: as multi-agent systems proliferate, formalizing agent belief, intention, and strategic deception (cf. research on public vs. off-the-record LLM divergence) pushes DEL toward richer, possibly non-idealized agent models.

Proof Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Proof theory treats proofs as first-class mathematical objects — syntactic entities that can be studied, classified, and transformed using mathematical techniques. Rather than asking "is this statement true?" (the model-theoretic question), proof theory asks "what is the structure of the derivation that establishes it?"

INFO: Formal Definition
A proof in a formal system $\mathcal{S}$ is a finite, inductively-defined data structure (tree, sequence, or DAG) whose leaves are axioms of $\mathcal{S}$ and whose internal nodes correspond to applications of inference rules. A proof of $\varphi$ is a witness that $\mathcal{S} \vdash \varphi$.

The central syntactic/semantic duality:

$$\underbrace{\mathcal{S} \vdash \varphi}_{\text{proof theory (syntax)}} \qquad \longleftrightarrow \qquad \underbrace{\mathcal{M} \models \varphi}_{\text{model theory (semantics)}}$$

Soundness and completeness theorems (e.g., Gödel's completeness theorem for FOL) are precisely what connects these two columns.

Why It Matters

Proof theory sits at the intersection of foundations, computation, and meaning:

  • Foundations of mathematics: Hilbert's program sought to secure all of mathematics via finitary proofs. Gödel's incompleteness theorems refuted the program's maximalist goals but sharpened the questions enormously.
  • Curry–Howard correspondence: Proofs in intuitionistic logic are identical to programs in typed lambda calculi — a fact with sweeping consequences for programming language theory, dependent types, and verified software.
  • Proof assistants: Coq, Lean, Agda, and Isabelle are direct applications; the type-theoretic foundations they rely on are proof-theoretic in nature.
  • Ordinal analysis: Assigns a "proof-theoretic ordinal" $|T|$ to a theory $T$, measuring its consistency strength. E.g., $|\mathsf{PA}| = \varepsilon_0$.
TIP: The Curry–Howard Iceberg
The surface reading is "proofs ↔ programs, propositions ↔ types." The deeper consequence is that proof normalization = program execution. Cut-elimination in sequent calculus corresponds to $\beta$-reduction in the $\lambda$-calculus. This makes proof theory a theory of computation, not merely of deduction.

Key Details

  1. Sequent calculus & natural deduction are the two dominant proof systems. Gentzen's Hauptsatz (cut-elimination theorem, 1935) is the field's cornerstone result: every proof using the "cut" rule (modus ponens generalized) can be transformed into a cut-free proof, establishing consistency of first-order arithmetic by transfinite induction up to $\varepsilon_0$.
  1. Ordinal analysis calibrates theories by the least ordinal whose well-foundedness cannot be proved inside the theory. The ladder runs: $\mathsf{PRA} < \mathsf{PA}$ (at $\varepsilon_0$) $< \mathsf{ATR}_0$ (at $\Gamma_0$, the Feferman–Schütte ordinal) $< \Pi^1_1\text{-}\mathsf{CA}_0 \cdots$
  1. Reverse mathematics inverts the usual direction: given a theorem, find the weakest axiomatic system that proves it. Most theorems of classical analysis fall into one of five canonical subsystems ($\mathsf{RCA}_0, \mathsf{WKL}_0, \mathsf{ACA}_0, \mathsf{ATR}_0, \Pi^1_1\text{-}\mathsf{CA}_0$).
  1. Proof complexity asks about the size of shortest proofs. The $\mathsf{P}$ vs. $\mathsf{NP}$ problem is equivalent to asking whether tautologies have polynomial-size proofs in some propositional proof system (Cook–Reckhow theorem). Extended Frege systems remain a major open battleground.
  1. Proof mining (Kohlenbach) extracts computational content — explicit witnesses and bounds — from non-constructive proofs, yielding quantitative results in analysis and ergodic theory without reproving results from scratch.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) and the Univalent Foundations program (Voevodsky), which enriches proof theory with homotopy-theoretic structure; proofs of equality become paths in a space.
  • Deep inference (Guglielmi's calculus of structures) allows inference rules to apply inside any formula context, breaking the asymmetry of sequent calculus and enabling more fine-grained proof-complexity analyses.
  • Automated theorem proving via SAT/SMT solvers and interactive proof assistants, with large formal libraries (Mathlib in Lean 4) pushing the boundary of what machine-checked mathematics covers.
  • Proof-theoretic semantics (Prawitz, Dummett) grounds meaning in proof conditions rather than truth conditions — a philosophical alternative to Tarskian model theory with implications for anti-realist semantics.
