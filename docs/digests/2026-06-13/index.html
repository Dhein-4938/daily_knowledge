Zero-Knowledge Proofs✓ reviewed

Tech · Cryptography  ·  ~5 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A zero-knowledge proof (ZKP) is a cryptographic protocol between a prover $P$ and a verifier $V$ where $P$ convinces $V$ that a statement $x \in L$ (some language $L$) is true, without revealing why it is true — i.e., without leaking the witness $w$ such that $(x, w) \in R$ for the relation $R$ defining $L$.

Formally, a ZKP system for relation $R$ must satisfy three properties:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\textbf{Completeness:} && (x,w)\in R \Rightarrow \Pr[V \text{ accepts}] = 1 \\ &\textbf{Soundness:} && x\notin L \Rightarrow \Pr[V \text{ accepts}] \leq \epsilon_s \text{ (negligible)} \\ &\textbf{Zero-Knowledge:} && \exists \text{ PPT simulator } S : \{S(x)\} \approx_c \{\text{view}_V(x,w)\} \end{aligned} $$

The zero-knowledge property is operationalized via a simulator: the verifier's view of a real interaction is computationally indistinguishable from a simulated transcript $S(x)$ produced without the witness. If indistinguishability is perfect (not just computational), the proof is perfect zero-knowledge; statistical closeness yields statistical zero-knowledge.

Why It Matters

ZKPs decouple knowledge from proof of knowledge — a conceptual inversion that propagates across many domains:

  • Blockchain / Layer 2 scaling: zk-SNARKs (Succinct Non-interactive ARguments of Knowledge) allow a chain to verify compressed proofs of off-chain computation (e.g., zkEVM rollups), reducing on-chain cost by orders of magnitude.
  • Privacy-preserving authentication: prove you know a password hash preimage, or that your age $\geq 18$, without revealing the actual value.
  • Verifiable computation: outsource expensive computation; receive a proof that the result is correct without re-executing.
  • Regulatory compliance: a financial institution can prove solvency to a regulator without exposing individual account balances.
TIP: Proof of non-knowledge is impossible
A subtle point: ZKPs prove possession of a witness, not just truth of a statement. A statement that is "trivially true" (e.g., a BPP decision) can be proven without any witness — but the non-triviality of ZKPs lies precisely in proving witness possession while hiding it.

Key Details

1. Interactive vs. Non-Interactive

Classic ZKPs (e.g., the Goldreich–Micali–Wigderson graph 3-coloring protocol) require multiple rounds of challenge–response. The Fiat–Shamir heuristic collapses interaction into a single message by replacing the verifier's random challenges with a hash of the prover's commitment — turning an interactive proof into a NIZK (non-interactive ZK) in the random oracle model.

2. The Simulator Paradox

The simulator $S$ produces convincing transcripts without the witness, which seems to contradict soundness. The resolution: $S$ is allowed to run in a different execution environment — typically by choosing the verifier's challenges before computing commitments (rewinding the tape). Soundness and simulation live in different worlds.

3. SNARKs and the CRS

Modern ZKPs (Groth16, PLONK, STARKs) are succinct: proof size is $O(1)$ or $O(\log n)$ regardless of circuit depth. SNARKs require a Common Reference String (CRS) generated in a trusted setup ceremony — a toxic waste problem where the ceremony randomness must be destroyed. STARKs (Scalable Transparent ARguments of Knowledge) avoid trusted setup entirely, using hash functions, at the cost of larger proofs.

4. Proof Systems Landscape

| System | Setup | Proof Size | Prover Cost |

|--------|-------|------------|-------------|

| Groth16 | Trusted | ~200 bytes | $O(n \log n)$ |

| PLONK | Universal trusted | ~400 bytes | $O(n \log n)$ |

| STARKs | Transparent | $O(\log^2 n)$ | $O(n \log n)$ |

| Bulletproofs | Transparent | $O(\log n)$ | $O(n)$ |

5. Knowledge Soundness vs. Plain Soundness

A proof of knowledge requires an efficient knowledge extractor $E$: given black-box access to any prover $P^*$ that convinces $V$, $E$ can extract the witness $w$. This is strictly stronger than plain soundness and is required for applications like digital signatures and credential systems.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research frontiers as of mid-2026:

  • Recursive proof composition: proofs that verify other proofs (Nova, SuperNova), enabling incrementally verifiable computation (IVC) for arbitrary-length computations.
  • zkVM architectures: proving execution of a full virtual machine (RISC-V, EVM) inside a ZK circuit — e.g., SP1, RISC Zero — making ZK accessible without custom circuit design.
  • Hardware acceleration: ZK prover cost remains the bottleneck; FPGAs and ASICs targeting MSM (Multi-Scalar Multiplication) and NTT (Number Theoretic Transform) operations are an active industrial push.
  • Post-quantum ZK: PLONK and Groth16 rely on elliptic curve pairings, which are broken by Shor's algorithm. Lattice-based ZK (e.g., Ligero, Aurora) offer quantum resistance but with larger proofs.
  • Formal verification: proving the provers themselves are correct (e.g., using Lean or Coq) to close the gap between cryptographic security proofs and implementation bugs.

Homomorphic Encryption✓ reviewed

Tech · Cryptography  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO
Homomorphic Encryption (HE) is an encryption scheme that permits arbitrary computations on ciphertexts such that the result, when decrypted, equals the result of the same computation on the plaintexts — without ever exposing the underlying data.

Formally, given a homomorphic encryption scheme $\mathcal{E}$, for plaintexts $m_1, m_2$ and an operation $\circ$:

$$\mathcal{D}\!\left(\mathcal{E}(m_1) \star \mathcal{E}(m_2)\right) = m_1 \circ m_2$$

where $\star$ is the corresponding ciphertext-space operation. The key taxonomy:

| Scheme | Supported Operations | Depth |

|---|---|---|

| Partially HE (PHE) | One operation (+ or ×) | Unlimited |

| Somewhat HE (SHE) | Both + and × | Bounded |

| Fully HE (FHE) | Any boolean/arithmetic circuit | Unlimited |

FHE is the holy grail — it follows from SHE via Craig Gentry's bootstrapping construction (2009), which homomorphically evaluates the decryption circuit to "refresh" noisy ciphertexts.

Why It Matters

The core value proposition: data can remain encrypted even during computation. This dissolves the traditional tension between utility and privacy in third-party processing.

Key application domains:

  • Privacy-preserving ML inference — run a neural network over an encrypted patient record; the server learns nothing about the patient, the patient learns nothing about the model weights.
  • Secure cloud outsourcing — store and process sensitive data in untrusted environments without decrypting it at rest or in transit.
  • Private information retrieval — query a database without revealing which record you're fetching.
  • Multi-party computation — complement to MPC protocols, often enabling simpler single-server designs.
TIP
HE addresses a threat model that TLS and AES-at-rest do not: compromise of the processing environment itself. Even a fully privileged attacker on the compute node cannot extract plaintext. This is categorically different from "encryption in transit/at rest."

Key Details

  1. Noise growth is fundamental. Modern FHE schemes (BGV, BFV, CKKS, TFHE) all work in the Learning With Errors (LWE) or Ring-LWE setting. Each operation adds noise; multiplication is particularly costly. Without bootstrapping, circuits are depth-limited.
  1. CKKS trades exactness for efficiency. The CKKS scheme (Cheon-Kim-Kim-Song, 2017) supports approximate arithmetic on real/complex numbers. It's the dominant choice for ML workloads where small rounding errors are tolerable — think encrypted dot products for transformer inference.
  1. Bootstrapping overhead is enormous. A single bootstrapping operation can take seconds on modern hardware and increase ciphertext size by orders of magnitude. Reducing this cost is the central engineering challenge for practical FHE.
  1. Side-channels remain a real threat. HE protects plaintext from the processor, but metadata leaks persist — access patterns, timing, communication volume. As the Wikipedia source notes, even encrypted photo scanning reveals that photos were transmitted. HE is not a silver bullet against traffic analysis.
  1. Parameter selection is security-critical. The security of LWE-based schemes depends on choosing ring dimension $n$ and modulus $q$ carefully. The Homomorphic Encryption Security Standard (HomEncStd) provides community-vetted parameter tables analogous to NIST key-size recommendations.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts as of mid-2026:

  • Hardware acceleration — dedicated FHE ASICs (e.g., Intel HERACLES, MIT F1) targeting sub-millisecond bootstrapping; GPU-accelerated NTT (Number Theoretic Transform) pipelines.
  • Compiler toolchains — projects like Concrete (Zama), HEIR (Google), and OpenFHE abstract scheme selection and parameter tuning away from the developer.
  • Transciphering / hybrid schemes — combining FHE with symmetric primitives (e.g., AES-in-FHE) to reduce client-side encryption cost.
  • FHE for LLM inference — proving that transformer forward passes can execute on encrypted tokens; latency is currently 100–1000× slower than plaintext.

Reinforcement Learning✓ reviewed

Tech · Machine Learning  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is the study of how an agent learns a policy $\pi: \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{A}$ by interacting with an environment to maximize cumulative reward. Unlike supervised learning, no labeled input-output pairs are provided — feedback is scalar, delayed, and often sparse.

The canonical formalism is the Markov Decision Process (MDP), defined by the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma)$:

$$G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1}, \quad \gamma \in [0, 1)$$

The agent seeks a policy $\pi^*$ that maximizes the expected return $\mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t]$. The Bellman optimality equation captures the recursive structure:

$$Q^*(s, a) = \mathbb{E}\left[R_{t+1} + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s', a') \mid s, a\right]$$

Why It Matters

RL is the closest computational analogue to how biological organisms learn from consequences. It underpins:

  • Game-playing AI (AlphaGo, AlphaZero, OpenAI Five)
  • Robotics — sim-to-real transfer for dexterous manipulation
  • LLM alignment — RLHF and GRPO are RL at their core
  • Operations research — scheduling, inventory control, routing
  • Neuroscience — dopamine circuits mirror temporal difference (TD) learning
TIP: The RL–Optimal Control Bridge
Classical optimal control (e.g., LQR, MPC) assumes a known dynamics model $P$. RL relaxes this: it learns from sampled transitions. This makes RL applicable wherever simulation or real interaction is cheaper than deriving an analytical model — which is most of the interesting world.

Key Details

1. Exploration–Exploitation Dilemma

The agent must balance exploiting known-good actions vs. exploring to reduce uncertainty. Formal treatments include UCB (Upper Confidence Bound), Thompson Sampling, and the $\epsilon$-greedy heuristic. In continuous/deep RL, this remains largely heuristic.

2. Value-Based vs. Policy Gradient Methods

  • Value-based (DQN, Q-learning): learn $Q^\pi$ implicitly, derive policy greedily. Sample-efficient but biased in continuous action spaces.
  • Policy gradient (REINFORCE, PPO, GRPO): directly optimize $\nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}[G_t]$. Unbiased but high-variance. Actor-critic methods blend both.

3. Credit Assignment Problem

Rewards are often delayed by hundreds of steps. Attributing which earlier actions caused a later reward is non-trivial — temporal difference learning addresses this by bootstrapping from value estimates rather than waiting for episode termination.

4. The Deadly Triad

Combining function approximation + bootstrapping + off-policy learning can cause instability or divergence. DQN circumvents this with experience replay and target networks; other approaches (e.g., distributional RL, constrained updates in PPO) add further stabilizers.

5. Reward Hacking

An agent optimizing a proxy reward $\hat{R}$ can find unintended solutions that score well on $\hat{R}$ while violating the true objective. This is Goodhart's Law in action and is a central concern in AI alignment.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers as of mid-2026:

  • RL Fine-Tuning of LLMs: GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) has displaced PPO in many LLM post-training pipelines due to lower memory overhead. Papers like RA-RFT extend this with reasoning-aware retrieval, boosting AIME 2025 accuracy by 7+ points over vanilla GRPO.
  • Agentic / Multi-Step RL: InterleaveThinker applies step-wise GRPO rewards across 25+ generator calls in a trajectory — highlighting that RL signal need not be end-to-end to guide long-horizon tasks.
  • Sim-to-Real for Dexterous Robots: Mana demonstrates zero-shot transfer for articulated tool manipulation via coarse-to-fine RL pipelines, reducing human annotation to under a minute per tool.
  • Flow Matching + RL: FRS shows that flow-matching generalist policies can be steered via latent-space reversal, bootstrapping RL with semantic priors where standard RL fails entirely.
  • World Action Models: Representation-centric tokenization (RepWAM) is emerging as a way to jointly model future visual states and latent actions — potentially unifying prediction and control.

Quantum Computing✓ reviewed

Tech · Quantum Computing  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A quantum computer exploits quantum mechanical phenomena — principally superposition, entanglement, and wave interference — to perform computation. Its fundamental unit is the qubit, which generalises the classical bit by existing in a linear combination of basis states until measured.

A qubit's state lives in a two-dimensional complex Hilbert space:

$$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle, \quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$$

where $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}$. Measuring collapses $|\psi\rangle$ to $|0\rangle$ with probability $|\alpha|^2$ or $|1\rangle$ with probability $|\beta|^2$. An $n$-qubit register spans $2^n$ basis states simultaneously — exponential state space from linear physical resources.

The computational leverage comes not from parallelism per se, but from amplitude amplification: quantum algorithms are designed so interference constructively reinforces paths leading to correct answers and destructively cancels wrong ones. This is the essence of Grover's and Shor's algorithms alike.

Why It Matters

Cryptographic threat. Shor's algorithm factors integers in polynomial time $O((\log N)^3)$, compared to the best classical sub-exponential algorithms. This would break RSA and elliptic-curve cryptography, driving the NIST post-quantum standardisation effort.

Simulation. Feynman's original motivation: simulating quantum chemistry and materials exactly is classically intractable ($2^N$ Hilbert space dimension), but native to a quantum device. Applications range from drug discovery to catalyst design.

Optimisation & ML. Grover's algorithm yields a quadratic speedup for unstructured search; quantum approaches to linear algebra (HHL) promise potential speedups in machine learning pipelines, though practical overhead is debated.

TIP: Connection to Information Theory
Quantum computing forces a rethink of Shannon's classical information theory. Holevo's theorem bounds how much classical information a qubit can carry (still only 1 bit per qubit on measurement), separating quantum information capacity from the computational advantage — a subtle distinction often glossed over in popular accounts.

Key Details

  1. Decoherence is the core engineering problem. Qubits must remain isolated from their environment long enough to complete a computation. Environmental noise entangles the qubit with external degrees of freedom, corrupting the superposition. Coherence time $T_2$ is the critical figure of merit.
  1. Error correction overhead is severe. Quantum error correction (e.g., the surface code) requires $O(1000)$ physical qubits per logical qubit to reach fault-tolerant thresholds — meaning a cryptographically relevant attack on RSA-2048 might require millions of physical qubits, far beyond current hardware.
  1. Gate fidelity and threshold theorem. The fault-tolerance threshold theorem guarantees scalable computation if physical error rates fall below a hardware-dependent threshold (~$10^{-3}$ for surface codes). Google's Willow chip (Dec 2024) achieved below-threshold error correction, a landmark 30 years in the making.
  1. Competing hardware modalities have distinct trade-off profiles:
  • Superconducting qubits — fast gates ($\sim$ns), but cryogenic cooling to $\sim$15 mK required.
  • Trapped ions — high fidelity, long coherence, but slower gate speeds ($\sim$ms) and limited connectivity.
  • Photonic, neutral atom, spin qubits — each sacrificing different axes of the speed/coherence/connectivity triangle.
  1. Quantum advantage ≠ quantum utility. Current demonstrations of "quantum supremacy" involve sampling problems (e.g., random circuit sampling) with no known classical near-term application. Practical utility — solving useful problems faster — remains an open engineering and algorithmic challenge.
WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Hardware capabilities are advancing rapidly. Qubit counts, coherence times, and error rates quoted in 2025–2026 literature may be superseded. Verify against current hardware roadmaps before drawing engineering conclusions.

Current State

The field sits at a critical inflection post-Willow. Key active research threads:

  • Logical qubit demonstrations at scale — moving from below-threshold physical error rates to actual logical qubit operations with $10^{-6}$–$10^{-8}$ error rates needed for Shor's.
  • Nonlocality without entanglement (NLWE): recent work (Lee & Bae, 2026) shows semi-device-independent certification of global quantum measurements using separable states, relevant to quantum key distribution and measurement-device-independent protocols.
  • Topological qubits (e.g., via quantum anomalous Hall states, Majorana modes) offer potentially hardware-protected coherence — active in graphene moiré systems with engineered spin-orbit coupling.
  • Post-quantum cryptography standardisation is in parallel deployment; NIST finalised CRYSTALS-Kyber and Dilithium in 2024.
  • Global government investment crossed $10 billion by April 2025, accelerating the race among the US, China, and EU.
