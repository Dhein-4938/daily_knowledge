Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems✓ reviewed

Math · Logic  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: First Incompleteness Theorem
Any consistent formal system $\mathcal{F}$ capable of expressing elementary arithmetic contains a sentence $G_\mathcal{F}$ such that neither $G_\mathcal{F}$ nor $\neg G_\mathcal{F}$ is provable in $\mathcal{F}$.
INFO: Second Incompleteness Theorem
No consistent system $\mathcal{F}$ (satisfying the above conditions) can prove its own consistency: $\mathcal{F} \nvdash \text{Con}(\mathcal{F})$.

The theorems apply to any effective, consistent, sufficiently expressive formal system — concretely, any system that can encode Robinson arithmetic Q (a fragment weaker than Peano arithmetic). "Effective" means its axioms are recursively enumerable; you can write an algorithm to enumerate theorems.

The proof method is Gödel numbering: encode every formula and proof as a natural number, then use the diagonal lemma to construct a self-referential sentence $G$ that asserts its own unprovability:

$$G \equiv \neg \exists p \; \text{Proof}_\mathcal{F}(p, \ulcorner G \urcorner)$$

where $\ulcorner G \urcorner$ is the Gödel number of $G$. If $G$ were provable, the system would be inconsistent; if $\neg G$ were provable, the system would be $\omega$-inconsistent (under the standard reading). In consistent systems, $G$ is therefore true but unprovable.

Why It Matters

For mathematics: Hilbert's program — find a finite, complete, consistent axiomatization of all mathematics — is provably impossible. Completeness and consistency cannot coexist for any system rich enough to do arithmetic.

For computer science: The theorems are deeply entangled with computability. The undecidability of the halting problem (Turing, 1936) and the unsolvability of the Entscheidungsproblem (Church, 1936) are essentially the same phenomenon viewed through a different lens. A non-halting program is the computational analogue of an unprovable statement.

For philosophy: The theorems constrain mathematical Platonism, formalism, and logicism in different ways. They don't say mathematics is broken — they say no single formal net can catch all mathematical truth.

TIP: Incompleteness ≠ Inconsistency
A common misreading conflates "unprovable" with "false." $G_\mathcal{F}$ is true in the standard model $\mathbb{N}$ — it's only unprovable within $\mathcal{F}$. Moving to a stronger system $\mathcal{F}'$ proves $G_\mathcal{F}$, but then $G_{\mathcal{F}'}$ escapes that system. The hierarchy never closes.

Key Details

  1. Conditions are essential. The theorems require consistency and $\omega$-consistency (first theorem, original proof) or just consistency (Rosser's 1936 strengthening via the Rosser trick). Inconsistent systems prove everything — they're trivially "complete."
  1. The arithmetical hierarchy. $G_\mathcal{F}$ sits at the $\Pi^0_1$ level: it has the form $\forall n \; P(n)$ where $P$ is decidable. This means its truth is verifiable "from outside" by checking each $n$, but no finite proof exists inside $\mathcal{F}$.
  1. Löb's theorem refines the second: $\mathcal{F} \vdash \Box\varphi \to \varphi$ only if $\mathcal{F} \vdash \varphi$. A system can only "trust its own provability" for statements it already proves outright — there's no non-trivial bootstrapping of self-trust.
  1. Non-standard models. Incomplete systems don't just have one model. There exist non-standard models of arithmetic where $G_\mathcal{F}$ is false — models containing "infinite integers" that encode fake proofs. Incompleteness is a statement about the interplay between syntax and the intended semantics.
  1. Proof-theoretic ordinals. Each consistent extension of PA that proves its own totality of some fast-growing function can be calibrated by an ordinal. PA corresponds to $\varepsilon_0$; stronger systems reach further into the Cantor normal form hierarchy. Incompleteness manifests as an ordinal ceiling.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research areas rooted in these theorems include:

  • Reverse mathematics: classifying which axioms are necessary for specific theorems (Simpson's program). Many classical results turn out equivalent to $\text{WKL}_0$ or $\text{ACA}_0$ over a weak base theory.
  • Proof complexity: measuring the length of proofs. Even for provable statements, proofs in weak systems can be astronomically long — a quantitative shadow of incompleteness (see Cook–Reckhow framework).
  • Ordinal analysis: Gentzen proved Con(PA) using transfinite induction up to $\varepsilon_0$, a result that transcends PA. Extending this to stronger systems (e.g., $\Pi^1_2$-CA) remains an open frontier.
  • Mechanised proof assistants (Coq, Lean, Isabelle) have fully formalised Gödel's theorems, most notably via Paulson's 2015 Isabelle proof and the Fleuriot/Harrison lineage.

  1. Epistemic ceiling or navigational tool? Gödel sentences are pathological constructs, not naturally arising mathematical questions — yet the independence of the Continuum Hypothesis from ZFC feels genuinely mathematical. Where is the boundary between "Gödelian artifact" and "deep undecidability"?
  1. Does incompleteness threaten the working mathematician? Almost all theorems of classical analysis, algebra, and combinatorics are provable in ZFC or weaker systems.   1. Self-reference and AI systems: Neural networks and large language models are formal computational systems. P vs NP Problem✓ reviewed

Math · Complexity Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Formal Definition
P is the class of decision problems solvable by a deterministic Turing machine in $O(n^k)$ time for some constant $k$. NP is the class solvable by a nondeterministic Turing machine in polynomial time — equivalently, problems whose solutions can be verified in polynomial time by a deterministic machine.

The central question: does $\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}$?

Formally, let $L$ be a decision problem (a language over $\{0,1\}^*$). Then:

$$L \in \mathrm{NP} \iff \exists \text{ poly-time verifier } V \text{ s.t. } x \in L \Leftrightarrow \exists w,\, |w| \leq \mathrm{poly}(|x|),\, V(x, w) = 1$$

where $w$ is a certificate (witness). P $\subseteq$ NP trivially — any polynomial solver serves as its own verifier. The open question is whether the containment is strict.

The widely-held belief is $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$, though no proof exists. It is one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems ($1,000,000 prize from the Clay Mathematics Institute).

Why It Matters

The stakes extend far beyond theoretical computer science:

  • Cryptography: Nearly all modern cryptographic primitives (RSA, elliptic-curve, hash-based schemes) derive security from the presumed hardness of NP problems like integer factorization and discrete log. If $\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}$, these collapse. This would break TLS, PGP, and essentially all public-key infrastructure overnight.
  • Optimization & AI: SAT solvers, scheduling, protein folding (NP-hard), and combinatorial optimization underpin enormous practical and scientific domains. Polynomial-time solutions would be transformative.
  • Mathematics itself: Proof verification is in NP (given a proof, checking it is poly-time). A $\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}$ result would imply that finding proofs is no harder than checking them — effectively automating mathematical discovery.
  • Economics & Game Theory: Nash equilibrium computation, mechanism design, and auction theory all bump against NP-hardness barriers.
TIP: The Asymmetry Insight
The problem captures a deep philosophical asymmetry: recognizing a solution versus finding one. This maps onto human experience — it is far easier to verify a crossword solution than to solve it from scratch. P vs NP asks whether this subjective difficulty gap is mathematically real.

Key Details

  1. NP-completeness: A problem $Q$ is NP-complete if $Q \in \mathrm{NP}$ and every problem in NP poly-time reduces to $Q$. Cook (1971) and Levin (1973) independently showed SAT is NP-complete. Karp then identified 21 canonical NP-complete problems. NP-complete problems are the "hardest" in NP — solving any one in poly-time implies $\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}$.
  1. The complexity landscape: The picture is richer than just P and NP:

$$\mathrm{P} \subseteq \mathrm{NP} \cap \mathrm{co\text{-}NP} \subseteq \mathrm{NP} \subseteq \mathrm{PSPACE} \subseteq \mathrm{EXP}$$

Integer factorization sits in $\mathrm{NP} \cap \mathrm{co\text{-}NP}$ but is not known to be NP-complete — suggesting it may occupy an intermediate zone even if $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$.

  1. Relativization barrier: Baker, Gill & Solovay (1975) showed there exist oracles $A$, $B$ such that $\mathrm{P}^A = \mathrm{NP}^A$ and $\mathrm{P}^B \neq \mathrm{NP}^B$. Any proof technique that relativizes (holds with any oracle) cannot resolve P vs NP — ruling out large swaths of classical proof strategies.
  1. Natural proofs barrier: Razborov & Rudich (1994) showed that "natural" combinatorial circuit lower-bound arguments cannot separate P from NP unless strong pseudorandom generators don't exist. This blocks another major proof avenue.
  1. Algebraization: Aaronson & Wigderson (2009) extended the oracle barrier to algebraic methods, further constraining viable proof strategies. The known barriers — relativization, natural proofs, algebraization — together suggest any resolution will require fundamentally new mathematics.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research directions include:

  • Circuit complexity: Proving super-polynomial lower bounds for explicit problems in restricted circuit classes (e.g., $\mathrm{ACC}^0$). Ryan Williams made progress in 2010 by separating $\mathrm{NEXP}$ from $\mathrm{ACC}^0$, the first major circuit lower bound in decades.
  • Geometric complexity theory (GCT): Mulmuley & Sohoni's program uses algebraic geometry and representation theory to approach the problem via the permanent vs. determinant question — a closely related algebraic analogue.
  • Proof complexity: Connections between proof length in formal systems and circuit complexity may offer indirect handles on the problem.
  • Meta-complexity: Problems like MCSP (Minimum Circuit Size Problem) sit in a curious zone — likely not NP-complete under standard assumptions, yet hard — and studying them sheds light on the structure of NP itself.

The consensus remains $\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}$, but no serious candidate proof has survived peer scrutiny. Dozens of claimed proofs have been submitted and refuted.

  1. If $\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}$ but the polynomial degree were $n^{10^6}$, the result would be theoretically explosive but practically irrelevant. How should complexity theory weight "practical" vs. "asymptotic" hardness, and does this tension undermine the problem's centrality?
  1. The fact that proof verification is in NP but proof discovery may not be in P suggests a deep link between P vs NP and the limits of formal mathematical reasoning. Does this imply any form of mathematical incompleteness beyond Gödel's?

Information Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Information Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Information theory is the mathematical framework for quantifying, storing, and transmitting information. Founded by Claude Shannon in his landmark 1948 paper A Mathematical Theory of Communication, it provides rigorous answers to: how much information does a message carry? and what is the fundamental limit of lossless compression or reliable transmission?

INFO: Shannon Entropy
For a discrete random variable $X$ with probability mass function $p(x)$, the Shannon entropy is: $$H(X) = -\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) \log_2 p(x) \quad \text{(bits)}$$ Entropy measures the average surprise — or equivalently, the minimum average code length needed to represent outcomes of $X$.

For a fair coin: $H(X) = -2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\log_2\frac{1}{2} = 1$ bit. A biased coin with $p = 0.99$ yields $H \approx 0.081$ bits — nearly no surprise.

Why It Matters

Information theory underlies virtually all of modern digital infrastructure:

  • Data compression (ZIP, JPEG, MP3) is bounded below by Shannon's source coding theorem: you cannot losslessly compress below $H(X)$ bits per symbol on average
  • Error correction (DSL, satellite comms, QR codes) is enabled by the noisy-channel coding theorem: reliable transmission is achievable at any rate below the channel capacity $C$
  • Machine learning: cross-entropy loss, KL divergence, mutual information — all are information-theoretic quantities used daily in training and evaluation
  • Applications extend to cryptography, neuroscience, statistical mechanics, and black hole physics
TIP: Deep Connection to Thermodynamics
Shannon entropy is formally identical to Boltzmann–Gibbs entropy $S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i$ up to a constant. This is not coincidence — Landauer's principle shows that erasing one bit of information dissipates at least $k_B T \ln 2$ joules of energy. Information has thermodynamic weight.

Key Details

1. Mutual Information

$$I(X; Y) = H(X) - H(X \mid Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log_2 \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)}$$

Measures how much knowing $Y$ reduces uncertainty about $X$. It is symmetric: $I(X;Y) = I(Y;X)$.

2. Channel Capacity

For a channel with input $X$, output $Y$, and transition probabilities $p(y|x)$:

$$C = \max_{p(x)} I(X; Y)$$

For a Gaussian channel with signal power $P$ and noise variance $N$:

$$C = \frac{1}{2}\log_2\!\left(1 + \frac{P}{N}\right) \text{ bits/channel use}$$

This is the Shannon–Hartley theorem — the theoretical ceiling no coding scheme can exceed.

3. KL Divergence (Relative Entropy)

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| Q) = \sum_x p(x) \log_2 \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \geq 0$$

Asymmetric "distance" between distributions. Zero iff $P = Q$. Fundamental in Bayesian inference, variational methods, and hypothesis testing.

4. Data Processing Inequality

For a Markov chain $X \to Y \to Z$: $I(X;Z) \leq I(X;Y)$. Processing cannot increase information — a hard bound on what any downstream computation can recover.

5. Differential Entropy

For continuous distributions, entropy generalizes to $h(X) = -\int p(x)\log p(x)\,dx$, but loses some discrete-case properties (e.g., it can be negative). The Gaussian maximizes $h(X)$ for fixed variance.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Quantum information theory: replacing bits with qubits introduces von Neumann entropy $S(\rho) = -\text{tr}(\rho \log \rho)$, quantum channel capacity, and entanglement as an information resource
  • Deep learning theory: the information bottleneck principle (Tishby et al.) proposes that DNNs learn by compressing inputs while preserving label-relevant information — still contested empirically
  • One-shot and finite-blocklength regimes: classical theory assumes infinite block lengths; real systems require tight finite-$n$ bounds (e.g., the dispersion framework)
  • Implicit information in generative models: recent work (e.g., VEGA-3D, 2026) suggests video diffusion models implicitly encode dense 3D structural priors — a reminder that information-theoretic capacity extends beyond explicit encodings

Linear Algebra✓ reviewed

Math · Algebra  ·  ~5 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Linear algebra is the study of vector spaces, linear maps between them, and the algebraic structures (matrices, tensors) used to represent these objects concretely. The central objects are systems of linear equations $$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}, \quad A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n},\ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n,\ \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$$ and the abstract maps $T: V \to W$ satisfying $T(\alpha u + \beta v) = \alpha T(u) + \beta T(v)$ for all scalars $\alpha, \beta$ and vectors $u, v$.

A vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ is a set equipped with addition and scalar multiplication satisfying eight axioms (closure, associativity, distributivity, etc.). Linear algebra is the full theory of what you can say about a space using only these two operations — no notion of distance or angle is required unless you add an inner product.

The rank-nullity theorem is perhaps the cleanest encapsulation of structure:

$$\dim(\ker T) + \dim(\operatorname{im} T) = \dim V$$

Every linear map is completely characterized by what it collapses (kernel) and what it produces (image).

Why It Matters

Linear algebra is the computational skeleton of modern science and engineering. Its centrality comes from two complementary facts:

  1. Universality of linearization. The derivative of a smooth map $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ at a point $p$ is the Jacobian matrix $Df(p)$ — the best linear approximation to $f$ near $p$. Almost all continuous optimization, physics simulation, and differential geometry reduces locally to linear algebra.
  1. Computational tractability. Matrix operations admit efficient algorithms (Gaussian elimination runs in $O(n^3)$; sparse and structured matrices allow much faster routines). No other mathematical framework matches this combination of generality and computability.
TIP: The Spectral Lens
Eigendecomposition $A = Q\Lambda Q^{-1}$ transforms a linear map into a diagonal action on independent subspaces. This is the reason PCA, quantum mechanics (energy eigenstates), Google's PageRank, and stability analysis of ODEs all reduce to the same algebraic operation.

Applications span: numerical PDE solvers (discretization → linear system), quantum mechanics (observables are Hermitian operators on Hilbert space), ML (neural network layers are affine maps; backprop is a chain rule over Jacobians), computer graphics (rotations/projections as matrix groups), and cryptography (lattice-based schemes).

Key Details

1. The Four Fundamental Subspaces. Any $m \times n$ matrix $A$ defines four subspaces — column space $\operatorname{col}(A)$, null space $\operatorname{null}(A)$, row space $\operatorname{row}(A)$, left null space $\operatorname{null}(A^T)$ — satisfying orthogonality relations in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\mathbb{R}^m$ respectively. Understanding their geometry is the key to solving (or explaining the failure of) $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.

2. SVD as the Canonical Decomposition. The Singular Value Decomposition $A = U\Sigma V^T$ exists for every matrix (not just square or symmetric ones) and reveals the true geometric action: rotation $V^T$, axis-aligned scaling $\Sigma$, then rotation $U$. Pseudoinverse, low-rank approximation (Eckart–Young theorem), and condition number all derive from it.

3. Change of Basis is Conjugation. If $T$ has matrix $A$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$, then in basis $\mathcal{B}'$ it has matrix $P^{-1}AP$ where $P$ is the change-of-basis matrix. The intrinsic property is the eigenvalues (invariant under conjugation); the matrix form is an artifact of the chosen coordinates.

4. Over Arbitrary Fields. Linear algebra works over any field — $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{F}_p$, $\mathbb{C}$, even finite fields. Algebraically closed fields (like $\mathbb{C}$) guarantee that the characteristic polynomial splits completely, which is why complex eigenvalue theory is cleaner and why quantum mechanics lives in $\mathbb{C}$.

5. Infinite Dimensions Require Care. In infinite-dimensional spaces (function spaces, sequence spaces), bounded linear operators can behave wildly: the spectrum need not consist of eigenvalues; inverses may exist but be unbounded; self-adjoint operators can have continuous spectrum. This is the domain of Functional Analysis.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research areas include:

  • Randomized numerical linear algebra — sketching algorithms (e.g., random projections, leverage-score sampling) that solve least-squares and low-rank problems in sublinear passes over data, critical for large-scale ML and streaming settings.
  • Tensor decompositions — generalizations of SVD to higher-order arrays (CP, Tucker, tensor-train). Uniqueness properties differ sharply from matrices; identifiability is an open problem in general.
  • Linear algebra in ML interpretability — recent work (e.g., sparse autoencoders, linear probing in VLA models as of 2026-03) uses linear subspace analysis to decompose model representations, finding that features like motor programs and goal semantics occupy separable activation subspaces — a direct application of the four-subspace picture to learned representations.
  • Numerical stability and mixed precision — with FP8/FP4 hardware, maintaining backward stability of matrix factorizations under aggressive quantization is an open engineering-science problem.
