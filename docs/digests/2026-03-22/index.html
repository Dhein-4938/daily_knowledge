Computability Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Computation Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Computability theory (also recursion theory) is the branch of mathematical logic studying which functions on natural numbers are effectively computable, and how non-computable problems are structured relative to one another. It originated in the 1930s through independent work by Gödel, Church, Turing, and Kleene.

The central object is the partial recursive function: a function $f : \mathbb{N}^k \rightharpoonup \mathbb{N}$ computable by some Turing machine (or equivalently, definable via the $\mu$-operator from primitive recursion). The Church-Turing Thesis asserts this captures all physically realizable computation — a thesis rather than a theorem, since "effective procedure" resists formal definition.

Decidability is the binary case: a set $A \subseteq \mathbb{N}$ is decidable (recursive) if its characteristic function is total computable. The canonical undecidable problem is the Halting Problem:

$$K = \{ e \mid \varphi_e(e) \downarrow \}$$

where $\varphi_e$ denotes the $e$-th partial recursive function. $K$ is computably enumerable (c.e.) but not computable — its complement $\overline{K}$ is neither.

Why It Matters

Computability theory establishes hard limits on what any algorithm can do, independent of hardware or cleverness. Results like Rice's Theorem (no non-trivial semantic property of programs is decidable) and the undecidability of first-order arithmetic have cascading consequences across logic, linguistics, and software verification.

TIP: Connection to Gödel Incompleteness
The undecidability of the halting problem and Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem are two faces of the same coin — both encode self-reference to produce statements that escape the system. Turing's proof predates and independently implies Gödel's result when viewed through the lens of arithmetization.

Beyond foundations, computability theory informs complexity theory (by providing the unrelativized baseline), program analysis (static undecidability results), and formal verification (which properties are semi-decidable vs. hopeless).

Key Details

1. Turing Reducibility and Degrees

$A \leq_T B$ ("$A$ is Turing reducible to $B$") means $A$ is decidable given an oracle for $B$. This induces an equivalence relation; equivalence classes are Turing degrees. The degree $\mathbf{0}$ contains all computable sets; $\mathbf{0'}$ contains $K$. The structure of degrees under $\leq_T$ is a dense upper semi-lattice with no greatest element — extraordinarily complex.

2. The Arithmetical Hierarchy

Sets are stratified by quantifier complexity over $\mathbb{N}$:

$$\Sigma^0_n, \Pi^0_n, \Delta^0_n$$

$\Sigma^0_1$ = c.e. sets; $\Pi^0_1$ = co-c.e.; $\Delta^0_1$ = computable. Each level strictly contains the previous (by a diagonalization argument). The hierarchy collapses to $\Delta^0_1$ exactly when the oracle is $\emptyset^{(n-1)}$ — the $(n{-}1)$-th Turing jump.

3. Post's Problem and Priority Arguments

Emil Post asked (1944) whether there exists a c.e. degree strictly between $\mathbf{0}$ and $\mathbf{0'}$. Friedberg and Muchnik (1956–57) independently answered yes, using priority arguments — an intricate finite injury construction that remains the core technique in higher computability theory.

4. Subrecursive Hierarchies

The Grzegorczyk hierarchy and the Fast-Growing Hierarchy stratify total computable functions by their growth rate, connecting to proof theory: the functions provably total in Peano Arithmetic are exactly the $\mathcal{E}^3$-level functions (primitive recursive). This links computability directly to consistency strength.

5. Relative Computability vs. Subrecursive Focus

Mathematical logicians study degree structures and definability in arithmetic; computer scientists focus on subrecursive classes ($\mathbf{PRIM}$, $\mathbf{LINSPACE}$, etc.) and decidable fragments of logical theories — a genuine bifurcation in methodology.

Current State

Note: this information may be dated

Active research includes: the fine structure of $\omega$-c.e. degrees, interactions between computability and reverse mathematics (calibrating proof strength of theorems over $\text{RCA}_0$), and algorithmic randomness (where Kolmogorov complexity meets measure theory — 1-random reals are precisely those with trivial initial-segment complexity). Connections to computable structure theory — which countable algebraic structures have computable presentations — are also a growth area.

Computational Complexity✓ reviewed

Math · Complexity Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Computational complexity theory classifies problems by the minimum resources any algorithm requires to solve them — not the cost of a specific algorithm, but an intrinsic property of the problem itself.

A complexity class is a set of problems solvable within a given resource bound on a particular computational model. The two primary resources are time and space, measured as functions of input size $n$.

Formally, for a deterministic Turing machine:

$$\text{TIME}(f(n)) = \{ L \mid L \text{ decided in } O(f(n)) \text{ steps} \}$$

$$\mathbf{P} = \bigcup_{k \geq 1} \text{TIME}(n^k), \quad \mathbf{PSPACE} = \bigcup_{k \geq 1} \text{SPACE}(n^k)$$

The nondeterministic analogue gives $\mathbf{NP}$: problems whose solutions can be verified in polynomial time, or equivalently, solved in polynomial time on a nondeterministic TM.

Why It Matters

Complexity theory draws the map of what is computably feasible, not just computable. This matters across:

  • Cryptography: RSA and elliptic-curve schemes rest on the assumed hardness of integer factoring and discrete log — problems believed outside $\mathbf{P}$ but in $\mathbf{NP}$.
  • Algorithm design: Knowing a problem is $\mathbf{NP}$-hard licenses approximation, randomization, or parametric approaches rather than futile search for exact poly-time solutions.
  • AI and ML: PAC learning complexity, VC dimension bounds, and the tractability of inference in graphical models all reduce to complexity questions.
  • Quantum computing: Complexity classes like $\mathbf{BQP}$ (bounded-error quantum polynomial time) let us reason about quantum advantage rigorously.
TIP: The Real Insight
Complexity theory shifts the question from "how fast is this algorithm?" (analysis of algorithms) to "how fast can any algorithm be?" This is a fundamentally different — and harder — question, often requiring lower-bound arguments rather than constructive proofs.

Key Details

1. Reductions and Completeness

A problem $A$ is polynomial-time reducible to $B$ ($A \leq_p B$) if any instance of $A$ can be transformed to an instance of $B$ in poly-time. $B$ is $\mathbf{NP}$-complete if $B \in \mathbf{NP}$ and every $A \in \mathbf{NP}$ reduces to $B$. Cook-Levin established 3-SAT as the first such problem.

2. The Complexity Hierarchy

$$\mathbf{P} \subseteq \mathbf{NP} \subseteq \mathbf{PSPACE} \subseteq \mathbf{EXPTIME}$$

All inclusions are known, but only $\mathbf{P} \subsetneq \mathbf{EXPTIME}$ is strict (by the time hierarchy theorem). Whether $\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{NP}$ remains open.

3. Randomization

$\mathbf{BPP}$ admits bounded-error probabilistic poly-time algorithms. Most believe $\mathbf{BPP} = \mathbf{P}$ — i.e., randomness provides no asymptotic advantage — but this is unproven. Derandomization connects tightly to circuit lower bounds.

4. Circuit Complexity

Boolean circuits offer a non-uniform model: $\mathbf{P/poly}$ captures problems solvable by polynomial-size circuits. Proving super-polynomial lower bounds for explicit functions is one of the deepest open problems; progress has stalled since Razborov–Smolensky (1987) for $\mathbf{AC}^0[p]$.

5. Oracle Separations

Relativization shows limits of proof techniques: there exist oracles $A, B$ such that $\mathbf{P}^A = \mathbf{NP}^A$ and $\mathbf{P}^B \neq \mathbf{NP}^B$. Any proof resolving $\mathbf{P}$ vs. $\mathbf{NP}$ must be non-relativizing.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include:

  • $\mathbf{P}$ vs. $\mathbf{NP}$: The central open problem; no credible resolution in sight. Ryan Williams' 2010 result ($\mathbf{NEXP} \not\subset \mathbf{ACC}^0$) was a rare circuit lower bound breakthrough.
  • GCT (Geometric Complexity Theory): Mulmuley and Sohoni's program uses algebraic geometry and representation theory to approach $\mathbf{P}$ vs. $\mathbf{NP}$; progress is slow but structurally novel.
  • Fine-grained complexity: Rather than poly vs. non-poly, this asks whether problems solvable in $O(n^2)$ can be solved in $O(n^{1.99})$. SETH (Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis) underpins many conditional lower bounds.
  • Quantum complexity: Clarifying the relationship between $\mathbf{BQP}$, $\mathbf{PH}$, and $\mathbf{PSPACE}$; it's known $\mathbf{BQP} \not\subset \mathbf{PH}$ relative to some oracle.
  • Meta-complexity: Studying the complexity of complexity problems themselves — e.g., is MCSP ($\text{Minimum Circuit Size Problem}$) $\mathbf{NP}$-hard?

Causal Inference✓ reviewed

Math · Statistics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Causal inference is the formal process of identifying the independent, actual effect of a cause variable on an outcome variable — as distinct from mere statistical association. The core question is counterfactual: what would have happened to $Y$ had $X$ been different, all else equal?

INFO: Potential Outcomes Framework (Rubin Causal Model)
For unit $i$, define potential outcomes $Y_i(1)$ and $Y_i(0)$ under treatment and control. The individual treatment effect is $\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$. The fundamental problem of causal inference is that only one potential outcome is ever observed. The Average Treatment Effect (ATE) is: $$\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]$$ Identification requires assumptions (e.g., ignorability, SUTVA) to recover this from observed data.

The complementary framework is Pearl's do-calculus, which encodes causal structure in a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). The interventional distribution $P(Y \mid do(X = x))$ is fundamentally different from the observational $P(Y \mid X = x)$ — the former surgically sets $X$, severing its incoming edges in the DAG.

Why It Matters

Causal inference is the backbone of any claim that an action produces an outcome rather than merely correlating with it. Its reach spans:

  • Medicine & Epidemiology — estimating drug efficacy from observational health records when RCTs are infeasible or unethical
  • Economics & Policy — evaluating the effect of minimum wage laws, education interventions, or monetary policy
  • Machine Learning — distinguishing spurious correlations (shortcuts) from robust, generalizable features; enabling models that perform under distribution shift
  • Reinforcement Learning & Robotics — agents must reason causally to plan: if I take action $a$, what state results?
TIP: Causality as the next frontier of ML
Purely associative models overfit to distributional patterns that break under intervention. Incorporating causal structure (e.g., via invariant risk minimization or causal representation learning) is increasingly seen as necessary for out-of-distribution generalization — a direct connection to the robustness problems in modern deep learning.

Key Details

1. Confounding and the Backdoor Criterion

A confounder $Z$ opens a spurious path $X \leftarrow Z \rightarrow Y$. Pearl's backdoor criterion gives a graphical condition: a set $\mathbf{Z}$ blocks all backdoor paths from $X$ to $Y$ without opening new ones, enabling identification via:

$$P(Y \mid do(X)) = \sum_z P(Y \mid X, Z=z) \cdot P(Z=z)$$

2. Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) as Gold Standard

Random assignment of treatment breaks all backdoor paths by construction ($X \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{Z}$), making $P(Y \mid do(X)) = P(Y \mid X)$. When randomization is impossible, quasi-experimental designs approximate it.

3. Instrumental Variables (IV)

When unmeasured confounding remains, an instrument $Z$ — affecting $X$ but affecting $Y$ only through $X$ — allows identification of the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE). Strong instruments are scarce; weak instruments inflate variance catastrophically.

4. Regression Discontinuity & Difference-in-Differences

Natural experiments exploit discontinuities (arbitrary cutoffs) or parallel-trends assumptions to extract causal effects from observational data. Both rest on non-trivial identification assumptions that must be argued, not assumed.

5. Collider Bias

Conditioning on a common effect (collider) of $X$ and $Y$ induces spurious correlation. This is a frequent, underappreciated error in selection-bias scenarios (e.g., conditioning on hospitalization when studying a disease-drug relationship).

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
The debate over which causal frameworks (potential outcomes vs. structural causal models) are most appropriate across disciplines is ongoing. Methodological consensus, especially in social sciences and ML, continues to evolve rapidly.

Current State

Active research fronts as of early 2026:

  • Causal Representation Learning — learning disentangled latent variables that correspond to causally meaningful factors, enabling interventional reasoning from high-dimensional data (images, text)
  • Causal Ablation in Neural Networks — using activation injection and causal intervention to identify which components of a network causally drive behavior, as opposed to merely correlating with outputs. Recent work on VLA robotics models (Grant et al., 2026) applies causal ablation to decompose visual vs. language vs. motor pathways, finding that visual features causally dominate action generation while language serves a disambiguating role only when visual context is insufficient
  • Double/Debiased Machine Learning — using flexible ML estimators for nuisance functions while preserving $\sqrt{n}$-consistent, asymptotically normal causal effect estimates (Chernozhukov et al.)
  • Transportability — formalizing when causal conclusions from one population transfer to another with different covariate distributions

Dark Matter✓ reviewed

Science · Cosmology  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Dark matter is a gravitationally active, electromagnetically inert component of the universe — it neither emits, absorbs, nor scatters photons, yet exerts measurable gravitational influence on baryonic matter and spacetime curvature.

INFO: Operational Definition
Dark matter is inferred wherever the observed gravitational potential $\Phi(\mathbf{r})$ cannot be reconciled with the visible mass distribution $\rho_\text{bary}(\mathbf{r})$ under standard GR. It interacts via gravity and (possibly) the weak nuclear force, but not electromagnetism or the strong force.

In the Lambda-CDM Model, the energy budget of the universe is:

$$\Omega_\text{total} = \Omega_b + \Omega_\text{DM} + \Omega_\Lambda \approx 0.05 + 0.268 + 0.682 = 1$$

Dark matter thus constitutes ~85% of all matter by mass, yet its particle identity remains unknown.

Why It Matters

Dark matter is not a peripheral anomaly — it is the dominant gravitational scaffolding of cosmic structure. Without it, galaxies could not form at the observed rate or scale; the cosmic web of filaments, voids, and superclusters would not exist. It matters across multiple scales:

  • Galactic dynamics: Flat rotation curves of spiral galaxies (Rubin et al., 1970s) require a diffuse halo $\rho_\text{DM}(r) \propto r^{-2}$ well beyond the luminous disk.
  • Gravitational lensing: The deflection angle $\hat{\alpha} = \frac{4GM}{c^2 b}$ (where $b$ is impact parameter) systematically exceeds predictions from visible mass — most dramatically in the Bullet Cluster, where the lensing centroid is spatially offset from the hot gas after collision.
  • CMB anisotropies: The acoustic peak structure in $C_\ell$ spectra is only reproducible with a cold, collisionless dark matter component seeding potential wells pre-recombination.
TIP: The Bullet Cluster as a Smoking Gun
The 1E 0657-558 cluster collision provides near-decisive evidence against MOND-only explanations: the gravitational potential (traced by weak lensing) separates from the baryonic mass (traced by X-ray emission), exactly as expected if dark matter is a collisionless fluid passing through while gas ram-pressure decelerates.

Key Details

  1. Thermal classification: Cold dark matter (CDM) has negligible free-streaming length ($\lambda_\text{fs} \ll$ galactic scales), enabling small-scale structure. Warm DM ($\lambda_\text{fs} \sim$ dwarf-galaxy scales) is invoked to address the Missing Satellites Problem and Core-Cusp Problem.
  1. WIMP miracle: WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles) with $m \sim 10$–$10^3$ GeV and weak-scale cross-sections naturally produce $\Omega_\text{DM} \approx 0.27$ via freeze-out. This "WIMP miracle" motivated decades of direct detection searches, now severely constrained by LUX-ZEPLIN and XENONnT.
  1. Axions: Originally proposed to solve the Strong CP Problem, the QCD axion ($m_a \sim 10^{-5}$–$10^{-3}$ eV) is a well-motivated dark matter candidate detectable via resonant cavity experiments (ADMX). Ultralight axion fields ($m \lesssim 10^{-22}$ eV) form "fuzzy" dark matter with quantum pressure suppressing structure below $\sim 1$ kpc.
  1. Primordial black holes (PBHs): Formed before nucleosynthesis, PBHs in the mass window $10^{17}$–$10^{22}$ kg remain viable dark matter candidates (not yet excluded by microlensing or gravitational wave constraints).
  1. Local density: Despite its cosmic dominance, the local dark matter density near Earth is only $\rho_\odot^\text{DM} \approx 0.3$–$0.4 \text{ GeV/cm}^3$ — low enough that the entire dark matter mass within Neptune's orbit is $\sim 10^{17}$ kg, comparable to a large asteroid.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Direct detection experiments (LUX-ZEPLIN 2024, XENONnT) have pushed WIMP cross-section limits below $\sigma \lesssim 10^{-47}$ cm² at $\sim 40$ GeV, significantly constraining canonical WIMP models. Axion searches (ADMX, HAYSTAC) are active. Theoretical interest has shifted toward: ultralight/fuzzy DM, self-interacting DM (SIDM) to resolve small-scale tensions, and dark sector models with hidden gauge groups. The Hubble Tension ($H_0$ discrepancy ~5σ) may implicate early dark energy or exotic DM physics, though no consensus exists.

Modified gravity alternatives (MOND, TeVeS, entropic gravity) persist as minority explanations but fail to simultaneously fit rotation curves, lensing, and CMB data without invoking some dark matter component anyway.
