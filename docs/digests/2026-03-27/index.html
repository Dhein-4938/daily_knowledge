Number Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Number Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Number theory is the branch of pure mathematics concerned with the properties of integers and objects built from them — rational numbers, algebraic integers, arithmetic functions — as well as the integers as solutions to equations.

At its core, number theory asks: what deep structure underlies the natural numbers? The central object is the ring $\mathbb{Z}$, but the field expands quickly into $\mathbb{Q}$, algebraic number fields $K/\mathbb{Q}$, $p$-adic fields $\mathbb{Q}_p$, and adele rings $\mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}$.

The fundamental theorem of arithmetic — every integer $n > 1$ factors uniquely into primes — is the bedrock, but the distribution of primes is far subtler. The Prime Number Theorem gives the asymptotic density:

$$\pi(x) \sim \frac{x}{\ln x}$$

where $\pi(x)$ counts primes up to $x$. The precise error term depends on the Riemann Hypothesis, the field's deepest open problem.

Why It Matters

Number theory was long considered the purest of pure mathematics — a virtue, not a flaw. That changed decisively in the 1970s when the RSA cryptosystem (Rivest, Shamir, Adleman, 1977) weaponized the difficulty of factoring large semiprimes. Modern public-key infrastructure — TLS, PGP, digital signatures — rests entirely on number-theoretic hardness assumptions.

TIP: Unexpected depth
Fermat's Last Theorem ($a^n + b^n = c^n$ has no positive integer solutions for $n > 2$) looks like a curiosity about integers, yet Wiles's 1995 proof required the full machinery of elliptic curves, modular forms, and Galois representations — connecting number theory to algebraic geometry, complex analysis, and representation theory simultaneously.

Beyond cryptography: lattice-based post-quantum cryptography (NTRU, Kyber, Dilithium) relies on the hardness of problems in algebraic number theory. Coding theory, hash functions, and pseudorandom generation all draw from the field.

Key Details

  1. Sub-disciplines — The field fractures into: elementary (divisibility, congruences), algebraic (rings of integers in number fields, ideal class groups), analytic (L-functions, sieve methods), arithmetic geometry (elliptic curves, Diophantine geometry), and computational (primality testing, factoring algorithms).
  1. The Riemann Zeta Function — The analytic continuation of $\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s}$ encodes prime distribution through its zeros. The Euler product $\zeta(s) = \prod_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$ makes explicit the link between analytic objects and primes. The Riemann Hypothesis — all non-trivial zeros lie on $\text{Re}(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ — remains unproven after 167 years.
  1. Quadratic Reciprocity — Gauss's theorema aureum: for distinct odd primes $p, q$,

$$\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}\cdot\frac{q-1}{2}}$$

This is the prototype for the vast generalizations in class field theory and Langlands reciprocity.

  1. Diophantine Hardness — Hilbert's 10th problem (solved negatively by Matiyasevich, 1970) shows there is no general algorithm to decide integer solvability of polynomial equations. Specific families (elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$, abelian varieties) remain active research fronts.
  1. The Langlands Program — A sweeping web of conjectures relating Galois representations to automorphic forms. It unifies algebraic number theory, representation theory, and harmonic analysis under one framework. Partial results (e.g., Wiles, Taylor, Harris–Taylor) have resolved major open problems.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include: the abc conjecture (Mochizuki's claimed 2012 proof remains disputed), Goldbach's conjecture (every even integer $> 2$ is a sum of two primes — unproven since 1742), and Bounded gaps between primes (Zhang, 2013 proved infinitely many prime pairs within 70 million; the bound has since been reduced to 246). Elliptic curve BSD conjecture (Clay Millennium Problem) and the Langlands correspondence for $p$-adic groups are intensely active.

Computationally, quantum algorithms (Shor's) would break RSA in polynomial time, driving migration to lattice cryptography whose security reduces to hard problems in algebraic number theory — closing a circle elegantly.

Graph Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Combinatorics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Graph theory studies graphs — abstract structures encoding pairwise relations between objects.

INFO: Formal Definition
A graph $G = (V, E)$ consists of a finite set of vertices $V$ and a set of edges $E \subseteq \binom{V}{2}$ (for undirected graphs) or $E \subseteq V \times V$ (for directed graphs, i.e., digraphs). The order of a graph is $|V|$; its size is $|E|$.

Key variants:

  • Undirected: edges are unordered pairs $\{u, v\}$; relation is symmetric.
  • Directed (digraph): edges are ordered pairs $(u, v)$; asymmetric — an arc from $u$ to $v$ does not imply one from $v$ to $u$.
  • Weighted: edges carry a scalar $w: E \to \mathbb{R}$, encoding cost, capacity, or probability.
  • Multigraph: allows multiple edges between the same pair of vertices.
  • Hypergraph: edges can connect more than two vertices; generalises the binary relation model.

The degree of vertex $v$ in an undirected graph is $\deg(v) = |\{u \in V : \{u,v\} \in E\}|$. The handshaking lemma follows immediately:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$

Why It Matters

Graph theory is the lingua franca of relational structure across science and engineering:

  • Algorithms & CS: shortest paths (Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford), spanning trees (Kruskal, Prim), network flow (Ford-Fulkerson), matching (Hopcroft-Karp), planarity testing (linear-time via Kuratowski's theorem).
  • Networks: the internet, social graphs, biological interaction networks, and power grids are all naturally modelled as graphs.
  • Combinatorics & Logic: graph coloring encodes constraint satisfaction; Ramsey theory asks what structure must appear in any sufficiently large graph.
  • Physics & Chemistry: molecular graphs, lattice models in statistical mechanics, and tensor network contractions are all graph-theoretic.
  • Machine Learning: Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) extend deep learning to non-Euclidean domains — predicting molecular properties, recommendation systems, knowledge-graph reasoning.
TIP: Unexpected Bridge
The graph spectrum — eigenvalues of the adjacency or Laplacian matrix — connects combinatorial properties (connectivity, bipartiteness, expansion) to linear algebra and geometry. Spectral graph theory underpins both fast solvers for PDEs on meshes and community detection in social networks via the Fiedler vector.

Key Details

  1. Graph Isomorphism: Determining whether two graphs are structurally identical ($G \cong H$) is one of the few natural problems not known to be in P or NP-complete. The Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy of isomorphism tests directly shapes the expressive power of GNNs.
  1. Planarity: A graph is planar iff it contains no subdivision of $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$ (Kuratowski's theorem). Euler's formula $V - E + F = 2$ holds for any connected planar graph embedded in the sphere.
  1. Graph Coloring: The chromatic number $\chi(G)$ is the minimum number of colors needed to color $V$ such that no adjacent vertices share a color. The four-color theorem ($\chi \le 4$ for planar graphs) required a computer-assisted proof (Appel & Haken, 1976) — one of the first such proofs in mathematics.
  1. Trees & Spanning Trees: A tree is a connected acyclic graph; it satisfies $|E| = |V| - 1$. Cayley's formula gives $n^{n-2}$ labeled trees on $n$ vertices, proven elegantly via the Prüfer sequence bijection.
  1. Random Graphs: The Erdős-Rényi model $G(n, p)$ places each edge independently with probability $p$. The sharp threshold phenomenon — properties like connectivity or triangle-freeness appear almost surely above a critical $p^*(n)$ — is a cornerstone of probabilistic combinatorics.
WARNING
Note: this information may be dated — the complexity status of Graph Isomorphism, GNN expressivity results, and random graph thresholds are all actively evolving research areas.

Current State

Active frontiers include:

  • Parameterized complexity: tractable algorithms for NP-hard graph problems when a structural parameter (treewidth, genus) is small.
  • Graph Neural Networks: characterising what GNNs can and cannot compute via the Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy; designing architectures that capture higher-order structure.
  • Extremal graph theory: Turán-type problems — how many edges force a given subgraph — and their connections to arithmetic combinatorics (e.g., Szemerédi regularity lemma).
  • Dynamic & streaming graphs: maintaining connectivity, matchings, and shortest paths under edge insertions/deletions in sub-linear space or time.

Differential Equations✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
A differential equation is an equation relating an unknown function $f$ and one or more of its derivatives. The order is the highest derivative that appears; the degree is the power of that derivative (when polynomial).

The general form of an $n$-th order ODE:

$$F\!\left(x,\, y,\, y',\, y'',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n)}\right) = 0$$

For PDEs, the unknown depends on multiple variables: $u = u(x_1, \ldots, x_k)$, and the equation involves partial derivatives $\partial u / \partial x_i$.

Classification axes:

| Axis | Values |

|---|---|

| Variable count | ODE vs. PDE |

| Linearity | Linear vs. nonlinear |

| Homogeneity | Homogeneous vs. inhomogeneous |

| Order | 1st, 2nd, … |

A linear ODE has the form $\sum_{k=0}^n a_k(x)\, y^{(k)} = g(x)$. Nonlinearity — e.g., $y'' + \sin y = 0$ (pendulum) — destroys superposition and makes closed-form solutions rare.

Why It Matters

DEs are the native language of continuous dynamics. Nearly every law of physics is a differential equation: Newton's second law $F = m\ddot{x}$, Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger's equation, the Navier–Stokes equations. Beyond physics:

  • Economics: optimal control (Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman PDE), Black–Scholes option pricing
  • Biology: epidemic models (SIR), neural firing (Hodgkin–Huxley)
  • Engineering: control systems, structural analysis, heat transfer
TIP: Deeper connection
Every ODE system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = F(\mathbf{x})$ defines a vector field on state space. Solutions are integral curves — geometry and analysis interlock here. This is why Differential Geometry and Dynamical Systems Theory are natural companions.

Key Details

1. Existence and Uniqueness (Picard–Lindelöf)

For the IVP $y' = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0)=y_0$: if $f$ is continuous and Lipschitz in $y$ on a rectangle around $(x_0, y_0)$, a unique local solution exists. Lipschitz failure signals potential blow-up or branching.

2. Linear Systems and the Matrix Exponential

$\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}$ has the exact solution $\mathbf{x}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{x}_0$. The eigenstructure of $A$ governs stability: all eigenvalues with $\text{Re}(\lambda) < 0$ → asymptotically stable equilibrium. This is the backbone of Linear Stability Analysis.

3. The Fundamental Solution / Green's Function

For a linear operator $\mathcal{L}u = f$, the Green's function $G(x, s)$ satisfies $\mathcal{L}_x G = \delta(x-s)$. The particular solution is then:

$$u(x) = \int G(x,s)\, f(s)\, ds$$

A powerful inversion framework linking PDEs to Functional Analysis.

4. Bifurcations in Nonlinear DEs

As a parameter $\mu$ varies, qualitative behavior can change abruptly — saddle-node, Hopf, pitchfork bifurcations. These are not perturbative phenomena; they require global geometric analysis (Phase Portraits, center manifold reduction).

5. Numerical Methods

When closed forms fail, discretization takes over. Runge–Kutta schemes (RK4 is the workhorse), multistep Adams methods, and stiff-equation solvers (BDF, implicit Runge–Kutta) each trade stability, accuracy, and cost differently. Stiffness — widely separated timescales — is the dominant practical challenge.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include:

  • Neural ODEs: parameterizing the vector field with a neural network and backpropagating through the ODE solver via the adjoint method — bridges DEs and deep learning.
  • Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): embedding PDE residuals into the loss function; promising for inverse problems and high-dimensional PDEs where classical methods hit the curse of dimensionality.
  • Mean-field and kinetic PDEs: Vlasov–Fokker–Planck equations for large particle systems are seeing renewed rigor alongside applications in multi-agent learning and interacting diffusions.
  • Data-driven discovery of DEs (SINDy and variants): sparse regression over a library of candidate terms to recover governing equations from trajectory data.

Category Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Abstract Algebra  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Category theory is the mathematics of structure and structure-preserving maps. Where set theory asks "what are things made of?", category theory asks "how do things relate?". It was introduced by Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane (1945) while formalizing constructions in algebraic topology.

INFO: Definition: Category
A category $\mathcal{C}$ consists of: - A collection of objects $\text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ - For each pair $A, B \in \text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$, a collection of morphisms $\text{Hom}(A, B)$ - A composition law: if $f \in \text{Hom}(A,B)$ and $g \in \text{Hom}(B,C)$, then $g \circ f \in \text{Hom}(A,C)$ - Identity morphisms $\text{id}_A \in \text{Hom}(A,A)$ for each object Subject to associativity: $(h \circ g) \circ f = h \circ (g \circ f)$, and unit laws: $f \circ \text{id}_A = f = \text{id}_B \circ f$.

A functor $F : \mathcal{C}_1 \to \mathcal{C}_2$ is a structure-preserving map between categories — it sends objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms while respecting composition:

$$F(g \circ f) = F(g) \circ F(f), \quad F(\text{id}_A) = \text{id}_{F(A)}$$

A natural transformation $\eta : F \Rightarrow G$ between two functors provides a coherent family of morphisms $\eta_A : F(A) \to G(A)$ such that for every morphism $f : A \to B$, the naturality square commutes: $G(f) \circ \eta_A = \eta_B \circ F(f)$.

Why It Matters

Category theory is uniquely powerful because it abstracts away the interior of mathematical objects. The same categorical construction — limits, colimits, adjunctions — appears independently in topology, algebra, logic, and computer science. This lets you prove theorems once and apply them everywhere.

TIP: The Adjunction Insight
Adjoint functors $F \dashv G$ (where $\text{Hom}(FA, B) \cong \text{Hom}(A, GB)$ naturally) capture an enormous range of mathematical duality: free/forgetful, product/exponential, quantifier/substitution. MacLane's dictum: "Adjoint functors arise everywhere."

In functional programming, category theory is the semantic backbone. Monads are monoids in the category of endofunctors; the fmap of Haskell's Functor typeclass is literally a functor mapping. In logic, the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence triangulates propositions, types, and objects of a cartesian closed category.

Key Details

  1. Morphisms over elements. A monoid is a category with one object — its elements are morphisms. A group is the same with all morphisms invertible. A preorder is a category where $|\text{Hom}(A,B)| \leq 1$. The categorical perspective unifies these with no extra effort.
  1. Universal properties. Products, coproducts, limits, and colimits are defined purely by universal properties — existence and uniqueness up to unique isomorphism. This is categorically the right way to define constructions; it guarantees they're canonical.
  1. Yoneda Lemma. Arguably the most important theorem in category theory:

$$\text{Nat}(\text{Hom}(A, -),\ F) \cong F(A)$$

An object is completely determined by how other objects map into it. This grounds representability and is the categorical foundation of sheaf theory and algebraic geometry.

  1. Size issues. Naïve category theory runs into set-theoretic paradoxes (the category of all sets). Handling this requires Grothendieck universes, or working with small vs. large categories explicitly.
  1. Contravariant functors. A contravariant functor $F : \mathcal{C}^{op} \to \mathcal{D}$ reverses arrows. This is how cohomology arises from homology — it's the same structure, dualised.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include Higher Category Theory (∞-categories, (∞,1)-categories via quasi-categories or complete Segal spaces), which has transformed modern homotopy theory through Lurie's Higher Topos Theory. Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) uses categorical semantics to give a computational foundation for mathematics where identity is homotopy. Applied category theory is growing rapidly — compositional approaches appear in quantum computing (ZX-calculus), systems biology, and database schema migration.
