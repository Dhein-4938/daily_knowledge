Mathematical Optimization✓ reviewed

Math · Optimization  ·  ~3 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Mathematical optimization is the selection of a best element from a feasible set according to some objective criterion — formally, finding $x^*$ such that: $$x^* = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{X}} f(x)$$ where $f : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the objective function and $\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is the feasible set, defined by equality and inequality constraints $g_i(x) \leq 0$, $h_j(x) = 0$.

Maximization reduces trivially: $\max f(x) \equiv \min -f(x)$. The field splits along the structure of $\mathcal{X}$ and $f$:

  • Continuous optimization — $\mathcal{X}$ is a continuous domain; gradient information often available
  • Discrete (combinatorial) optimization — $\mathcal{X}$ is finite or countably infinite (e.g., integer programs, graph problems)
  • Mixed-integer — hybrid of both

Why It Matters

Optimization is the mathematical backbone of nearly every quantitative discipline. Machine learning training reduces to minimizing a loss function; circuit design minimizes power under timing constraints; logistics solves vehicle routing; economics finds equilibria via utility maximization. The universality is not coincidental — any decision under constraint is an optimization problem in disguise.

TIP: Unifying Lens
Many seemingly distinct algorithms — EM, policy gradient, belief propagation, auction mechanisms — are optimization algorithms over different spaces (parameter space, policy space, marginal polytope, price space). Recognizing the underlying optimizer often reveals convergence guarantees hidden in domain-specific language.

Key Details

  1. Convexity is the central structural property. If $f$ is convex and $\mathcal{X}$ is a convex set, every local minimum is a global minimum. The KKT conditions become necessary and sufficient. Most tractable large-scale methods exploit convexity either directly or approximately.
  1. First- and second-order conditions. At a smooth unconstrained local minimum: $\nabla f(x^*) = 0$ (stationarity) and $\nabla^2 f(x^*) \succeq 0$ (positive semidefinite Hessian). Constrained problems introduce Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_i$, giving the Lagrangian:

$$\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \nu) = f(x) + \sum_i \lambda_i g_i(x) + \sum_j \nu_j h_j(x)$$

  1. Duality. Every primal problem has a dual; strong duality ($p^* = d^*$) holds under constraint qualifications (e.g., Slater's condition for convex programs). Duality gaps signal non-convexity and guide branch-and-bound in integer programming.
  1. Complexity landscape. Linear programs (LP) are solvable in polynomial time (ellipsoid method, interior-point). Quadratic programs (QP) with positive-definite Hessians are tractable; non-convex QPs are NP-hard. Most combinatorial problems (TSP, max-cut) are NP-hard exactly but admit approximation algorithms or semidefinite relaxations.
  1. Stochastic and gradient-free settings. When $f$ is a sum over data $f(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i f_i(x)$, stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with noise variance $\sigma^2$ converges at rate $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ for convex $f$. Zeroth-order (derivative-free) methods are essential when gradients are unavailable — e.g., black-box simulators.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include inference-time optimization — using optimization as a search mechanism at deployment, not just training. Recent robotics work (e.g., VERITAS, 2026) frames policy improvement as a generator-verifier loop where a gradient-free verifier steers action selection, effectively performing constrained search in trajectory space without gradient access. This connects to best-of-N sampling, MCTS, and diffusion-based planning.

In deep learning, non-convex landscape analysis has matured: saddle points (not local minima) dominate in overparameterized networks, and implicit regularization from SGD noise partially explains generalization. Second-order methods (Shampoo, K-FAC) are resurging for large-scale training as compute costs justify better curvature estimates.

On the discrete side, neural combinatorial optimization — learning heuristics for NP-hard problems via RL — challenges classical OR solvers on practical instances.

Lambda Calculus✓ reviewed

Logic · Computation Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Lambda calculus (λ-calculus) is a formal system for expressing computation through function abstraction and function application. Introduced by Alonzo Church in the 1930s, it is one of the two foundational models of computation — the other being the Turing Machine — and they are provably equivalent in expressive power.

INFO: Syntax (BNF)
Every λ-term $e$ is one of: $$e ::= x \mid \lambda x.\, e \mid e_1\; e_2$$ — a variable, a lambda abstraction (binding $x$ in body $e$), or an application (applying $e_1$ to $e_2$).

The sole computational rule is β-reduction:

$$(\lambda x.\, e_1)\; e_2 \;\longrightarrow_\beta\; e_1[x := e_2]$$

Substituting $e_2$ for free occurrences of $x$ in $e_1$. An additional rule, α-conversion, allows bound variable renaming to avoid capture:

$$\lambda x.\, e \;\equiv_\alpha\; \lambda y.\, e[x := y] \quad (y \notin \mathrm{FV}(e))$$

η-conversion captures extensionality: $\lambda x.\, f\, x \equiv_\eta f$ when $x \notin \mathrm{FV}(f)$.

Why It Matters

λ-calculus is the theoretical backbone of functional programming — Haskell, ML, Lisp, and Scheme are all direct descendants. Its significance extends far beyond PL design:

  • Foundations of mathematics: Church used it to investigate decidability, leading directly to the negative solution of the Entscheidungsproblem (independently resolved by Turing).
  • Proof theory: The Curry-Howard Correspondence maps λ-terms to proofs and types to propositions — programs are proofs.
  • Denotational semantics: Provides the mathematical substrate for giving meaning to programs without executing them.
  • Type theory and dependent types: Typed λ-calculi (simply-typed, System F, CoC) underpin proof assistants like Coq and Lean.
TIP: Church–Turing Thesis connection
The equivalence of untyped λ-calculus and Turing machines is non-trivial: λ-calculus encodes data (via Church numerals), conditionals, and recursion (via the Y combinator) purely through functions. There are no side effects, no memory — only substitution. This makes it an unusually pure model of computation.

Key Details

1. Church Encodings — Natural numbers, booleans, and pairs can all be represented as pure functions. The Church numeral for $n$ applies a function $f$ exactly $n$ times:

$$\bar{n} \equiv \lambda f.\,\lambda x.\, f^n(x)$$

2. The Y Combinator — Recursion without named functions is achieved via the fixed-point combinator:

$$Y \equiv \lambda f.\,(\lambda x.\, f\,(x\,x))\,(\lambda x.\, f\,(x\,x))$$

$Y\,g$ reduces to $g\,(Y\,g)$, threading recursion through self-application. Note: $Y$ has no normal form in the untyped calculus — it diverges if $g$ doesn't force termination.

3. Confluence (Church-Rosser Theorem) — If a term $e$ reduces to both $e_1$ and $e_2$, there exists a $e_3$ such that both reduce to $e_3$. This guarantees determinism of normal forms: evaluation order affects efficiency, not result.

4. Undecidability of β-equality — There is no algorithm to decide whether two arbitrary λ-terms are β-equal. This is the λ-calculus analogue of the halting problem.

5. Typed vs. Untyped — Untyped λ-calculus is Turing-complete but permits non-termination. Simply-typed λ-calculus (STLC) is strongly normalizing (all reductions terminate) but not Turing-complete — a fundamental trade-off between expressivity and decidability.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research frontiers include:

  • Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT): Extensions of dependent type theory (rooted in typed λ-calculi) that treat type equality as path spaces from Homotopy Theory.
  • Probabilistic and quantum λ-calculi: Extending the formalism to model randomized and quantum computation.
  • Optimal reduction: Lévy's work on sharing redexes (implemented in interaction nets) seeks the minimal number of β-steps — still an open efficiency problem for practical implementations.
  • Linear λ-calculus: Restricting variable use to exactly once, connecting to Linear Logic and resource-sensitive computation models.

Type Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Type theory is the study of formal systems in which every expression is assigned a type — a classification that governs what the expression is and what operations are valid on it. This is not merely bookkeeping: the type discipline itself carries logical content.

INFO: Formal Definition
A type theory is a formal system comprising a set of types $\tau$, a set of terms $t$, and a typing judgment of the form: $$\Gamma \vdash t : \tau$$ meaning "in context $\Gamma$, term $t$ has type $\tau$." Rules for forming valid judgments are called typing rules.

The foundational dichotomy is between simple and dependent type theories:

  • Simple type theory (Church, 1940): types are fixed independently of terms. Function types written $\alpha \to \beta$.
  • Dependent type theory (Martin-Löf, 1972–84): types can depend on terms. The type $\text{Vec}(n)$ of vectors of length $n$ is a canonical example. This enables propositions to be encoded as types via the Curry–Howard correspondence: a proof of proposition $P$ is a term of type $P$.

The identity type $\text{Id}_A(a, b)$ — whose inhabitants are proofs that $a = b$ — is the engine behind Homotopy Type Theory, where paths between points in a space model propositional equality.

Why It Matters

Type theory occupies a rare intersection of logic, computation, and mathematics:

  1. Foundation of mathematics: Martin-Löf Type Theory (MLTT) and the Calculus of Inductive Constructions (CIC) are serious rivals to Set Theory (ZFC) as foundational frameworks — constructive by default, proof-relevant, and computation-aware.
  2. Proof assistants: Rocq (Coq) runs on CIC; Lean 4 is built on a dependent type theory called the Calculus of Constructions with universe polymorphism. These tools machine-check real mathematical proofs.
  3. Programming language design: Hindley-Milner type inference underlies ML and Haskell. Dependent types appear in Agda, Idris, and increasingly in industrial languages.
TIP: Propositions as Types
The Curry–Howard correspondence is not an analogy — it is an isomorphism. $\Pi$-types are universal quantifiers; $\Sigma$-types are existential quantifiers; the empty type $\bot$ is falsehood; the unit type $\top$ is truth. Writing a program is writing a proof.

Key Details

  1. Universes and Russell's Paradox: Naïve type theory admits $\text{Type} : \text{Type}$, which leads to Girard's Paradox (analogous to Russell's). The fix is a universe hierarchy $\mathcal{U}_0 : \mathcal{U}_1 : \mathcal{U}_2 : \cdots$, preventing a type from containing itself.
  1. Definitional vs. propositional equality: Two terms can be definitionally equal (interchangeable by computation rules, checked algorithmically) or merely propositionally equal (connected by an inhabitant of the identity type). The gap between these notions is where much subtlety lives.
  1. Univalence Axiom: In Homotopy Type Theory, Voevodsky's Univalence Axiom states $(A \simeq B) \simeq (A = B)$ — equivalence of types is their identity. This is not provable in MLTT; it must be added, and Cubical Type Theory makes it definitionally true.
  1. Termination and decidability: Typed lambda calculi with strong normalization (e.g., System F, CoC) guarantee all well-typed terms terminate — at the cost of not being Turing-complete as a programming language. The tension between expressiveness and decidability is fundamental.
  1. Linear and substructural types: Restricting how variables can be used (used exactly once in linear types) gives resource-aware type systems. Rust's ownership model is an industrial instance of affine typing.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include:

  • Cubical type theories (CCHM, Cartesian Cubical TT): give computational interpretations of Univalence, making HoTT implementable.
  • Two-level type theories (2LTT): separating a "fibrant" layer (homotopy-aware) from a "strict" layer for meta-reasoning.
  • Multimodal and graded type theories: tracking resource usage, phase distinctions (compile-time vs. runtime), and security levels within a single framework.
  • Proof assistant interoperability: projects like Dedukti aim to translate proofs across systems built on different type theories.

Curry-Howard Correspondence✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
The Curry–Howard correspondence (also isomorphism or proofs-as-programs) is a structural identity between systems of formal logic and models of computation: propositions are types, and proofs are programs.

The correspondence is not merely metaphorical — it is an exact syntactic and semantic isomorphism between derivation trees in a logic and well-typed terms in a lambda calculus. The canonical alignment:

| Logic | Type Theory |

|---|---|

| Proposition $P$ | Type $\tau$ |

| Proof of $P$ | Term $t : \tau$ |

| Implication $P \Rightarrow Q$ | Function type $\tau \to \sigma$ |

| Conjunction $P \land Q$ | Product type $\tau \times \sigma$ |

| Disjunction $P \lor Q$ | Sum type $\tau + \sigma$ |

| Falsehood $\bot$ | Empty type $\mathbf{0}$ |

| Universal quantification $\forall x.\ P(x)$ | Dependent product $\Pi (x : A).\ B(x)$ |

| Existential quantification $\exists x.\ P(x)$ | Dependent sum $\Sigma (x : A).\ B(x)$ |

The base case: intuitionistic propositional logic corresponds exactly to the simply typed lambda calculus ($\lambda^\to$). Extend to first-order logic and you arrive at dependent type theory — the foundation of proof assistants like Coq and Agda.

$$ \frac{\Gamma, x : \tau \vdash t : \sigma}{\Gamma \vdash \lambda x.\ t : \tau \to \sigma} \quad\longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{\Gamma, P \vdash \pi : Q}{\Gamma \vdash \lambda\text{-abstraction} : P \Rightarrow Q} $$

Proof normalization (cut-elimination) corresponds exactly to beta-reduction in the lambda calculus — computation is proof simplification.

Why It Matters

This is not a curiosity — it is the deepest bridge between mathematics and computer science:

  • Proof assistants: Coq, Agda, Lean, and Idris are direct engineering consequences. Writing a type-correct program is constructing a proof.
  • Certified software: Security protocols, compilers (CompCert), and OS kernels can be verified by encoding correctness as a type that the program must inhabit.
  • Type system design: Linear types correspond to linear logic; session types correspond to classical sequent calculi; effect systems connect to modal logics.
  • Foundations of mathematics: Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) extends the correspondence into homotopy theory, where types are spaces and proofs are paths.
TIP: The Constructive Constraint
The correspondence holds for intuitionistic (constructive) logic, not classical logic. The law of excluded middle ($P \lor \neg P$) has no direct computational content — its "proof" doesn't tell you which side holds. Classical logic corresponds instead to lambda calculi with continuations (Parigot's $\lambda\mu$-calculus), where $\neg\neg P \Rightarrow P$ maps to double-negation elimination via callcc.

Key Details

  1. BHK Interpretation precedes it: Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov gave the semantic reading (a proof of $P \Rightarrow Q$ is a function from proofs of $P$ to proofs of $Q$); Curry–Howard made this syntactic and formal.
  1. Normalization = Termination: Strong normalization of the typed calculus corresponds to consistency of the logic (no proof of $\bot$). An inconsistent type system allows non-terminating programs to inhabit every type — undefined :: a in Haskell being the canonical cheat.
  1. The Lambek extension: Category theory completes the trinity. A cartesian closed category (CCC) simultaneously models both the typed lambda calculus and intuitionistic logic. Objects = types/propositions; morphisms = terms/proofs; composition = cut/function application.
  1. Linear logic correspondence: Girard's linear logic (1987) corresponds to session types and resource-aware computation — a "use exactly once" proposition maps to a linear function type.
  1. Proof relevance: In dependent type theory, the identity of two proofs of the same proposition can matter (proof relevance). HoTT radicalizes this: two proofs being "equal" becomes a homotopy path between points in a space.
WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Active research areas like cubical type theory implementations, the precise computational content of classical logic extensions, and HoTT's homotopy-theoretic foundations continue to evolve rapidly.

Current State

The correspondence has expanded well beyond its origins:

  • Cubical type theory (Cohen et al., 2016 onward) gives computational content to the univalence axiom in HoTT, resolving a long-standing gap where the axiom had no canonical reduction rule.
  • Linear and graded type systems (e.g., Granule) track resource usage via correspondences with substructural logics.
  • Realizability theory generalizes the correspondence beyond lambda calculi to arbitrary models of computation (Turing machines, PCAs).
  • Proof mining uses the correspondence in reverse: extract computational witnesses (algorithms, bounds) from classical proofs by translating them into constructive form.
