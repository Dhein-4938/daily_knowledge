Computability Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Computation Theory  ·  ~5 min read

Core Concept

Computability theory (also recursion theory) is the branch of mathematical logic and theoretical computer science concerned with formalizing what it means for a function to be effectively calculable, and with classifying functions that are not.

INFO: Church-Turing Thesis
Every effectively calculable function is computable by a Turing machine. This is not a theorem — it's a definition-level thesis that identifies the informal notion of "algorithm" with a precise mathematical model. The major equivalent models (λ-calculus, general recursive functions, Turing machines) all converge on the same class.

The primary object of study is the class of partial computable (recursive) functions $f : \mathbb{N}^k \rightharpoonup \mathbb{N}$, built from zero, successor, and projection via composition, primitive recursion, and the minimization ($\mu$) operator. Functions requiring $\mu$ may diverge — they are partial, not total.

A set $A \subseteq \mathbb{N}$ is computably enumerable (c.e.) if it is the domain of some partial computable function, equivalently if a Turing machine can enumerate its members (possibly without halting on non-members). $A$ is computable iff both $A$ and $\bar{A}$ are c.e.

The canonical non-computable problem is the Halting Problem $K = \{e \mid \varphi_e(e) \downarrow\}$, which is c.e. but not computable. Its undecidability is proven by diagonalization: assume a decider exists, construct a contradiction.

Why It Matters

Computability theory draws the hard boundary of what any algorithm can ever do — not due to engineering limits, but in principle. Results here are absolute: no faster hardware, no cleverer language, no quantum speedup crosses the boundary set by Rice's Theorem or the undecidability of first-order logic.

Connections fan out widely:

  • Proof theory: Gödel's incompleteness theorems are essentially computability arguments in disguise — unprovable sentences correspond to non-computable predicates.
  • Descriptive set theory: The arithmetical hierarchy $\Sigma^0_n / \Pi^0_n$ mirrors the Borel hierarchy of sets of reals; effective versions (lightface) are central to both fields.
  • Complexity theory: Computability theory is the "infinite-resource" ceiling above which complexity theory carves out finer, resource-bounded distinctions like $\mathsf{P}$ vs $\mathsf{NP}$.
  • Formal verification: Undecidability results (e.g., program equivalence, termination) set hard limits on what static analysis and model checkers can guarantee.

Key Details

  1. The Arithmetical Hierarchy. Sets are stratified by quantifier alternation over $\mathbb{N}$:

$$A \in \Sigma^0_n \iff A = \{x \mid \exists y_1\, \forall y_2\, \cdots\, Q y_n\; R(x, \vec{y})\}$$

$\Sigma^0_1$ = c.e. sets; $\Delta^0_1$ = computable sets. The hierarchy is strict and does not collapse.

  1. Turing Reducibility and Degrees. $A \leq_T B$ (A is Turing-reducible to B) if a Turing machine with oracle $B$ can decide $A$. This induces an equivalence relation; the equivalence classes are Turing degrees. The degrees form an upper semilattice under join $A \oplus B$, but lack a meet in general — the structure is extraordinarily complex (every countable partial order embeds into it).
  1. The Jump Operator. $A' = \{e \mid \varphi_e^A(e) \downarrow\}$ (the Turing jump) strictly increases Turing degree: $A <_T A'$ always. Iterating gives $\mathbf{0}^{(n)}$, the degree of $\Sigma^0_n$-complete sets.
  1. Rice's Theorem. For any non-trivial semantic property $P$ of partial computable functions, the index set $\{e \mid \varphi_e \in P\}$ is undecidable. "Non-trivial" means $P$ holds for some functions and fails for others. This kills most hopes of static analysis in one stroke.
  1. Post's Problem and the Priority Method. Emil Post asked (1944) whether there exist c.e. degrees strictly between $\mathbf{0}$ and $\mathbf{0}'$. Friedberg and Muchnik (1956) independently answered yes, using the finite injury priority argument — a foundational technique for constructing degrees with incompatibility constraints.
TIP: Recursion ≠ Repetition
The term "recursion theory" predates the programming usage of "recursion." It refers to general recursive functions (Gödel-Herbrand-Kleene), not to self-referential procedure calls. The renaming to "computability theory" reflects a cleaner conceptual framing, not a change of subject.

  • Computable structure theory: Which countable algebraic/topological structures have computable presentations? What is the computable categoricity spectrum? (E.g., linear orders, Boolean algebras, groups.)
  • Continuous and higher computability: Extending the framework to real-number computation (Type-2 theory, computable analysis) and higher-type functionals — bridging to constructive analysis and homotopy type theory.
  • Algorithmic randomness: Characterizing random sequences via incompressibility (Kolmogorov complexity) or unpredictability (martingales). Martin-Löf randomness, Schnorr randomness, and their degree-theoretic properties remain actively studied.

Computational Complexity✓ reviewed

Math · Complexity Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Computational complexity theory classifies problems — not algorithms — by the minimum resources any algorithm must expend to solve them. This is a subtle but crucial distinction: it asks what is inherently hard about a problem, independent of the cleverness of any particular solution.

INFO
A complexity class is a set of decision problems solvable within a given resource bound on a specified computational model. The two primary resources are time (number of steps) and space (memory cells used), measured as functions of input size $n$.

The canonical classes are defined on a deterministic Turing machine:

$$\text{TIME}(f(n)) = \{ L \mid L \text{ decidable in } O(f(n)) \text{ steps} \}$$

$$\mathbf{P} = \bigcup_{k \geq 1} \text{TIME}(n^k), \qquad \mathbf{PSPACE} = \bigcup_{k \geq 1} \text{SPACE}(n^k)$$

Nondeterminism yields $\mathbf{NP}$ — problems verifiable in polynomial time — with the famous containment chain:

$$\mathbf{P} \subseteq \mathbf{NP} \subseteq \mathbf{PSPACE} \subseteq \mathbf{EXP}$$

At least one of these inclusions is strict; which ones, and how many, remains largely open.

Why It Matters

Complexity theory is the formal backbone of modern cryptography: RSA, elliptic-curve cryptography, and lattice-based schemes all stake their security on assumed hardness results (e.g., integer factorization $\notin \mathbf{P}$, or worst-case hardness of lattice problems). It also governs the tractability frontier for AI search, optimization, and verification — knowing a problem is NP-hard or #P-hard tells you to look for approximation algorithms, heuristics, or special-case structure rather than exact polynomial-time solutions.

Beyond classical computation, the framework extends cleanly: BQP captures quantum polynomial time, and the question of whether $\mathbf{BQP} \supsetneq \mathbf{P}$ (believed true, unproven) drives the entire quantum computing industry.

TIP
Complexity theory and logic are deeply intertwined via descriptive complexity: Fagin's theorem shows $\mathbf{NP}$ equals the class of problems expressible in existential second-order logic over finite structures — a purely logical characterization with no mention of Turing machines or time bounds.

Key Details

  1. Reductions as the central tool. A polynomial-time many-one reduction $A \leq_p B$ means an instance of $A$ can be transformed to an instance of $B$ in poly time. NP-completeness = NP-membership + hardness under these reductions. Cook–Levin (1971/1973) established SAT as the first NP-complete problem.
  1. The oracle barrier. Results like Baker–Gill–Solovay (1975) showed that $\mathbf{P}^A \neq \mathbf{NP}^A$ for some oracles $A$ and $\mathbf{P}^B = \mathbf{NP}^B$ for others. This means any proof of $\mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP}$ cannot relativize — ruling out a huge class of naive proof strategies.
  1. Circuit complexity as an alternative lens. Boolean circuits replace Turing machines; complexity is measured by circuit size (gates) and depth (parallel time). Showing that a specific function requires superpolynomial circuits would separate $\mathbf{P}$ from $\mathbf{NP}/\text{poly}$. Lower bound proofs (e.g., for $\mathbf{AC}^0$ via the switching lemma) are among the field's hardest technical achievements.
  1. Interactive proofs vastly expand verifiability. $\mathbf{IP} = \mathbf{PSPACE}$ (Shamir, 1992) and $\mathbf{MIP}^* = \mathbf{RE}$ (Ji et al., 2020) show that with interaction and entanglement, verifiers can check problems far beyond NP — the latter result using quantum entangled provers to decide undecidable languages.
  1. Average-case vs. worst-case. Standard complexity is worst-case. Cryptography requires average-case hardness — a problem useless for crypto if it's only hard on rare inputs. Bridging worst-case to average-case hardness (as lattice-based crypto does) is both theoretically deep and practically critical.

  • Meta-complexity: Studying the complexity of complexity itself — e.g., the hardness of circuit minimization (MCSP), which connects to cryptography and learning theory in unexpected ways.
  • Proof complexity: Lower bounds on the length of proofs in systems like Resolution, Frege, and $\mathbf{AC}^0$-Frege, closely tied to whether $\mathbf{NP} \neq \mathbf{coNP}$.
  • Quantum separations: Post-2019 oracle separations (e.g., Raz–Tal) confirm $\mathbf{BQP} \not\subseteq \mathbf{PH}$ relative to an oracle, strengthening the case for quantum advantage.

Causal Inference✓ reviewed

Math · Statistics  ·  ~5 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Causal inference is the process of identifying the independent, actual effect of a variable (cause) on another (effect) within a larger system — distinct from mere statistical association. It asks: if I intervene on X, what happens to Y?

The canonical formalism is Pearl's do-calculus. Where standard probability conditions on observed values, the do-operator models intervention:

$$P(Y \mid do(X = x)) \neq P(Y \mid X = x)$$

The left side is a causal quantity (what would happen if we set $X$ to $x$); the right is an associational quantity (what we observe among units where $X = x$ happened naturally). Conflating them is the source of most causal fallacies.

The Structural Causal Model (SCM) framework encodes this via a directed acyclic graph (DAG) $\mathcal{G}$ over variables $V$, with structural equations $X_i := f_i(\text{pa}(X_i), U_i)$ where $\text{pa}(X_i)$ are parents in $\mathcal{G}$ and $U_i$ are exogenous noise terms. Intervention $do(X = x)$ surgically removes incoming edges to $X$ and fixes its value.

The Potential Outcomes (Rubin) framework is complementary: each unit $i$ has counterfactual outcomes $Y_i(1)$ and $Y_i(0)$ under treatment and control, and the fundamental problem of causal inference is that only one is ever observed. The Average Treatment Effect is:

$$\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]$$

Why It Matters

Correlation is abundant and cheap; causation is rare and expensive. The practical stakes are enormous:

  • Policy: Does a job training program cause higher earnings, or do motivated people self-select into both?
  • Medicine: Does drug X cause recovery, or do healthier patients receive it more often?
  • ML fairness: Is a model's disparity a causal effect of a protected attribute, or a proxy correlation?
  • Science replication: Many "discoveries" collapse under causal scrutiny — they were spurious correlations amplified by publication bias.
TIP: Observational Data as the Norm
Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are the gold standard precisely because randomization breaks the dependence between treatment and confounders, making $P(Y \mid do(X)) = P(Y \mid X)$. But RCTs are often unethical, expensive, or impossible — hence the entire machinery of causal inference exists to extract causal signal from messy observational data.

Key Details

  1. Confounding and the Backdoor Criterion: A confounder $Z$ opens a backdoor path from $X$ to $Y$ — a non-causal path that creates spurious association. Pearl's backdoor criterion gives a graphical condition: a set $S$ blocks all backdoor paths from $X$ to $Y$ iff $P(Y \mid do(X)) = \sum_s P(Y \mid X, S=s)\,P(S=s)$. This justifies covariate adjustment — but only for the right covariates.
  1. Collider Bias: Conditioning on a collider (a node that is a common effect of two variables) opens a path that was previously closed, inducing spurious association. This is a subtle and frequently violated assumption in applied work (e.g., conditioning on a mediator or on sample selection).
  1. Instrumental Variables (IV): When unmeasured confounders exist, a valid instrument $Z$ — one that affects $X$ but affects $Y$ only through $X$ — allows identification of the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE). The exclusion restriction (no direct $Z \to Y$ path) is unverifiable from data alone, making IV credibility partly a domain-knowledge judgment.
  1. Identification vs. Estimation: These are distinct problems. Identification asks whether a causal quantity is in principle recoverable from the observational distribution (a graphical/algebraic question). Estimation asks how to estimate it from finite data (a statistical question). Conflating them leads to either overclaiming or unnecessary complexity.
  1. Regression Danger Zone: Linear regression estimates associational coefficients, not causal effects, unless the causal identification assumptions hold. The coefficient on $X$ in $Y \sim X + Z$ is causal only if $Z$ blocks all backdoors and opens no collider paths — conditions that require domain knowledge, not statistical testing alone.
WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
The debate over best practices in observational causal inference (DiD vs. synthetic control vs. regression discontinuity) is extremely active, and methodological recommendations have shifted considerably in the 2020s — particularly around the credibility of parallel trends assumptions in difference-in-differences designs.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated
Active research frontiers as of mid-2026 include causal representation learning (learning SCMs from raw data, e.g., images), causal discovery algorithms (constraint-based like PC/FCI, score-based like GES, and hybrid), and integrating causal structure into large neural models. The arXiv papers above (PhysiFormer, ArBG) hint at adjacent concerns: neural simulators that implicitly must respect physical causality (temporal ordering, momentum conservation) — whether they do so structurally or merely behaviorally is an open question. Causal ML — using causal methods to make learned models more robust to distribution shift — is increasingly mainstream.

Key unresolved tensions:

  • Can LLMs perform genuine causal reasoning, or do they pattern-match causal language?
  • How to do causal inference on time-series with feedback loops (violating DAG acyclicity)?
  • Scalable causal discovery in high dimensions remains computationally intractable without strong prior assumptions.

Dark Matter✓ reviewed

Science · Cosmology  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Dark matter is a hypothetical, non-luminous form of matter inferred entirely through its gravitational effects. It does not interact with the electromagnetic force — it neither emits, absorbs, nor reflects photons — yet accounts for the overwhelming majority of the universe's matter content.

INFO
In the Λ-CDM (Lambda Cold Dark Matter) model, the mass–energy budget of the universe is: $$\Omega_b \approx 0.05, \quad \Omega_{DM} \approx 0.268, \quad \Omega_\Lambda \approx 0.682$$ Dark matter thus constitutes ~85% of all matter and ~27% of total mass-energy. The remainder is ordinary baryonic matter (~5%) and dark energy (~68%).

The "cold" in CDM refers to the thermal velocity distribution at the time of structure formation: cold dark matter (CDM) particles had non-relativistic velocities, enabling hierarchical bottom-up clustering — small structures forming first, merging into progressively larger ones.

Why It Matters

Dark matter is not merely a cosmological curiosity — it is the gravitational scaffold upon which all large-scale structure depends. Without it, the observed clustering of galaxies, the rotation curves of spiral galaxies, and the cosmic web of filaments and voids cannot be reproduced by standard gravity acting on baryons alone.

Key phenomenological roles:

  • Galactic rotation curves: Stars at the periphery of spiral galaxies orbit far faster than Keplerian mechanics predicts from visible mass alone — the canonical observation by Vera Rubin (1970s).
  • Gravitational lensing: The bending of light by galaxy clusters (e.g., the Bullet Cluster) maps mass concentrations that do not coincide with hot X-ray-emitting gas, directly evidencing a decoupled dark component.
  • CMB anisotropies: The acoustic peak structure in the cosmic microwave background encodes the ratio of baryonic to dark matter, tightly constraining $\Omega_{DM}$.
  • Structure formation: The matter power spectrum $P(k)$ — the Fourier-space distribution of density fluctuations — is only reproduced when CDM seeds early collapse.
TIP
The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558) is often cited as the most direct observational evidence: during a cluster–cluster collision, gas (traced by X-ray emission) is slowed by electromagnetic interactions, while the dark matter halos (traced by weak lensing) pass through each other unimpeded — exactly what a weakly-interacting particle predicts.

Key Details

  1. Candidate particles: The most theoretically motivated candidates are WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles, $m \sim 10\text{–}10^4\ \text{GeV}$) and axions ($m \sim 10^{-5}\ \text{eV}$). Primordial black holes (PBHs) remain a viable macroscopic alternative. Sterile neutrinos occupy warm dark matter territory.
  1. Free-streaming length distinguishes thermal regimes: cold DM ($\lambda_{fs} \ll$ Mpc), warm DM ($\lambda_{fs} \sim 0.1\text{–}1\ \text{Mpc}$), hot DM (e.g., standard neutrinos, ruled out as dominant component by structure formation constraints).
  1. Local density: Despite global dominance, dark matter is dilute in the Solar System. The integrated dark matter mass within Neptune's orbit is $\sim 10^{17}\ \text{kg}$ — roughly a large asteroid. Local density estimates: $\rho_{DM,\odot} \approx 0.3\ \text{GeV/cm}^3$.
  1. Halo structure: Simulations (e.g., Millenium, Illustris) predict NFW (Navarro–Frenk–White) halo profiles: $\rho(r) \propto \frac{1}{r(1 + r/r_s)^2}$, with a central cusp — in tension with observed dwarf galaxy cores ("cusp-core problem").
  1. Dirac fermion DM: Several extensions propose dark matter as a Dirac fermion. Recent theoretical work on hidden-ordered Dirac fermions and non-Hermitian Dirac systems (arXiv, June 2026) explores exotic fermion structures that may inform model-building for fermionic dark matter candidates, particularly in scenarios where emergent quasiparticles mimic massive neutral fermions.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Direct detection experiments (LUX-ZEPLIN, XENONnT, PandaX) continue to probe WIMP-nucleon cross-sections without a confirmed signal, steadily squeezing the WIMP parameter space. Axion searches (ADMX, HAYSTAC) are gaining sensitivity in the $\mu$eV range. Modified gravity alternatives (MOND, TeVeS, entropic gravity) remain observationally incomplete — no single alternative accounts for all evidence simultaneously, leaving CDM as the default framework even among skeptics.
