Game Semantics✓ reviewed

Logic · Semantics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Game semantics reinterprets logical truth not as static set-theoretic satisfaction, but as the existence of a winning strategy in an adversarial dialogue game. A formula $\varphi$ is "true" or "valid" iff a designated player — the Proponent (P) or Verifier — has a winning strategy against the Opponent (O) or Falsifier, regardless of O's moves.

INFO
Formal kernel. Given formula $\varphi$, the game $\mathcal{G}(\varphi)$ is defined inductively on the structure of $\varphi$: - $\varphi \land \psi$: O chooses which conjunct to play next. - $\varphi \lor \psi$: P chooses. - $\neg \varphi$: roles swap; play continues in $\mathcal{G}(\varphi)$. - $\exists x.\, \varphi(x)$: P provides a witness $t$; play continues in $\mathcal{G}(\varphi(t))$. - $\forall x.\, \varphi(x)$: O chooses the witness. P wins an atomic position iff the atom is true in the underlying model.

A winning strategy for P is a function from game histories to P-moves such that every play consistent with the strategy ends in a P-win. $\varphi$ is valid iff such a strategy exists. This immediately connects semantic truth to constructive content: a proof of $\exists x.\,\varphi(x)$ must exhibit the witness.

Two major traditions diverge here:

  • Dialogical Logic (Lorenzen & Lorenz, 1950s–60s): rules are purely syntactic; no background model. Validity = P has a formal winning strategy.
  • Game-Theoretical Semantics / GTS (Hintikka, 1960s–): a model $\mathcal{M}$ is fixed; games are evaluation games over $\mathcal{M}$.

Why It Matters

Game semantics is more than a curiosity — it fills genuine gaps left by Tarskian model theory.

  1. Intuitionistic and substructural logics. The Curry-Howard correspondence tells us proofs are programs, but what are proofs of? Game semantics gives a direct answer: a proof is a P-strategy. This is especially clean for linear logic (Girard), where structural rules (contraction, weakening) correspond precisely to game resources.
  1. Full abstraction for programming languages. Abramsky, Jagadeesan, Malacaria, and Hyland & Ong (mid-1990s) used game semantics to solve the long-open full abstraction problem for PCF (a typed $\lambda$-calculus with recursion). A denotational model is fully abstract iff contextual equivalence ⟺ semantic equality; game models achieve this where Scott domains famously failed.
  1. Concurrency and interaction. Concurrent computation is inherently dialogic. Game models naturally handle the interleaving of moves between components, making them a natural fit for process calculi and session types.
TIP
Game semantics makes the compositionality of interaction structurally explicit: composing two strategies (via "hiding" internal moves) corresponds directly to cut-elimination in proof theory and to linking components in a hardware or software system.

Key Details

  • Strategies as morphisms. In the Abramsky–Hyland categorical framework, arenas (game types) are objects and strategies (P's behavior) are morphisms. Composition is defined by parallel play plus hiding — a precise categorical structure (compact closed, or *-autonomous categories for linear logic).
  • Innocence and history-freeness. A strategy is innocent if P's move depends only on the current O-move and P's own prior moves, not O's full history. Innocence characterizes definability in higher-order functional languages.
  • Positionality vs. history-dependence. Classical logic admits positional (memoryless) winning strategies for certain games; intuitionistic logic typically requires history-sensitivity, reflecting the constructive content of proofs.
  • Independence-Friendly (IF) logic. Hintikka extended GTS to IF logic, where $\forall / \exists$ quantifiers can be informationally independent — P cannot see all of O's prior choices. This captures imperfect information and yields a logic strictly beyond first-order expressibility.
  • Complexity classes via games. Polynomial-time strategies in evaluation games characterize complexity classes: e.g., PTIME ↔ strategies computable in polynomial time. This gives a game-semantic account of descriptive complexity.
WARNING
Note: this information may be dated — active research into game semantics for quantum computing, probabilistic languages, and higher-order model checking is moving quickly; specific claims about open problems may no longer hold.

Riemannian Geometry✓ reviewed

Math · Geometry  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Riemannian geometry is the study of smooth manifolds equipped with a Riemannian metric — a smoothly varying inner product on each tangent space.

INFO: Formal Definition
A Riemannian manifold is a pair $(M, g)$ where $M$ is a smooth manifold and $g$ is a smooth section of the symmetric tensor bundle $T^*M \otimes T^*M$ such that $g_p : T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}$ is a positive-definite inner product for every $p \in M$.

The metric $g$ induces local structure: the length of a curve $\gamma : [a,b] \to M$ is

$$L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}\!\left(\dot\gamma(t),\, \dot\gamma(t)\right)}\; dt$$

and geodesics — length-minimizing curves — satisfy the geodesic equation

$$\ddot\gamma^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\,\dot\gamma^i\,\dot\gamma^j = 0$$

where $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ are the Christoffel symbols of the Levi-Civita connection, uniquely determined by compatibility with $g$ and torsion-freeness.

Curvature is encoded in the Riemann curvature tensor $R^l{}_{kij}$, which measures the failure of parallel transport to commute. Its contractions give the Ricci tensor $R_{ij}$ and scalar curvature $R$.

Why It Matters

Riemannian geometry is the mathematical backbone of general relativity: spacetime is a pseudo-Riemannian manifold (signature $(-,+,+,+)$), and Einstein's field equations

$$G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}$$

are purely statements about its curvature. Beyond physics, the framework drives:

  • Global analysis (index theorems, spectral geometry on manifolds)
  • Geometric group theory (symmetric spaces, lattices)
  • Machine learning (optimisation on manifolds, information geometry)
  • Topology (soul theorem, Sphere theorem, Perelman's proof of the Poincaré conjecture via Ricci flow)
TIP: Ricci Flow as a Canonical Smoothing
The Ricci flow $\partial_t g_{ij} = -2R_{ij}$ can be thought of as a nonlinear heat equation on the space of metrics — it diffuses curvature, driving a manifold toward constant curvature. This is why it could resolve the Geometrisation Conjecture.

Key Details

  1. Levi-Civita uniqueness. Among all affine connections on $(M,g)$, exactly one is torsion-free and metric-compatible. This uniqueness is what makes the Riemannian framework so rigid and powerful.
  1. Sectional curvature controls topology. Comparison theorems (Bonnet–Myers, Cartan–Hadamard, Toponogov) relate the sign of sectional curvature $K$ to global topology: $K > 0$ forces finite $\pi_1$; $K \leq 0$ makes the universal cover diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$.
  1. Geodesic completeness vs. metric completeness. By the Hopf–Rinow theorem, for connected Riemannian manifolds these are equivalent, and further equivalent to any two points being joinable by a minimising geodesic.
  1. Exponential map and normal coordinates. The map $\exp_p : T_pM \to M$ is a local diffeomorphism near the origin; in normal coordinates, $g_{ij}(p) = \delta_{ij}$ and $\Gamma^k_{ij}(p) = 0$, isolating curvature effects to second order.
  1. Intrinsic vs. extrinsic. Gauss's Theorema Egregium showed that Gaussian curvature of a surface is intrinsic — determined by $g$ alone, not by how the surface sits in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Riemannian geometry fully abstracts away the ambient space.

Spectral Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Analysis  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Spectral theory generalizes the eigenvalue/eigenvector decomposition of finite-dimensional matrices to linear operators acting on infinite-dimensional spaces — chiefly Hilbert and Banach spaces. The spectrum of an operator $T$ on a Banach space $X$ is $$\sigma(T) = \{\lambda \in \mathbb{C} : (T - \lambda I) \text{ is not boundedly invertible}\}$$ Its complement $\rho(T) = \mathbb{C} \setminus \sigma(T)$ is the resolvent set, and the resolvent operator $R(\lambda, T) = (T - \lambda I)^{-1}$ is analytic on $\rho(T)$.

Unlike the finite-dimensional case, the spectrum decomposes into three disjoint parts:

  • Point spectrum $\sigma_p(T)$: $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue (kernel of $T - \lambda I$ nontrivial)
  • Continuous spectrum $\sigma_c(T)$: $T - \lambda I$ injective with dense but non-closed range
  • Residual spectrum $\sigma_r(T)$: $T - \lambda I$ injective with range not dense

For self-adjoint operators on Hilbert spaces, $\sigma(T) \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ and the residual spectrum vanishes entirely — the spectral theorem then provides a projection-valued measure $E$ such that:

$$T = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda \, dE(\lambda)$$

Why It Matters

Spectral theory is the mathematical backbone of quantum mechanics — observables are self-adjoint operators, and their spectra are the possible measurement outcomes. Beyond physics, it underpins:

  • PDE analysis: Understanding the Laplacian's spectrum controls heat diffusion, wave propagation, and stability of equilibria
  • Numerical linear algebra: Iterative solvers, preconditioning, and convergence rates are all spectral phenomena
  • Data science / ML: PCA, graph Laplacians, and kernel methods are finite-rank approximations to infinite-dimensional spectral problems
  • Dynamical systems: Koopman operator theory lifts nonlinear dynamics into a linear (spectral) framework on function spaces
TIP: Analytic functions are central, not peripheral
The resolvent $R(\lambda, T)$ is a Banach-space-valued analytic function on $\rho(T)$. Nearly all spectral theory can be recovered from complex analysis applied to this function — the spectrum is literally the set of singularities of the resolvent, making Cauchy's theorem, Laurent expansions, and contour integrals essential tools.

Key Details

  1. Spectral radius formula: For any bounded operator, $r(T) = \lim_{n\to\infty} \|T^n\|^{1/n} = \sup_{\lambda \in \sigma(T)} |\lambda|$. This links iteration behavior directly to spectral geometry.
  1. Compact operators: If $T$ is compact on an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, then $0 \in \sigma(T)$ always, and any nonzero spectral point is an isolated eigenvalue of finite multiplicity — a structure much closer to finite dimensions.
  1. Functional calculus: For a normal operator $T$, one can define $f(T)$ for any bounded Borel function $f : \sigma(T) \to \mathbb{C}$, extending polynomial calculus in a spectrally consistent way. This is the rigorous foundation of expressions like $e^{itH}$ in quantum evolution.
  1. Unbounded operators: Physically relevant operators (position, momentum, Hamiltonians) are typically unbounded and only densely defined. Self-adjointness must be carefully distinguished from mere symmetry; self-adjoint extensions (von Neumann theory) govern whether a spectral theorem applies.
  1. Pseudospectrum: For non-normal operators, $\sigma(T)$ can be wildly sensitive to perturbations. The $\epsilon$-pseudospectrum $\sigma_\epsilon(T) = \{\lambda : \|R(\lambda,T)\| > \epsilon^{-1}\}$ gives a more physically honest picture of spectral sensitivity.

Recent ML work (e.g., metric similarity analysis using Riemannian tools) repurposes these ideas to compare learned neural representations by their intrinsic spectral geometry — treating representation spaces as statistical manifolds.
  • Koopman spectral methods: Data-driven algorithms (DMD, EDMD) approximate infinite-dimensional Koopman spectra from trajectory data, connecting spectral theory to nonlinear dynamical systems and control.
  • Random matrix theory: Spectral statistics of large random matrices (GUE, Wigner semicircle) have deep connections to number theory, quantum chaos, and the universality phenomenon.
  • Non-self-adjoint and non-normal operators: Classical theory assumed self-adjointness. Modern PDE and control theory increasingly demands spectral tools for operators with no such symmetry, where pseudospectra and resolvent estimates are central.

Materials Science✓ reviewed

Science · Physics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Materials science studies the relationships between structure, properties, processing, and performance of matter — the canonical four-corner paradigm that organizes the entire discipline:

$$\text{Processing} \rightarrow \text{Structure} \rightarrow \text{Properties} \rightarrow \text{Performance}$$

Structure is understood across multiple length scales: atomic bonding and crystal lattice (~Å), defects and grain boundaries (~nm–μm), and macroscopic microstructure (~mm). Each scale feeds into observable properties — mechanical, electrical, thermal, optical, magnetic.

INFO: Defining Insight
The same element or compound can exhibit radically different properties depending solely on processing history. Diamond and graphite are both pure carbon; their divergent properties arise from bonding geometry alone ($sp^3$ tetrahedral vs. $sp^2$ planar). Processing selects structure; structure dictates behavior.

Why It Matters

Materials science is the substrate on which most of engineering runs. It underpins:

  • Aerospace & civil structures — fatigue life, fracture toughness, creep resistance
  • Semiconductors & electronics — band-gap engineering, doping profiles, thin-film deposition
  • Biomaterials — implant osseointegration, biocompatibility, controlled drug release
  • Energy systems — electrode kinetics in batteries, thermal barrier coatings, photovoltaic absorbers
  • Forensic / failure analysis — determining root causes of catastrophic failures (e.g., stress-corrosion cracking in aviation components)
TIP: Cross-disciplinary leverage
Because materials science borrows machinery from quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and continuum mechanics simultaneously, advances here often propagate upstream into chemistry and physics. High-temperature superconductors, for instance, forced condensed-matter theorists to revisit BCS theory.

Key Details

1. Crystal Defects Are Features, Not Bugs

Point defects (vacancies, interstitials), line defects (dislocations), and planar defects (grain boundaries, stacking faults) largely control yield strength, diffusivity, and conductivity. The Hall–Petch relation captures grain-boundary strengthening:

$$\sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k_y \, d^{-1/2}$$

where $d$ is mean grain diameter. Smaller grains → more boundaries → higher yield stress.

2. Phase Diagrams as Thermodynamic Maps

Phase diagrams encode equilibrium stable phases as a function of composition and temperature. The lever rule quantifies phase fractions in two-phase regions. Non-equilibrium processing (quenching, rapid solidification) traps metastable phases — the basis of martensite in steel and metallic glasses.

3. Diffusion Controls Kinetics

Most solid-state transformations are diffusion-limited. Fick's second law:

$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \nabla^2 C$$

where $D = D_0 \exp(-Q/RT)$ is thermally activated. This governs carburization, sintering, and dopant drive-in in semiconductors.

4. Structure–Property Duality in Electronic Materials

Band theory extends periodic potential quantum mechanics to crystalline solids. Band gap $E_g$ determines optical absorption edge and intrinsic carrier concentration $n_i \propto \exp(-E_g / 2k_BT)$. Strain engineering (epitaxial mismatch) tunes $E_g$ in III–V heterostructures.

5. Fracture Mechanics as a Materials Property

Griffith's criterion links fracture stress to crack size:

$$\sigma_f = \sqrt{\frac{2 E \gamma_s}{\pi a}}$$

Critical stress intensity factor $K_{Ic}$ (fracture toughness) is an intrinsic material property used in damage-tolerant design.
