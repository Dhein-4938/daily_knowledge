Mathematical Optimization✓ reviewed

Math · Optimization  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Mathematical optimization is the systematic selection of a best element from a feasible set according to some objective criterion. The canonical form is:

$$ \min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}} \; f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{subject to} \quad g_i(\mathbf{x}) \leq 0, \; h_j(\mathbf{x}) = 0 $$

where $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is the objective function, $\mathcal{X}$ the feasible set, and $g_i, h_j$ define inequality and equality constraints respectively. Maximization is equivalent by negating $f$.

INFO: Two Primary Subfields
- Continuous optimization — $\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$; leverages calculus, geometry, and analysis - Discrete optimization — $\mathcal{X}$ is countable (e.g., integer programs, combinatorial structures); often NP-hard

Why It Matters

Optimization is the lingua franca of quantitative reasoning. It underlies:

  • Machine learning: training a neural network is minimizing a loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ over parameters $\theta$
  • Control theory: optimal control via Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman Equation or Pontryagin's minimum principle
  • Economics: utility maximization, equilibrium finding, mechanism design
  • Operations research: scheduling, routing, resource allocation
  • Physics: variational principles — mechanics, field theory, and thermodynamics all emerge from extremizing functionals
TIP: Duality as a Unifying Lens
The Lagrangian Duality framework transforms constrained problems into unconstrained ones via multipliers, and strong duality (zero gap) reveals deep geometric structure. Many apparently different algorithms — SVM training, interior-point methods, message passing — are Lagrangian dual ascent in disguise.

Key Details

1. Convexity is the key structural divide.

A problem is convex when $f$ is convex and $\mathcal{X}$ is a convex set. Any local minimum is then global, and strong optimality conditions (KKT) are both necessary and sufficient. Convex problems are tractable in theory and practice; non-convex ones are generally hard.

$$ f(\lambda x + (1-\lambda)y) \leq \lambda f(x) + (1-\lambda) f(y), \quad \lambda \in [0,1] $$

2. First- and second-order conditions.

A smooth interior point $x^*$ satisfies the first-order necessary condition $\nabla f(x^*) = 0$. The second-order condition requires $\nabla^2 f(x^*) \succeq 0$ (positive semidefinite Hessian) for a local minimum. For constrained problems, these generalize via KKT multipliers.

3. Gradient descent and its variants.

The workhorse of large-scale optimization is the update $\mathbf{x}_{t+1} = \mathbf{x}_t - \eta_t \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_t)$. Convergence rates depend critically on the condition number $\kappa = L/\mu$ (ratio of Lipschitz smoothness to strong-convexity). Accelerated (Nesterov) methods achieve $O(1/t^2)$ vs. $O(1/t)$ for vanilla gradient descent.

4. Combinatorial hardness and approximation.

Discrete problems (TSP, max-cut, set cover) are often NP-hard. The theory of Approximation Algorithms studies polynomially-computable solutions guaranteed to be within factor $\alpha$ of optimal; SDP relaxations and LP relaxations are the primary tools.

5. Stochastic and online variants.

When $f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbb{E}_\xi[\ell(\mathbf{x}, \xi)]$, stochastic gradient descent (SGD) replaces the full gradient with noisy estimates. Convergence is noisier but per-iteration cost drops from $O(n)$ to $O(1)$, enabling modern deep learning at scale.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Non-convex landscape theory: understanding why SGD finds good minima in overparameterized networks despite non-convexity — saddle-point avoidance, loss landscape geometry
  • Diffusion models as optimization: recent generative models (e.g., diffusion-based motion synthesis) frame sampling as iterative denoising, with explicit optimization steps enforcing kinematic or geometric constraints during the diffusion trajectory
  • Second-order methods at scale: Shampoo, K-FAC, and distributed quasi-Newton methods are making Hessian-aware optimization practical for LLMs
  • Bilevel optimization: problems where the objective depends on the solution of another optimization problem — prominent in meta-learning and hyperparameter tuning

  1. The duality gap as information. In non-convex settings, the Lagrangian dual provides a lower bound but strong duality may fail. How should one interpret a large duality gap — as a hardness certificate, a modeling failure, or an algorithmic limitation?

Lambda Calculus✓ reviewed

Logic · Computation Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
Lambda calculus (λ-calculus) is a formal system for expressing computation via function abstraction and application. Every program is either a variable, an abstraction, or an application — nothing else.

The full grammar fits in three lines:

$$ e \;::=\; x \;\mid\; \lambda x.\, e \;\mid\; e_1\; e_2 $$

where $x$ ranges over variables, $\lambda x.\, e$ binds $x$ in body $e$, and $e_1\; e_2$ applies $e_1$ to argument $e_2$.

Computation proceeds by β-reduction: substituting the argument for the bound variable.

$$ (\lambda x.\, e_1)\; e_2 \;\longrightarrow_\beta\; e_1[x \mapsto e_2] $$

A term with no further β-redexes is in β-normal form. An additional rule, η-reduction ($\lambda x.\, f\, x \longrightarrow_\eta f$ when $x \notin \text{FV}(f)$), captures extensional function equality.

Church introduced the untyped λ-calculus in the 1930s while investigating the foundations of mathematics; a logically consistent fragment was documented by 1940.

Why It Matters

Lambda calculus is Turing-complete — any Turing machine computation can be encoded in it, and vice versa. This makes it the minimal universal model of computation, smaller and more mathematically tractable than Turing machines.

Its influence is enormous:

  • Programming languages — Lisp, ML, Haskell, and most functional languages are essentially typed or untyped λ-calculi with syntactic sugar.
  • Type theory — the Curry-Howard correspondence maps λ-calculus terms to proofs and types to propositions, bridging logic and computation.
  • Denotational semantics — programs are modelled as mathematical functions, formalizing meaning independent of any machine.
  • Proof assistants — Coq, Agda, and Lean are all built on dependent type theories that extend λ-calculus.
TIP: Curry-Howard at a glance
A typed λ-term $t : A \to B$ is a proof that proposition $A$ implies $B$. Function application is modus ponens. This correspondence runs deep enough that proof checking and type checking are literally the same algorithm.

Key Details

1. Capture-avoiding substitution is non-trivial.

Naïve substitution breaks under variable shadowing. $(\lambda y.\, \lambda x.\, y)\; x$ must rename the bound $x$ before substituting, yielding $\lambda z.\, x$, not the broken $\lambda x.\, x$.

2. Fixed-point combinators enable recursion.

The untyped calculus has no primitive recursion, yet the Y combinator

$$Y \;=\; \lambda f.\,(\lambda x.\, f\,(x\,x))\,(\lambda x.\, f\,(x\,x))$$

satisfies $Y\, f \longrightarrow_\beta f\,(Y\, f)$, encoding arbitrary recursion. Notably, $Y$ cannot be typed in the simply-typed λ-calculus, which rules out non-termination by design.

3. Church encodings represent data as functions.

Booleans: $\mathbf{true} = \lambda t.\, \lambda f.\, t$, $\mathbf{false} = \lambda t.\, \lambda f.\, f$. Church numerals: $\overline{n} = \lambda f.\, \lambda x.\, f^n\, x$. Every data structure is ultimately a higher-order function.

4. Confluence (Church-Rosser theorem).

If $e \twoheadrightarrow_\beta e_1$ and $e \twoheadrightarrow_\beta e_2$, there exists $e_3$ with $e_1 \twoheadrightarrow_\beta e_3$ and $e_2 \twoheadrightarrow_\beta e_3$. Normal forms are unique when they exist, but not all terms terminate.

5. Reduction strategy matters.

Normal-order (leftmost-outermost) reduction finds a normal form whenever one exists; applicative-order (innermost-first, as in most languages) can diverge on terms that do have normal forms. Lazy evaluation is essentially call-by-need, a memoized normal-order strategy.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active research lives in the typed extensions:

  • Dependent types (Martin-Löf, CoC) unify types and terms, enabling proof-carrying code.
  • Linear and affine type systems (relevant to Rust Ownership Model) track resource usage at the type level, deriving from linear logic.
  • Gradual typing mixes static and dynamic guarantees in a single λ-calculus framework.
  • Optimal reduction (Lévy, Lamping) minimises β-steps by sharing redexes via interaction nets — still an area of implementation research.

Type Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO
A type theory is a formal system in which every term carries a type, and well-formedness of expressions is governed by typing rules. At its core, it is an alternative to set theory as a foundation for mathematics — one where membership (∈) is replaced by inhabitation (∶).

The central judgment is:

$$\Gamma \vdash t : T$$

read "in context $\Gamma$, term $t$ has type $T$." A context $\Gamma$ is an ordered list of variable-type bindings $(x_1 : T_1, \ldots, x_n : T_n)$. Type theories specify which such judgments are derivable via inference rules — the mechanical engine of the whole system.

Two canonical pillars:

  • Church's Simply Typed λ-Calculus (STLC) — introduces function types $A \to B$; terms are λ-abstractions and applications. The type system prevents paradoxes like untyped self-application.
  • Martin-Löf Type Theory (MLTT / Intuitionistic TT) — adds dependent types, where types can depend on terms: $\prod_{x:A} B(x)$ (dependent function) and $\sum_{x:A} B(x)$ (dependent pair). This collapses the proposition/type distinction into the Curry–Howard correspondence: propositions are types, proofs are terms.

Why It Matters

Type theory is simultaneously a foundation for mathematics, a logic, and a programming language theory. This triple role is unique among foundational systems.

TIP
The Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence extends the duality further: types ↔ propositions ↔ objects in a category. A proof of $A \Rightarrow B$ is a λ-term of type $A \to B$ is a morphism $A \to B$ in a Cartesian closed category. This is not metaphor — it is a precise equivalence.

Proof assistants like Coq, Agda, Lean 4, and Rocq are all grounded in dependent type theories. The Curry–Howard lens means writing a program of a given type is constructing a mathematical proof. Correctness-by-construction becomes literal.

In programming languages, type theory underpins:

  • Hindley–Milner type inference (ML, Haskell)
  • Subtyping and object-oriented systems
  • Linear/affine types for resource management (Rust's borrow checker)
  • Effect systems for tracking computational side-effects

Key Details

  1. Dependent vs. Simple Types. In STLC, types and terms live in separate universes. In dependent type theory, types are first-class — $\text{Vec}(A, n)$ is a type parameterised by value $n : \mathbb{N}$. This expressivity is what enables formal verification.
  1. Universes and the Universe Hierarchy. Naïve type-in-type ($\text{Type} : \text{Type}$) leads to Girard's paradox (analog of Russell's). The resolution is a universe hierarchy $\mathcal{U}_0 : \mathcal{U}_1 : \mathcal{U}_2 : \cdots$, with universe polymorphism to avoid duplication.
  1. Intensional vs. Extensional Identity. Intensional type theories (MLTT) distinguish definitional equality (reduction) from propositional equality (inhabited identity type $\text{Id}_A(a,b)$). Extensional theories collapse these, gaining expressivity but losing decidable type-checking — a critical tradeoff.
  1. Calculus of Inductive Constructions (CIC). Coquand and Huet's CIC underpins Coq/Rocq. It merges the Calculus of Constructions (impredicative $\text{Prop}$, universe polymorphism) with inductive types — the mechanism for defining $\mathbb{N}$, lists, trees, and the recursion/induction principles that come with them.
  1. Proof Irrelevance and Squashing. Distinguishing data from mere proofs matters for extraction and computation. The $\|-\|$ truncation (or $\text{Prop}$ in CIC) collapses all proofs of a proposition to a single irrelevant witness, essential for separating logical content from computational content.

Active frontiers include:

  • Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) and Cubical Type Theory — Identity types are reinterpreted as paths in a space; the Univalence Axiom ($A \simeq B \Rightarrow A = B$) makes equivalent structures interchangeable. Cubical type theory (as in Agda's --cubical mode) gives a computational interpretation of univalence, avoiding the non-constructivity of the original HoTT book.
  • Directed / Modal Type Theory — Extending HoTT to directed homotopies (morphisms, not just paths) for ∞-category theory; modal operators ($\Box$, $\bigcirc$) for controlling access to resources or dimensions.
  • Two-Level Type Theory (2LTT) — Combining a strict metatheory with an inner homotopy layer, resolving some coherence nightmares in synthetic ∞-category theory.
  • Linear and Graded Type Theories — Tracking resource usage at the type level, with applications in quantum computing (no-cloning theorem enforced by linear types) and differential privacy.

  1. Computation vs. Logic: CIC's Prop impredicativity is justified logically but causes inconsistency if used carelessly with classical axioms. Where should a proof assistant draw the line between a logical system and a programming language foundation?

Curry-Howard Correspondence✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO: Definition
The Curry-Howard Correspondence is a deep structural isomorphism between systems of formal logic and typed computational calculi: propositions are types, proofs are programs, and proof normalization is computation (β-reduction).

The correspondence maps logical constructs to type-theoretic ones with precise fidelity:

| Logic | Type Theory |

|---|---|

| Proposition $A$ | Type $A$ |

| Proof of $A$ | Term $t : A$ |

| $A \Rightarrow B$ | Function type $A \to B$ |

| $A \wedge B$ | Product type $A \times B$ |

| $A \vee B$ | Sum type $A + B$ |

| $\bot$ (false) | Empty type $\mathbf{0}$ |

| $\top$ (true) | Unit type $\mathbf{1}$ |

| $\forall x{:}A.\ P(x)$ | Dependent product $\Pi_{x:A}\ P(x)$ |

| $\exists x{:}A.\ P(x)$ | Dependent sum $\Sigma_{x:A}\ P(x)$ |

To inhabit a type is to prove the corresponding proposition. A program that typechecks is a proof; a proof that normalizes is a terminating computation. The logical cut-elimination rule and β-reduction are the same thing, viewed through different lenses.

The isomorphism is grounded in intuitionistic (constructive) logic — classical logic with the Law of Excluded Middle ($A \vee \neg A$) corresponds to type systems with call/cc (continuations), a non-trivial and somewhat uncomfortable extension.

Why It Matters

The correspondence isn't a curiosity — it collapses three previously separate disciplines:

  • Programming languages: type systems gain logical meaning; well-typed programs are correctness certificates.
  • Proof theory: proofs become executable objects; proof assistants (Coq, Agda, Lean) exploit this directly.
  • Mathematics foundations: mathematical theorems can be run; existence proofs yield algorithms.

The three-way Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence extends the picture: categories provide a third column, with objects as types/propositions and morphisms as programs/proofs. This unification is the backbone of categorical logic and topos theory.

TIP: Practical Payoff
Every time a Haskell type signature encodes a contract, or Lean verifies a proof by constructing a term, the correspondence is doing real work. Dependent types (Idris, Agda) push this further: the value of a term can appear in the type of another, allowing full program specifications as types.

Key Details

1. Normalization = computation.

Proof normalization (cutting out detours in a proof) corresponds exactly to β-reduction. Strong normalization of a type theory implies all programs terminate — which is why total languages like Agda reject general recursion.

2. Classical logic requires control operators.

Adding $\neg\neg A \Rightarrow A$ (double-negation elimination) to the type theory requires delimited continuations or call/cc. Parigot's λμ-calculus and Griffin's observation (1990) made this precise. Classical proofs correspond to programs that can inspect and manipulate the call stack.

3. Linear logic yields linear types.

Girard's linear logic ($A \multimap B$, resources used exactly once) corresponds to linear type systems — where a value cannot be duplicated or discarded. This is the logical foundation of Rust's ownership/borrow checker.

4. The correspondence is syntax-sensitive.

It lives at the level of proof terms, not just provability. Two different proofs of the same theorem are genuinely different programs with potentially different computational behavior. This is why proof relevance vs. irrelevance matters in dependent type theory.

5. Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) extends it further.

In HoTT, types are spaces, terms are points, proofs of equality are paths, and higher-dimensional structure (homotopies) corresponds to proof transformations. The Univalence Axiom states that equivalent types are equal — a statement with no direct classical analogue.

$$\text{Univalence:}\ (A \simeq B) \simeq (A =_{\mathcal{U}} B)$$

Active research threads as of early 2026:

  • Cubical type theory (cubical Agda, Redtt): constructive models of HoTT that make Univalence computable, resolving the prior situation where the axiom blocked computation.
  • Quantitative type theory (QTT): tracking resource usage in types, merging linear and dependent typing — foundation of Idris 2.
  • Proof mining: extracting computational content (algorithms, bounds) from classical mathematical proofs via the correspondence, used actively in numerical analysis.
  • Propositions-as-sessions: extending the correspondence to session types for concurrent processes, where propositions in linear logic correspond to communication protocols.
