Homotopy Type Theory✓ reviewed

Logic · Foundations  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) is a foundational framework for mathematics that interprets types not merely as sets or propositions, but as spaces in the sense of homotopy theory — with elements as points, equalities as paths, and higher equalities as homotopies between paths.

INFO
The central insight: in Martin-Löf dependent type theory, the identity type $\text{Id}_A(a, b)$ (proofs that $a = b$ in type $A$) is reinterpreted as the path space between points $a$ and $b$ in a topological space. This transforms equality from a flat, propositional relation into a structured, higher-dimensional object.

The key axiom distinguishing HoTT from standard type theory is the Univalence Axiom (due to Voevodsky):

$$\text{Univalence}: (A \simeq B) \simeq (A =_{\mathcal{U}} B)$$

That is, an equivalence of types $A \simeq B$ is itself equivalent to a path between $A$ and $B$ in the universe $\mathcal{U}$. Equivalences become the same thing as identifications.

Higher Inductive Types (HITs) extend this: you can define a type by specifying not just its point constructors but also its path constructors. The circle $S^1$, for instance, is the HIT with one point base : S^1 and one path loop : base = base.

Why It Matters

HoTT sits at a remarkable intersection:

  • Foundations of mathematics: It provides an alternative to ZFC set theory where homotopy-invariant structure is native, not encoded. Mathematical objects behave correctly under equivalence by construction.
  • Proof assistants: HoTT has been formalized in Agda, Coq, and Lean. Proofs are programs; the univalence axiom makes transport along equivalences automatic rather than tedious.
  • ∞-category theory: HoTT serves as the internal language of (∞,1)-toposes. Results proved synthetically in HoTT hold in any such topos simultaneously.
  • Computer science: Dependent types with univalence give a principled account of abstraction boundaries — isomorphic implementations are provably interchangeable.
TIP
Univalence enforces "representation independence" for free. If two data structures are provably equivalent, any theorem about one transfers to the other via transport. This is the formal analogue of the informal mathematical practice of treating isomorphic objects as identical.

Key Details

  1. $n$-types and truncation: Types are stratified by homotopy complexity. A proposition (mere $(-1)$-type) has at most one path between any two points. A set (0-type) has contractible path spaces. Higher $n$-types carry non-trivial homotopy up to level $n$. The truncation operator $\|{-}\|_n$ projects any type down.
  1. Path induction: The eliminator for identity types — J — says that to prove a property of all paths, it suffices to prove it for reflexivity. This is exactly the statement that the path space $\text{Id}_A(a,-)$ is contractible (has a as a center of contraction).
  1. Cubical type theory: Classical HoTT with the Univalence Axiom is not computationally satisfying — transport along univalence has no canonical reduction. Cubical type theory (CCHM, XTT, Cartesian cubical) solves this by building paths from first principles using an interval type, making univalence provable and computational.
  1. Synthetic homotopy theory: HoTT lets you calculate homotopy groups of spaces purely type-theoretically. The classical result $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ has a fully formal proof in HoTT requiring no point-set topology.
  1. Propositional vs. definitional equality: HoTT sharply distinguishes $a = b$ (a type, possibly inhabited by multiple paths) from $a \equiv b$ (definitional/judgmental equality, a metatheoretic assertion). Conflating these is a common source of confusion.

Current State

WARNING
Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include: completing Agda's cubical library and the HoTT-relevant parts of Lean 4's Mathlib; formalizing condensed mathematics and synthetic algebraic geometry in cohesive HoTT; and resolving the coherence problem for semi-simplicial types (defining Kan complexes internally without assuming univalence). The HoTT Book (IAS, 2013) remains the canonical reference, but cubical developments have substantially moved the computational story forward.

Synthetic Biology✓ reviewed

Science · Biotechnology  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

INFO
Synthetic Biology (SynBio) applies the engineering paradigm — abstraction, modularity, and standardisation — to biological systems. The goal is to design and construct novel biological parts, devices, and systems, or to redesign existing natural systems with predictable, robust, and useful behaviours.

The field borrows its conceptual vocabulary directly from electrical engineering: parts (promoters, RBS sequences, terminators) compose into devices (toggle switches, oscillators), which compose into systems (metabolic pathways, whole-chassis organisms). This hierarchy mirrors the abstraction stack from transistors to logic gates to CPUs.

A central formalism is the use of ordinary differential equations to model gene regulatory networks. For a simple activator-repressor circuit:

$$ \frac{d[A]}{dt} = \frac{\alpha_A}{1 + ([R]/K_R)^n} - \delta_A [A] $$

where $\alpha_A$ is max transcription rate, $K_R$ is repressor binding affinity, $n$ is the Hill coefficient (cooperativity), and $\delta_A$ is the degradation rate. Tuning these parameters is the SynBio equivalent of setting resistor values.

Why It Matters

SynBio sits at the intersection of a remarkable number of high-impact domains:

  • Medicine: engineered CAR-T cells, programmable bacteria for tumour microenvironment sensing, and biosensors for diagnostics.
  • Materials: biologically synthesised polymers, living materials with self-repair, and spider-silk proteins expressed in heterologous hosts.
  • Sustainability: microbial production of biofuels, carbon capture pathways, and replacing petroleum-derived chemicals with bio-fermentation.
  • Computing: DNA-based data storage (theoretical density ~$10^{18}$ bytes/g) and biological logic circuits for in vivo computing.
TIP
The ribosome is already a molecular assembler — it reads a linear tape (mRNA) and produces arbitrary 3D polymer structures. SynBio's deeper ambition is to co-opt and extend this machinery rather than build nanomachines from scratch, exploiting 3.8 billion years of optimisation.

Key Details

  1. The BioBricks Standard: Parts are defined by flanking restriction sites (EcoRI, XbaI, SpeI, PstI), enabling combinatorial assembly. The Registry of Standard Biological Parts (~20,000 entries) is SynBio's equivalent of an open-source component library — though characterisation quality varies enormously.
  1. Chassis Organisms: E. coli, S. cerevisiae, and B. subtilis are canonical chassis, but minimal-genome organisms (e.g., JCVI-syn3.0, ~473 genes) offer reduced metabolic crosstalk for cleaner circuit behaviour.
  1. Retroactivity and Context-Dependence: Biological parts are not truly modular. Loading effects (retroactivity) mean a downstream device alters the dynamics of an upstream promoter — analogous to impedance mismatch. This is a core unsolved engineering challenge.
  1. Design-Build-Test-Learn (DBTL) Cycle: The iterative loop is accelerated by automated platforms (liquid-handling robots, high-throughput sequencing, machine learning-guided design), collapsing weeks-long cycles to days.
  1. Biosafety and Containment: Genetic firewalls — auxotrophic strains dependent on synthetic amino acids, or recoded genomes with non-canonical codons — prevent engineered organisms from persisting or exchanging genes outside the lab.
WARNING
Note: this information may be dated. The minimal-genome and synthetic chromosome projects are advancing rapidly; capability benchmarks from even 2–3 years ago may significantly understate current state of the art.

Current State

Active research frontiers as of mid-2026:

  • Cell-free systems: TX/TL (transcription-translation) systems enable rapid prototyping without live cells, and are being deployed for point-of-care diagnostics (e.g., SHERLOCK, DETECTR).
  • Whole-genome synthesis and recoding: Extending work on E. coli with all UAG codons reassigned, enabling sense-codon reassignment and expanded genetic alphabets.
  • Multicellular engineering: Moving from single-cell circuits to programmed morphogenesis — engineering developmental-biology-like differentiation programs in synthetic consortia.
  • AI-assisted design: AlphaFold-class structure prediction is being integrated into protein-part design pipelines, dramatically shortening the characterisation bottleneck.

Game Theory✓ reviewed

Math · Applied Mathematics  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

[!info] Definition

Game theory is the mathematical study of strategic interactions among rational agents, where each agent's outcome depends not only on their own choices but on the choices of others. Formally, a game in normal form is a triple $G = (N, A, u)$ where $N$ is a set of players, $A = A_1 \times \cdots \times A_n$ is the joint action space, and $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_n)$ is a vector of utility functions $u_i : A \to \mathbb{R}$.

The foundational solution concept is the Nash Equilibrium: a strategy profile $a^* \in A$ such that no player $i$ can increase their utility by unilaterally deviating:

$$u_i(a_i^*, a_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(a_i, a_{-i}^*) \quad \forall a_i \in A_i, \forall i \in N$$

Nash proved that every finite game admits at least one equilibrium in mixed strategies (probability distributions over actions), using Brouwer's fixed-point theorem — the same topological tool von Neumann used earlier for two-player zero-sum games.

Why It Matters

Game theory is the backbone of modern microeconomics, mechanism design, and auction theory — the latter earning Milgrom and Wilson the 2020 Nobel Prize for designing spectrum auctions worth hundreds of billions. Its reach is strikingly broad:

  • Economics: market competition, bargaining, contract theory, oligopoly pricing
  • Political science: voting systems, arms races, coalition formation
  • Biology: evolutionary stable strategies (Maynard Smith's ESS), predator-prey signaling
  • CS/AI: multi-agent reinforcement learning, adversarial ML, algorithmic mechanism design
  • Logic & philosophy: common knowledge, epistemic game theory

[!tip] The Deeper Insight

Game theory doesn't just model competition — it models any situation involving interdependence of decisions. Cooperation, signaling, reputation, and commitment are all game-theoretic phenomena. The framework unifies strategic reasoning across wildly different domains because the structure of interdependence, not the content, is what matters.

Key Details

1. Zero-sum vs. Non-zero-sum. In zero-sum games $\sum_i u_i(a) = 0$ for all $a$ — one player's gain is another's loss. The minimax theorem guarantees an optimal mixed strategy. Non-zero-sum games introduce cooperation, coordination failures, and the Pareto frontier as distinct from Nash equilibria.

2. Dominant Strategies & Iterated Elimination. A strategy $a_i$ strictly dominates $a_i'$ if $u_i(a_i, a_{-i}) > u_i(a_i', a_{-i})$ for all $a_{-i}$. Iteratively eliminating dominated strategies (IESDS) can solve games without equilibrium computation, but requires common knowledge of rationality — a strong epistemic assumption.

3. Repeated Games & Folk Theorems. In infinitely repeated games with discount factor $\delta \to 1$, virtually any individually rational payoff profile can be sustained as an equilibrium. This formalizes how cooperation and reputation emerge without central enforcement — the formal underpinning of why long-term relationships deter defection.

4. Mechanism Design (Reverse Game Theory). Rather than analyzing a given game, mechanism design asks: what rules induce desired outcomes among strategic agents? The revelation principle shows any implementable outcome can be achieved by a direct, incentive-compatible mechanism — agents truthfully report preferences.

5. Incomplete Information & Bayesian Games. When players have private information (types), the game becomes a Bayesian game. Equilibrium generalizes to a Bayes-Nash Equilibrium, where strategies are functions from types to actions. Harsanyi's type-space model handles arbitrary belief hierarchies.

Current State

[!warning] Note: this information may be dated

Active research fronts include:

  • Algorithmic game theory: computational complexity of finding Nash equilibria (PPAD-completeness of Nash), no-regret learning in games, and convergence of gradient-based dynamics
  • Multi-agent AI: game-theoretic foundations for LLM agents cooperating or competing, alignment under strategic behavior, and mechanism design for AI systems
  • Evolutionary and behavioral game theory: bounded rationality, quantal response equilibria, and empirical departures from classical predictions
  • Auction & market design: combinatorial auctions, matching markets (Roth, Shapley), and real-time bidding systems at internet scale

Elliptic Curves✓ reviewed

Math · Number Theory  ·  ~4 min read

Core Concept

An elliptic curve over a field $K$ (with $\text{char}(K) \neq 2, 3$) is a smooth, projective algebraic curve of genus one equipped with a distinguished point $O$. In affine coordinates it takes the Weierstrass form:

$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b, \quad a, b \in K$$

Non-singularity imposes the discriminant condition:

$$\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0$$

which rules out cusps and node self-intersections. The projective closure adds the unique point at infinity $O = [0:1:0]$, which serves as the group identity.

INFO: Genus vs. Ellipse
An elliptic curve is not an ellipse. The name descends from elliptic integrals (arc-length of an ellipse involves $\int dx/\sqrt{\text{cubic}}$). A conic (ellipse, parabola) has genus 0; an elliptic curve has genus 1 — the topological difference is fundamental.

The $j$-invariant classifies elliptic curves up to isomorphism over $\bar{K}$:

$$j(E) = -1728 \frac{(4a)^3}{\Delta/(-16)} = 1728 \frac{4a^3}{4a^3 + 27b^2}$$

Two curves over $\bar{K}$ are isomorphic iff they share the same $j$-invariant.

Why It Matters

Elliptic curves sit at the intersection of several deep mathematical worlds:

  • Number theory: Central to Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem via the Taniyama–Shimura–Weil conjecture (every rational elliptic curve is modular). The BSD conjecture — one of the Millennium Prize Problems — concerns the rank of $E(\mathbb{Q})$.
  • Cryptography (ECC): The discrete logarithm problem on $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is computationally harder per bit than RSA, enabling shorter keys with equivalent security (e.g., 256-bit ECC $\approx$ 3072-bit RSA).
  • Complex analysis: Over $\mathbb{C}$, every elliptic curve is isomorphic to a torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ for some lattice $\Lambda$. This identification is a group isomorphism — the addition law on $E$ is addition on the torus.
TIP: Abelian Variety of Dimension 1
Elliptic curves are the simplest non-trivial abelian varieties. Studying them provides the template for higher-dimensional generalizations (Jacobians, Shimura varieties) and makes the abstract machinery concrete.

Key Details

1. The Group Law (Chord-and-Tangent)

Given $P, Q \in E(K)$, draw the line through them; it meets $E$ at a third point $R'$. Reflect $R'$ over the $x$-axis to get $P + Q$. This is algebraically expressed by explicit rational formulas — no choice of coordinates involved — making it computable.

2. Torsion and Rank

For $K = \mathbb{Q}$, the Mordell–Weil theorem states $E(\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \oplus E(\mathbb{Q})_{\text{tors}}$. The torsion subgroup is classified (Mazur's theorem: at most 16 possibilities). The rank $r$ is the deep mystery — no algorithm proven to always compute it.

3. Reduction Modulo $p$

A curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ can be reduced mod primes $p$. If $\Delta \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, the curve has bad reduction (nodal or cuspidal singularity). Primes of good reduction feed the $L$-function $L(E,s)$, whose analytic properties encode the arithmetic of $E$.

4. Endomorphisms and CM

Generic curves have $\text{End}(E) \cong \mathbb{Z}$. Curves with complex multiplication (CM) have $\text{End}(E) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ an imaginary quadratic field — they are arithmetically special and appear in class field theory.

5. Weil Conjectures (Hasse Bound)

Over $\mathbb{F}_p$: $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) = p + 1 - t_p$ where $|t_p| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$ (Hasse's theorem, the genus-1 case of the Weil conjectures). The trace of Frobenius $t_p$ is the key local datum.

Current State

WARNING: Note: this information may be dated

Active frontiers include: (1) Langlands program — the modularity theorem for elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ is complete, but extensions to totally real fields and beyond are ongoing; (2) BSD conjecture — verified computationally for vast families but unproven in general; (3) post-quantum ECC — classical ECC is vulnerable to Shor's algorithm; isogeny-based schemes (e.g., CSIDH) leverage the structure of isogeny graphs between elliptic curves as a quantum-resistant replacement; (4) explicit Chabauty–Coleman methods for bounding rational points on curves of higher genus via elliptic curve machinery.

  1. Torus duality: Over $\mathbb{C}$, the group law on $E$ becomes addition on $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$. Does this complex-analytic perspective explain why the chord-and-tangent law is associative, or is it merely a correspondence — and what's the cleanest proof of associativity?
